## Centering Principle

**"We hand them the reins of our very fast horse."**

This is the editorial center of gravity for everything published on this Substack. Every post, note, and technical brief answers one question: *does this give the reader more control, more understanding, or more leverage over their own AI tools?*

Liana Banyan is an **infrastructure provider**, not a curator. Not a portal. Not an oracle.  
We build the protocol. The reader drives.

This framing is not marketing. It is the actual design principle of the architecture. Substack content that contradicts this — that positions LB as the "best AI for you" or the "place to come get your AI served" — contradicts the product, not just the brand. The editorial voice must match the product.

**The test:** after reading a paragraph, does the reader feel more capable and in control, or more dependent on LB to curate for them? The former is the target. The latter is drift.

## Prohibited Framings (Anti-Patterns)

Do not publish content that:

1. **Claims LB decides what's right** — "LB's AI knows what you need" / "We select the best tools for your workflow"
2. **Uses walled-garden language** — "inside our platform" / "the LB ecosystem" / "your LB experience"
3. **Positions LB as the AI** — LB is infrastructure for AI products; LB is not itself an AI product
4. **Publishes empirical claims without methodology** — "our system is 86.1% accurate" with no citation of R10, R12, methodology notes, N, arms, grading rubric
5. **Publishes pre-empirical work as if empirically proven** — concepts labeled as concepts, benchmarks labeled as benchmarks; the label must match the evidence tier
6. **Uses adjectives in place of numbers** — "significantly better" / "dramatically improved" are not publishable claims without the underlying measurement
7. **Boasts without invitation** — the Keystones are boast-inversion moves; they ground the claim in biographical grit, not institutional pride

## Approved Framings (Pattern Library)

### Opening moves
- "Here's what we measured, and here's how." *(empirical brief opener)*
- "I've been thinking about [problem] differently since [biographical moment]." *(Keystone-driven essay opener)*
- "The architecture is straightforward. The implications took longer to see." *(concept → depth pattern)*
- "We built a very fast horse. Here's how to drive it." *(product post opener)*

### Empirical sections
- "The benchmark: [N] questions, [arms], [grading rubric]. The number: [X%] HOT on [corpus type]."
- "Methodology first: [brief description]. Results: [table]. Interpretation: [what this means for the user]."
- "This number — [X] — is the one that changes the architectural prediction. Here's why."

### Voice transitions (Keystone integration)
Keystones enter naturally in context, not as set pieces. Examples:
- An essay on cost discipline → cites "53 years of surviving the trenches of poordom" in the opening paragraph to ground the cost-consciousness claim
- A governance post → "Nothing about us without us" appears as a section heading or closing sentence, not as a quote drop
- A technical brief on the Cathedral → "when all the Scribes sing together, the Harmony is Glorious" appears as a closing framing of the full-corpus test results

### Closing moves
- "The reins are yours. [One sentence on what the reader can do with this information today]."
- "Prove it first. We have. [Link to methodology + results]. Product it second. [Link to library/CLI]."
- "This is the commons. [The protocol / number / finding] is available to verify."

## Technical Rigor Standard

Every post that touches a number follows this protocol:

1. **State the number** — e.g., "18% HOT on zero-web-prior Cranewell corpus"
2. **Cite the methodology** — e.g., "K475/B122 benchmark: 50 questions × Perplexity-in-Comet, cathedral auto-only arm, R10 three-tier grading rubric (HOT = both required elements present)"
3. **State what it means for the reader** — e.g., "This means: on facts your AI has never seen before, a Cathedral-enabled query answers correctly 18% of the time vs 0% cold."
4. **Name what we don't know yet** — e.g., "K477 injection-pathway iterations underway; ceiling may be higher."

**The claim ladder (Cost-Slasher model):**
- Rung 1 (empirically secured): 18pp HOT lift, Cranewell cold→cathedral, K475 benchmark, 50Q, Perplexity-in-Comet
- Rung 2 (inference-permitted): Cathedral Effect is architecture-earned (confirmed: zero-web-prior corpus, no training contamination possible)
- Rung 3 (extrapolation-permitted-with-label): may generalize across vendors; K475 Covenant arm supports cross-prior generalization
- Rung 4 (aspiration, must be labeled as such): K477 may push toward 30%+ if injection-pathway is the ceiling driver

**Never publish Rung 3–4 without labeling it.** "We expect" / "the architecture predicts" / "K477 is testing whether" are the appropriate qualifiers.

## Substack Notes Voice

Notes are shorter than posts (typically 200–500 words). Same voice rules apply, but the density changes:

- **One claim, one number, one implication** per Note
- Keystones appear as single lines, not as developed sections
- Technical content: state the headline, link to the full post
- Distribution tactic: Notes get recommended by Substack to non-subscribers; the hook is the most important sentence

**Example Note:**
> "We ran 50 questions through a zero-web-prior corpus (Perplexity has never seen it). Cold: 0% correct. With Cathedral: 18% correct. That's 18 percentage points of recall on facts the model never trained on — architecture-earned, no fine-tuning. Full methodology: [link]. The reins are yours."
