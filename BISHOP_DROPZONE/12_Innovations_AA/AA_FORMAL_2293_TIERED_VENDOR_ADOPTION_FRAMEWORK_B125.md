## 1. Background

The Liana Banyan Cooperative has produced an empirically-validated substrate-grounded retrieval architecture (the *Cathedral Effect*, R10 / R12 / R13 — +86.2pp mean accuracy lift across four major commercial AI vendors at order-of-magnitude lower cost than current frontier-model retrieval baselines). The architecture is implemented in `librarian-mcp` (AGPL, public, member-installable) and is portable across cloud LLM vendors AND, by design, to local-LLM deployments (Layer 6 of the 7-Layer Defense, K-future test pending).

Commercial AI vendors face a structural problem: their consumer-tier "memory" products (Anthropic Claude Projects, OpenAI ChatGPT Memory, Google Gemini Memory, Perplexity Spaces) are *vendor-locked* by design — the user's context dies the day the vendor changes its terms. The Cathedral architecture inverts this: substrate-grounded retrieval is *vendor-portable* and *member-owned*. This is both an architectural property and a commercial opportunity: vendors who incorporate the Cathedral architecture into their proprietary product surfaces can offer their users dramatically improved retrieval quality whilst the substrate itself remains member-owned and vendor-portable — preserving the cooperative-economic principle (*you keep what you make*, keystone #42) AND enabling the vendor's commercial differentiation.

The structural analogy is *Android Open Source Project (AOSP) / Google Mobile Services (GMS)*: the open base layer is universal; the value-add proprietary services layer is licensed; OEM-branded forks compete on integration and brand. Liana Banyan's open base is `librarian-mcp` + the Cathedral substrate. The value-add layer is the *Tiered Vendor Adoption Framework* described herein.

A risk to address: AI vendors — once aware of the Cathedral architecture's empirical superiority — will allocate engineering resources to *workaround the IP* (rename concepts, re-architect implementations, cite parallel-invented prior art, license defensive third-party patents). The longer the framework remains unsigned, the more vendor R&D sinks into workaround engineering, and the harder it becomes to negotiate cooperative terms post-hoc. This priority filing establishes the canonical record for the commercial structure itself, before such workaround R&D commences.

## 2. The Innovation Claimed

**A tiered vendor-adoption commercial framework with the following defining structural properties:**

### 2.1 Identical-Terms Time-Window Stratification

All commercial AI vendors are offered **identical** baseline terms (cash amount, equity percentage, restrictions, member-portability covenant, cooperative-defensive obligations). The variable across the framework is **time-locked value-add**: the closer to the public announcement date a vendor signs, the longer the preview window, branded-fork co-marketing, and observer-seat tenure that vendor receives. Vendor self-selects timing; the framework does not discriminate by vendor identity, size, or relationship.

This structurally distinguishes the framework from variable-price vendor-licensing patterns (which carry antitrust coordination risk under FTC / EU competition law) by ensuring **equal terms, vendor-self-selected timing**.

### 2.2 Stable Cooperative Floor

The framework includes a **stable cooperative tier** that remains available *indefinitely* and *to all vendors regardless of timing*. The stable cooperative tier is access to the open-source substrate and the Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge (#2260) — no cash required, no equity required, no preview, no co-marketing. This is the *AOSP-equivalent floor*: vendors who never sign a tiered adoption agreement still have access to the commons; they simply do not receive the time-locked value-add.

This structurally distinguishes the framework from exclusionary licensing patterns by ensuring no vendor is ever shut out of the substrate.

### 2.3 First-Mover Equity Capture by Cooperative

Vendors entering at any tier (Founder Vendor / Pioneer / Adopter) provide both **fixed cash consideration** (to the LB Collective treasury) AND **fixed equity consideration** (to the LB Collective ownership) in exchange for the tier's value-add. The equity consideration is *the cooperative's structural entanglement* with the vendor — preventing extract-and-leave behavior whilst aligning the vendor's incentive with cooperative-economic outcomes.

The equity is governed via the existing 60/20/10/10 IP-allocation framework, with vendor-derived equity flowing to the cooperative pool and pro-rata to members under existing Pedestal Stake mechanics.

### 2.4 Branded Fork Rights with Member-Portability Covenant

Vendors at any tier (Founder Vendor / Pioneer / Adopter) gain the right to ship a **vendor-branded proprietary fork** of the substrate-injection integration ("Cathedral by Perplexity", "Sonar Memory", "Anthropic Long-Memory", etc.). The fork right grants UI/UX/integration exclusivity within the vendor's own product surface but **does not** grant exclusivity over the substrate itself — members retain the right to use the substrate via *any* vendor or via local inference (Layer 6). The covenant binds the vendor to support member-portability; the vendor cannot lock member content into their fork.

This is the AOSP/Chrome model: the open base supports many proprietary forks; users move freely between them.

### 2.5 Cooperative-Defensive Pledge Integration (#2260)

Every tier — including the stable cooperative floor — requires the vendor to sign the Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge (#2260). Vendors who sign cannot be sued for infringement on substrate-architecture patents by other Pledge signers; vendors who do not sign and ship infringing implementations face cooperative-defensive litigation (every Pledge signer can join the litigation under the cooperative-defensive terms — not one company suing one company, but a cooperative defending its commons against a free-rider).

This integration ensures the framework's commercial structure compounds the existing patent posture rather than replacing it.

### 2.6 Always-Offer-What-You-Would-Want Anchor (#40)

The framework's terms are explicitly structured as *what the cooperative would want if positions were reversed*: a clean offer, time-locked self-selection, no exclusion, structural alignment of incentive, equity-flow back to creators. The framework's defensibility cross-vendor stems from this design constraint — any vendor evaluating the offer can verify the cooperative would accept the same terms in their position.

This integration ensures the framework reads as *fair* to vendor counsel reviewing it, not as *extractive*.

### 2.7 Member-Substrate Consent and Sovereignty (#42)

The framework's commercial value-add layer is licensed to vendors *on the architectural pattern* — not on member content. Member substrates remain member-owned, member-portable, and member-sovereign per keystone #42 (*you keep what you make*). Vendors do not gain commercial access to member content via the framework; they gain access to the architectural pattern, the preview window for next-generation cooperative innovations, and the right to ship a branded integration.

This integration ensures the framework cannot be construed as the cooperative selling member content for commercial gain.
