## TL;DR (3 lines)

**A regulatory compliance architecture comprising the attachment of a measurable algorithmic efficiency standard — specifically, a minimum AI inference cache hit rate floor — to the existing large-load grid interconnection review process administered by a public utility commission, regional transmission organization, or equivalent regulatory body.** The architecture produces compliance without creating a new regulatory bureaucracy by piggybacking on existing interconnection review workflows, operates as a technology-neutral output standard (measured cache hit rate rather than prescribed technology), and composes cleanly with existing Title III EPCA appliance efficiency standard precedent, FERC Order 2222 distributed energy interconnection rules, and the European Union Ecodesign Directive framework. **This is a patentable business method under U.S. law in the regulatory-compliance-architecture category**, subject to counsel's evaluation of Alice/Bilski jurisprudence, and is the political-economy companion to the technical invention disclosed in #2249.

## The Problem the Invention Solves

Existing regulatory responses to AI datacenter resource consumption fall into three categories, each of which addresses a real problem through the wrong lever:

**Category 1 — Moratoriums.** Complete pauses on new datacenter construction. Addresses the growth rate but freezes the architectural inefficiency in place and foreclosures constructive industry negotiation.

**Category 2 — Hardware efficiency mandates.** Chip-level or facility-level power usage effectiveness (PUE) disclosure and reporting requirements. Measures the wrong layer — a datacenter can operate at world-class chip-level efficiency while running those chips on 70% work that does not need to happen.

**Category 3 — Compute taxes.** Pigouvian pricing of the externality. Economically coherent but politically difficult, administratively costly, and creates adversarial rather than aligned relationships between regulators and industry.

**The invention addresses none of these directly. Instead, it identifies a fourth lever: a process rule that attaches to an existing regulatory workflow and produces compliance at near-zero administrative cost, without creating a new bureaucracy.**

The invention is specifically a *regulatory compliance architecture pattern*, not a substantive standard. What makes the invention novel is the architectural decision to attach the algorithmic efficiency standard to **grid interconnection review** — a workflow that already exists in every U.S. state, at the federal level, and in every major international regulatory framework — rather than to any of the more commonly proposed attachment points (environmental impact review, facility siting review, corporate ESG disclosure, tax incentives).

Attaching the standard to grid interconnection review has five specific advantages that together constitute the invention's non-obvious technical and policy contribution:

1. **The workflow already exists.** Every large-load interconnection already requires regulatory approval. Adding a cache hit rate compliance requirement adds one evaluation criterion to an existing review rather than creating a new review.

2. **The forcing function is structural, not enforcement-based.** A facility that fails the cache hit rate floor does not get fined or sanctioned — it simply does not receive interconnection approval. This preserves the regulatory-industry relationship and avoids enforcement litigation.

3. **The measurement point is natural.** Grid interconnection review is the point at which the operator is already reporting expected load, facility design, and operational parameters to the regulator. Adding the cache hit rate disclosure to that reporting stream is a marginal extension of existing compliance work.

4. **The timing is right.** Grid interconnection queues are currently the primary binding constraint on hyperscale datacenter expansion (per ERCOT's 226 GW Texas queue, PJM's capacity constraints, and similar data from other regions). Attaching the efficiency standard to the constraint that is already binding gives the rule immediate operational traction.

5. **The political coalition is available.** Ratepayer-protection advocates, environmental groups, grid reliability planners, and (per the Jevons Flip analysis) hyperscaler CFOs themselves all support the rule simultaneously, producing an unusually broad political base.

## The Invention (Independent Claim Scaffold)

**Claim 1 (the root claim — regulatory compliance method).** A method for regulating the algorithmic efficiency of artificial intelligence inference workloads at hyperscale datacenter facilities, comprising:

- **(a)** Establishing, by a regulatory body having jurisdiction over grid interconnection approvals, a minimum algorithmic efficiency floor expressed as a measured AI inference cache hit rate applicable to hyperscale datacenter facilities whose contracted or projected peak electrical load exceeds a specified threshold;
- **(b)** Requiring, as a condition of grid interconnection approval, that the operator of an affected facility demonstrate compliance with the floor established in (a) through one of: (i) existing compliance evidence from facilities operated by the operator in prior regulatory periods; (ii) a forward-looking Algorithmic Efficiency Implementation Plan accompanied by a performance bond or equivalent guarantee;
- **(c)** Adjusting the floor established in (a) periodically based on disclosed measurement data submitted by affected operators under an associated disclosure rule, such that the floor evolves with the state of the art in AI inference caching;
- **(d)** Treating the compliance determination as exclusively a precondition for interconnection approval, such that non-compliance results in denial of new interconnection rather than in civil penalty, administrative sanction, or enforcement action;
- **(e)** Maintaining technology-neutrality whereby the compliance evaluation examines only the measured cache hit rate output, without reference to the specific caching technology, architecture, vendor product, or software stack by which the rate is achieved;
- **(f)** Integrating the compliance evaluation with the existing grid interconnection review workflow administered by the regulatory body without creating a separate regulatory bureaucracy, a separate enforcement division, or a separate administrative hearing process.

**Dependent Claim 1.1** — The method of Claim 1, wherein the hyperscale datacenter threshold in (a) is specified as a facility or co-located group of facilities under common ownership or operational control whose aggregate contracted or interconnected electrical load exceeds 50 megawatts for disclosure purposes and 100 megawatts for algorithmic efficiency floor compliance.

**Dependent Claim 1.2** — The method of Claim 1, wherein the initial minimum algorithmic efficiency floor in (a) is set at not less than 40 percent measured cache hit rate, with subsequent adjustments made not more frequently than every 36 months based on disclosed data.

**Dependent Claim 1.3** — The method of Claim 1, further comprising an associated disclosure rule requiring annual public reporting by affected operators of their measured cache hit rate, power usage effectiveness, water usage effectiveness, and AI inference workload share of total facility load, with reporting methodology standardized through rulemaking by the regulatory body.

**Dependent Claim 1.4** — The method of Claim 1, wherein the forward-looking Algorithmic Efficiency Implementation Plan referenced in (b)(ii) specifies the caching architecture, timeline, verification procedures, and performance bond terms by which the new facility will achieve the floor within a defined compliance period not to exceed 24 months following operational commencement.

**Dependent Claim 1.5** — The method of Claim 1, wherein the integration with the existing grid interconnection review workflow in (f) is implemented through amendment of the regulatory body's existing large-load interconnection rules, without creation of a new statute, administrative order, or rulemaking proceeding outside the existing workflow.

**Dependent Claim 1.6** — The method of Claim 1, further comprising an exclusion provision whereby facilities operated by institutions of higher education, governmental entities, and registered 501(c)(3) cooperatives for research, educational, or non-commercial cooperative purposes are exempt from the algorithmic efficiency floor compliance requirement.

**Dependent Claim 1.7** — The method of Claim 1, wherein the regulatory body is selected from: a state Public Utility Commission; the Federal Energy Regulatory Commission; a regional transmission organization; a European Union regulatory authority operating under the Ecodesign Directive framework; or an equivalent national-level regulatory body in any jurisdiction having authority over grid interconnection approvals for large electrical loads.

**Dependent Claim 1.8** — The method of Claim 1, wherein the technology-neutrality provision in (e) is expressly codified in the enabling legislation or rule, such that the regulatory body is statutorily or administratively prohibited from specifying a particular caching technology, vendor, product, or standard as the means of compliance.

**Dependent Claim 1.9** — The method of Claim 1, further comprising a preemption provision expressly intended to provide a constructive alternative to broad moratoria on new hyperscale datacenter construction, such that adoption of the method in a jurisdiction preempts or forecloses the adoption of a moratorium in the same jurisdiction.

**Dependent Claim 1.10** — A regulatory system comprising: a regulatory body having grid interconnection approval authority; a standardized measurement methodology for AI inference cache hit rates; a disclosure reporting infrastructure; and a compliance evaluation workflow integrated with existing large-load interconnection review, configured to execute the method of Claims 1 through 1.9.

## Prior Art Distinction

The invention is distinguishable from existing regulatory frameworks on the following grounds:

**1. Title III of the Energy Policy and Conservation Act (EPCA, 1975, 42 U.S.C. §§ 6291–6309)** establishes federal appliance efficiency standards. The present invention adapts the output-standard pattern of EPCA Title III to AI inference architecture, but applies it through grid interconnection review rather than through product-sale exclusion — which is a materially different regulatory attachment point that the EPCA framework does not address.

**2. FERC Order 2222 (2020)** required regional transmission organizations to allow distributed energy resources to participate in wholesale electricity markets. The invention's use of grid interconnection review as the compliance attachment point is structurally analogous to Order 2222's use of RTO market participation rules, but the present invention applies the pattern to demand-side efficiency rather than supply-side market access — a materially different application of the regulatory-attachment principle.

**3. EU Ecodesign Directive (2009/125/EC)** establishes minimum efficiency standards for products sold in the EU internal market. The present invention adapts the output-standard pattern of the Ecodesign Directive to AI inference architecture, but applies the standard at the regulatory interconnection gate rather than at the product-sale gate.

**4. Existing AI datacenter disclosure and efficiency proposals** (including various provisions of the EU AI Act, state-level energy disclosure rules, and federal efficiency reporting requirements) address chip-level or facility-level PUE rather than algorithmic-level efficiency. **No prior regulatory framework has proposed attaching an algorithmic efficiency floor to grid interconnection review as the specific regulatory architecture.** The attachment point is the invention's central novelty.

**5. Proposed AI compute taxes and moratorium frameworks** (Sanders' 2025 moratorium proposal, various EU compute tax discussions) address the wrong lever and do not operate through grid interconnection review. The invention's architectural choice to attach to the interconnection gate, rather than to operate through taxation or prohibition, produces qualitatively different political economics and implementation characteristics that are not present in the prior art.

**Counsel note on business method eligibility:** The invention is a regulatory compliance architecture and may encounter Alice/Bilski business-method scrutiny at USPTO. Counsel is directed to evaluate whether the invention's *integration with measurable technical outputs* (the cache hit rate, itself a measurable technical metric produced by the physical operation of a computer system per Innovation #2249) is sufficient to satisfy the "something more" prong of the Alice two-step analysis. Bishop's tentative view is that the cache hit rate measurement, because it is derived from the physical execution of Innovation #2249's computer-implemented method, provides the "something more" anchor that distinguishes the present invention from abstract business methods historically rejected under Alice. Counsel's independent evaluation is required.

- Netherlands Authority for Consumers and Markets

**Commercial significance of the regulatory rule itself:**

The invention's commercial significance is indirect but substantial. By creating the forcing function that drives adoption of Innovation #2249 (ROM-First AI Inference Cost Architecture), the regulatory rule produces measurable savings in the range of $8.67 billion per year at 2024 baseline, rising to $36.6 billion per year by 2030, distributed across global hyperscaler operators. **The rule is the policy lever that unlocks the economic dividend of Innovation #2249 at scale.**

**Licensing consideration:** Regulatory architecture patents are typically licensed to regulatory bodies themselves, to government consulting firms specializing in regulatory design, or to industry associations that draft model legislation for legislators. The licensing value is modest compared to the technical patent (#2249), but the strategic value — positioning Liana Banyan Corporation as the architectural authority behind the regulatory framework that drives adoption of its own technical patent — is substantial. Counsel to evaluate whether direct licensing is appropriate, or whether the rule should be dedicated to the public domain to maximize policy adoption velocity while preserving the technical licensing revenue stream from #2249.
