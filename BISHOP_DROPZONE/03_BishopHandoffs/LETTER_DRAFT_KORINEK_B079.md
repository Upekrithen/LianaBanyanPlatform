## LETTER BODY

Dear Prof. Korinek,

Ben Casselman quoted you in the New York Times on April 3. You said: *"In the past, our social safety net was designed to help people over transitory shocks. This one might actually be a more permanent shock."*

You were the only economist in the piece willing to name that distinction plainly.

I have been building toward that diagnosis for several years. The architecture I built assumes the shock is permanent. It does not assume workers return to comparable jobs. It does not assume retraining restores prior income. It does not assume the social safety net will hold.

It assumes participation history is the asset.

Liana Banyan is a Wyoming cooperative corporation with three currencies designed for permanence:

- **Credits** — day-to-day transactional medium, $1 = 1 Credit equivalent value
- **Marks** — effort-differential currency. They compound across initiatives, guilds, and production. They do not expire. Governance weight scales with them.
- **Joules** — surplus that persists. A "forever stamp." Once contributed, the record stands.

Ownership earns down over time. A member who rides in a cooperative vehicle, buys meals from a cooperative kitchen, or uses the canister manufacturing system is moving toward owning a share of the underlying infrastructure. Not speculation. Not equity. Participation compounds into standing.

This is not UBI. It is not wage insurance. It is a member-owned economic operating system where the floor is something the worker built rather than something given to them.

I am attaching *The Five Dollar Career* — a paper written before your NYT remarks. It argues that a permanent-shock architecture must replace the transitory-shock safety net, and it describes the currency mechanics above.

One note of full disclosure: Liana Banyan's multi-agent development pipeline runs on Claude. I am sending this letter before you begin at Anthropic so it arrives as an academic correspondence, not a commercial one. If a conversation becomes easier after your transition, that is your call. I wanted the argument in front of you while you are still the person who said it publicly in the Times.
