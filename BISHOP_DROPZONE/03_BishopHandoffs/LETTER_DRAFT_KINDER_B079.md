## LETTER BODY

Dear Ms. Kinder,

Ben Casselman quoted you in the New York Times on April 3. You said you no longer know what a college student can bring to your team that Claude can't do. You said: if you can do your job locked in a closet with a computer, ultimately you're going to be in trouble.

You named the frame.

I want to propose an additional clause. The workers in that closet are in trouble because the closet does not belong to them. The computer does not belong to them. The output does not belong to them. They are renters of a closet they cannot leave, doing work they do not own, on infrastructure designed to extract value from their labor.

I built a platform that flips all three.

Liana Banyan is a Wyoming cooperative corporation. Membership is five dollars a year — the same terms I have. Creators keep 83.3% of every transaction. Platform pricing follows a Cost + 20% floor. There is no rake beyond operating costs. There is no outside capital to answer to. The closet, the computer, the output, and the platform itself all belong to the members.

I am attaching a paper called *The Five Dollar Career*. It was written before the NYT piece. It argues that the permanent displacement you are describing requires an architecture that compounds contribution history rather than resetting it. Not unemployment insurance. Not retraining grants. A member-owned economic operating system where participation IS the asset.

I will be at the Yale AI Symposium on April 28. If you are attending, or if you have 30 minutes before then, I would like to walk you through the architecture. You named the frame. I would like to show you what answers it.

Respectfully,

Jonathan Jones
Founder & General Manager
Liana Banyan Corporation
