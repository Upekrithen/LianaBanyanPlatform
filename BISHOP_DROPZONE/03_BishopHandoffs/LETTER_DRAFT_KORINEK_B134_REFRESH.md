## LETTER BODY (Bishop scaffold — Founder revoices at prose-pass)

Dear Prof. Korinek,

Ben Casselman quoted you in the New York Times on April 3. You said: *"In the past, our social safety net was designed to help people over transitory shocks. This one might actually be a more permanent shock."*

You were the only economist in the piece willing to name that distinction plainly.

I have been building toward that diagnosis for several years. The architecture I built assumes the shock is permanent. It does not assume workers return to comparable jobs. It does not assume retraining restores prior income. It does not assume the social safety net will hold. **It assumes participation history is the asset.**

Liana Banyan is a Wyoming cooperative corporation with three currencies designed for permanence:

- **Credits** — day-to-day transactional medium, $1 = 1 Credit equivalent value
- **Marks** — effort-differential currency. They compound across initiatives, guilds, and production. They do not expire. Governance weight scales with them.
- **Joules** — surplus that persists. A "forever stamp." Once contributed, the record stands.

Ownership earns down over time. A member who rides in a cooperative vehicle, buys meals from a cooperative kitchen, or uses the canister manufacturing system is moving toward owning a share of the underlying infrastructure. Not speculation. Participation compounds into standing.

This is not UBI. It is not wage insurance. **It is a member-owned economic operating system where the floor is something the worker built rather than something given to them.**

---

I am writing to you now for a second reason that has emerged this week.

In the same week that I am sending this letter, three independent signals appeared. Ruben Hassid published *"ChatGPT-5.5 or Claude 4.7? How to pick the right AI for the job"* — tacit admission that no single model dominates. EmergingAI published *"The Anatomy of an Agent Harness"* — framing the harness, not the model, as the next-shift architectural concept. And our own Cathedral Effect cross-vendor benchmark (K535) closed with a 3.5-percentage-point HOT spread across five vendors at twenty-three times the cost spread.

These three receipts converge on a single conclusion: AI companies whose strategy depended on winning the model arms race have a problem, and the rational move to close that gap is to **rebuild on cooperative substrate** that already makes lesser models equal frontier ones.

I call this the Cathedral Adoption Pathway. Once it is empirically obvious that substrate beats model selection, the strategic move for AI companies is to adopt the substrate. The question is not whether they will — for the companies that survive the next eighteen months, they must — but **on what terms, and for whose benefit.**

We are offering the substrate to AI companies under a cooperative path. Specifically: a Collaboration Partnership Stake structure that admits AI companies as Research Partners with the existing Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge framework (free forever to nonprofits, cooperatives, and academics on EIN verification), and a Reversion Clause where derivative patents that build on the cooperative substrate flow back into the cooperative defensive moat at a calibrated rate (lower for major-vendor partners; standard 20% for smaller partners). The resulting moat protects every Partner's customers from rival enclosure. **Refusing the offer requires positively articulating why one would refuse a path that protects one's own customers.** The cooperative version of the offer means the offer is hard to argue with, even from the other side of the table.

I am sharing this with you for a specific reason: **your transition to the Anthropic Institute, to study how transformative AI restructures economic activity, places you at the exact junction where this thesis lives.** The economic-restructuring frame and the architectural-substrate frame are the same problem viewed from different scales. I would value your read on whether the cooperative-substrate path is the one your work points toward, or whether you see a different shape.

Two enclosures accompany this letter:

1. *The Five Dollar Career* — a paper written before your NYT remarks. It argues that a permanent-shock architecture must replace the transitory-shock safety net, and it describes the currency mechanics above.
2. *The Cathedral Adoption Pathway: Why AI Companies Will Rebuild on Cooperative Substrate* — a Foundation paper draft (April 29, 2026) articulating the architectural thesis that emerged this week from the three receipts. It includes a comparison table contrasting cooperative-substrate governance from vendor-layer guardrail frameworks (OpenAI Agents SDK / AWS Bedrock Guardrails / Anthropic Constitutional AI), and a pair of worked examples (cooperative-asset partial-liquidation; high-risk MCP shell deployment) showing how the cooperative governance layer composes with vendor-runtime safety layers.

One note of full disclosure: Liana Banyan's multi-agent development pipeline runs on Claude. I am sending this letter before you begin at Anthropic so it arrives as an academic correspondence rather than a commercial one. **If a conversation becomes easier after your transition, that is your call.** I wanted the argument in front of you while you are still the person who said it publicly in the Times.

I had hoped to attend Yale's AI Symposium on April 28; circumstances prevented it. A phone or video conversation at your convenience before semester's end — or any time after your transition — would be welcome.

Respectfully,
