Your work on surveillance law, cybersecurity, and AI's national-security implications takes seriously a problem most technologists avoid: when technological capability outpaces legal constraint, the constraint must be architectural, not regulatory. The failure mode you have written about in the AI national-security context — that current AI systems lack verifiable governance constraints that hold under adversarial conditions — maps onto a cooperative governance problem I have been solving for several years. The specific problem: how do you build a governance system whose constraints hold even when the vendor changes, the model updates, or the operating context shifts?

My answer is a cooperative-owned substrate with three structural invariants:

1. **Economic invariant** — Cost+20% and 83.3% creator-keep are in bylaws, not a settings page. No single actor can override them without a constitutional amendment by the full membership.
2. **Informational invariant** — Stone Tablet append-only records. No retroactive edit. The audit trail is structurally incorruptible.
3. **Governance invariant** — ADAPT Score governance weight scales with contribution, not dollars. No single actor can capture governance through capital injection.

These are not policy promises. They are architectural primitives. The national-security relevance: a cooperative substrate with these properties cannot be captured by a foreign-state actor through standard pressure channels (capital injection, key-person compromise, regulatory capture) because the architecture does not have the capture surface.

*W-001: "Never accept a No from someone who can't give you a Yes to begin with."* — The regulatory-capture version of this: never accept that a vendor-governance promise is sufficient from someone who is not the constitutional authority over the system. A vendor whose governance model can be overridden by a future CEO is not offering rule-of-law. The cooperative architecture offers it structurally.
