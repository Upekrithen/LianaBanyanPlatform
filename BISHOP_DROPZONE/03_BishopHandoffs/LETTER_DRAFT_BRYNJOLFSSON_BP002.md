## LETTER BODY (Knight scaffold — Founder revoices at prose-pass; 350-700 word target)

Dear Prof. Brynjolfsson,

The Turing Trap named the failure mode precisely: when we optimize AI systems to pass the Turing Test — to substitute for human judgment at lowest cost — we destroy the complementarity that generates the most economic value. The J-Curve follow-on made the institutional implication explicit: the AI productivity payoff requires a real-economy adaptation lag that most organizations are not structured to survive.

What neither paper could specify was the institutional form in which that adaptation occurs. I have been building it.

Liana Banyan is a Wyoming cooperative corporation structured around complementarity as a first principle. Our sixteen integrated initiatives — food delivery, grocery, retail, health concierge, academic publishing, gaming, political expedition — are not AI-substitution plays. Every initiative is designed so the AI layer amplifies human contribution rather than replacing it. The economic invariant that makes this hold: **83.3% of every transaction flows to the creator or worker.** Not as a policy promise. In the bylaws.

The J-Curve insight is that the adaptation lag is real and costly. The institutional response most platforms have taken — capture more value from workers to subsidize the transition period — is the adaptation failure your paper warns against. The cooperative response: absorb the adaptation cost at the organizational level while preserving worker economic share at the transaction level. The Cost+20% margin makes this structurally achievable. The member-owned governance makes it contractually durable.

*W-007: "Speak to the person in front of you, not the abstract beneficiary."* — You have spent thirty years developing the quantitative case that complementarity beats substitution. I am writing to the economist who built that case, not to an abstract academic audience. The institution I am describing is the operational realization of the J-Curve prescription at production scale. I am inviting your evaluation of whether it closes the institutional-form gap your papers identified.

The Collaborative Partnership Stake we are offering to research institutions includes access to our ADAPT Score governance mechanism, our Downstream IP Reversion Clause (#2287), and our production dataset of member-owner contribution patterns across sixteen initiative types. For an empirical economist, the cooperative-labor-market data Liana Banyan will generate is a natural experiment in AI-complementarity at scale — not a simulation.

Two enclosures:

1. *The Cathedral Adoption Pathway: Why AI Companies Will Rebuild on Cooperative Substrate* — Foundation paper (April 29, 2026), ~7,000 words. Section 4 addresses the J-Curve adaptation problem directly and argues cooperative ownership is the institutional form the adaptation requires.
2. Innovation #2287 one-page cooperative-offer term sheet.

A conversation at your convenience would be welcome.

Respectfully,

Jonathan Jones
Founder & General Manager
Liana Banyan Corporation
