Dear Mr. Puglisi,

Your work on building operational AI tools and policy — grounded in law enforcement praxis rather than academic abstraction — identifies a governance problem that most AI policy frameworks miss entirely: the accountability gap between who deploys the system and who bears the consequences.

In law enforcement deployment, that gap is fatal. In commercial AI deployment, it is systematic.

I have been building the architecture that closes it.

Liana Banyan is a Wyoming cooperative corporation. The accountability framework that makes it structurally different from extractive platforms is not policy-layer governance — it is ownership-layer governance. When the people who bear consequences of the platform are also the constitutional owners of the platform, the accountability gap collapses. The mechanism: every member, from the $5/year membership tier, is a constitutional owner. Platform decisions that affect members require member consent. This is not a policy promise. It is the ownership structure.

From an operational standpoint, the specific accountability properties that distinguish cooperative AI deployment:

1. **Audit trail by architecture** — Stone Tablet append-only records. No retroactive edit. Every action is logged, every log is immutable, every action is attributable to the agent that took it. This is the audit standard you would recognize from law enforcement evidence-handling, applied to AI agent actions.
2. **Fire-Control gate for consequential writes** — Sensitive actions (financial transactions, external communications, records modification) require explicit human approval before execution. The gate is architectural, not policy.
3. **Scope discipline** — Each AI agent operates within defined scope. Actions outside scope are blocked before they occur, not reviewed after.

*W-003: "The map is not the territory — show them the working infrastructure."* — Policy frameworks describe governance. Liana Banyan's operational infrastructure implements it. From an LEO-praxis standpoint, the difference between a policy that says "AI must be accountable" and an architecture that makes unaccountable AI actions technically impossible is the difference between a procedure manual and a physical constraint.

The Applied Technology Institute's mission of building operational tools, not theoretical frameworks, is exactly the orientation that the cooperative AI governance model requires for deployment partners. I am writing to you because your operational background means you will evaluate the architecture at the implementation layer, not the policy layer — which is the layer where Liana Banyan is designed to hold.
