## The technical claim

In 2003, Founder built a mashup that combined Google Maps with Craigslist. The product was useful. Customers used it. Founder operated it.

Google had published terms forbidding this kind of integration.

Google did not enforce those terms.

Then Google launched Google-Maps-plus-X internally — the same category of integration. They hired the third-party developer who had done it externally first. Founder s small operation was destroyed in the process.

Three things happened in that sequence:
1. Google published rules.
2. Google did not enforce them — for everyone — for years.
3. Google enforced them — selectively — when Google itself wanted the territory.

This is not a complaint about Google specifically. This is a structural observation about cooperative-rule-systems.

## The deeper claim

(Founder voice — rewrite from scratch.)

A rule that is published but not enforced is not a rule. It is bait.

Bait protects the rule-maker. The rule-maker can claim "I told you the rules" while the unenforced space attracts third parties. Then the rule-maker can change strategy at any time and invoke the rules to clear the field.

The third parties who entered the unenforced space did not violate the rules. They acted in the territory the rule-maker permitted by inaction. Then the rule-maker cooperatively-defected — used the rules selectively against parties who had been operating in good faith under the apparent operating norms.

Cooperative defection by the rule-maker is a special class of betrayal. It is worse than rule-violation by a peer because the rule-maker designed the rules and chose when to enforce them. The asymmetry is structural.

## The lesson Founder built into LB

You cannot prevent cooperative defection in a system you don t control. You CAN prevent it in a system you DO control — by structurally pre-committing.

The Liana Banyan Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge (A and A #2260) is the structural pre-commitment.

Specifically:
- LB members get rights to LB substrate that LB Corporation cannot revoke.
- Member-Portability Covenant (A and A #2293) ensures members keep substrate sovereignty even if a vendor branded fork emerges.
- The platform CANNOT rule-bait its members because the rules are AGPL-licensed and structurally enforceable by member action.
- The Year of Jubilee Cathedral Reconciliation Ledger (A and A #2308) ensures even derived data has explicit reconciliation events that members can audit.

These are not philosophical commitments. They are structural commitments that make cooperative defection impossible at the platform layer.

## Why the lesson generalizes

(Founder voice — rewrite.)

Every cooperative platform faces this temptation. Network effects accumulate. Ecosystem participants invest in the platform. Then platform changes terms; ecosystem participants are stuck.

The pattern repeats:
- Twitter to ecosystem developers (2012, API restrictions)
- Reddit to third-party clients (2023, API pricing)
- Apple to App Store developers (ongoing, fee changes)
- Cloud providers to customers (cost changes, deprecations)
- Industry consortia to members (proprietary forks)

Cooperative defection is the dominant strategy under network-effect dynamics if the platform is structurally able to defect.

The only structural defense is to make defection IMPOSSIBLE — not unlikely, not unattractive, but architecturally impossible. The platform that succeeds at this is the platform that earns long-term trust because trust is no longer dependent on the platform s benevolence.
