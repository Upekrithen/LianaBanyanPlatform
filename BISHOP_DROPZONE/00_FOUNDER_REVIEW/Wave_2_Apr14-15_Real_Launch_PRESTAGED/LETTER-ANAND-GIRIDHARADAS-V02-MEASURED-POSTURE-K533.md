Liana Banyan isn't philanthropy. It's infrastructure.

Three commercial portals (.com/.biz/.net) generate Cost+20% margin (locked structurally in the operating agreement, not by policy choice that a future board could revise) that permanently funds 16 charitable initiatives across food access, prescription medications, group purchasing, legal defense, medical savings, community coordination, manufacturing, education, and more. Not "donate to continue" — structural sustainability through architecture.

Every member earns participation allocation through their actual contribution to the platform — not options, not tokens, not speculative instruments. The allocation is blockchain-anchored, publicly verifiable, and immutable, but it cannot be sold, traded, transferred off-platform, or redeemed for cash. There is no secondary market. There is no financialization. The allocation is a notarized record of contribution and the rights it earns inside the cooperative — what cannot be traded cannot be extracted. The people using the platform own their participation; the platform itself is a cooperative member-governed entity (Liana Banyan Corporation, Wyoming C-Corp, filed November 2025).

The 2,270 documented utility patent innovations across 13 provisionals filed are the legal architecture making sure no future board, no acquirer, no IPO could rewrite the cooperative substrate into an extraction substrate.

When I step back, there's no foundation board to capture, no donor class to appease, no billionaire deciding what counts as worthy. The members govern what they participate in.

Is this still compromise? Probably. Is it perfect? No. But it doesn't depend on the continued generosity of people whose wealth comes from the problems we're trying to solve.
