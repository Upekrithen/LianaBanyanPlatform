Dear Professor Schneider,

My parents were missionaries in Tanzania before we left on the last commercial flight ahead of Idi Amin's invasion. They chose for our family not to have television until years later, when I was thirteen and we moved to Montana after they spent five years working as foster parents back in the States. That meant I read voraciously, watched closely, and most crucially grew up inside fundamentally flawed societal systems that all seemed to share the same malaise: poor design, driven by the base motivations of designers and participants.

In *Everything for Everyone*, you documented the convergence of cooperatives—two hundred years of proven history—and digital platforms—a quarter century of infrastructure—and argued that the combination could address the weaknesses of both. I've often treated that as a design specification rather than just a diagnosis.

I've found in chess, engineering, and system design that combining problems can cause them to mutually resolve or cancel out—like a scale that only balances with both weights in place. When I applied that logic to platform cooperativism, the mechanisms that emerged were these:

**Cost + 20% — Locked.** The margin is fixed in the operating agreement and cannot be changed for every transaction. Not aspirational—structural. I call it "Margin Economics": a fixed, locked margin that applies across workers, creators, consumers, and owners, designed so that participants capture the vast majority of value creation.

**Non-Tradeable Provenance Ledger.** Every participant earns a recorded ownership stake through contribution — not options, not tokens, not speculative instruments. The ledger is blockchain-anchored, publicly verifiable, and immutable. Testnet-by-design: stakes cannot be sold, traded, transferred off-platform, or redeemed for cash. No secondary market. No financialization. This is not an NFT. It is a notarized deed — proof of contribution, proof of rights, proof of share. What cannot be traded cannot be extracted.

**The Boaz Principle.** Built into the economic engine, not added as charity afterward: automated resource allocation to newcomers and those in need, modeled on ancient gleaning law. The platform cannot operate without "sharing the corners of the field." Structural, not optional.

**The Three-Gear Currency Differential.** Purchasing power parity disparities are absorbed at acquisition, not at spending—so a worker in Lagos and a worker in Louisville stand on equivalent economic footing inside the platform. The strong-currency privilege problem is addressed at the infrastructure layer.

**The 300 Framework.** Fixed-capacity governance with 300 tiered leadership positions. Leadership is earned through demonstrated competence, not purchased through token accumulation. The structure cannot metastasize into bureaucracy—by purposeful design.

These are not principles posted on a wall. They are patented mechanisms. There are 1,244 documented utility innovations, 210 formal claims across 7 applications. Eight have cleared deep prior-art review with zero competing patents found, with nine more strong candidates in the pipeline. The portfolio is independently valued in the $9M–$90M+ range—not to monetize cooperative economics, but to prevent anyone from strip-mining what we've built for member-owners, by donating the IP into the platform's cooperative structure.

Sixty percent of IP revenue goes directly to the cooperative entity (Liana Banyan Corporation, member-governed), with an additional 20% allocated to platform operating capital for self-sustainment from launch. The remaining 20% to the founder follows the same breakdown available to all members—no special treatment, no founder carve-out, no preferred class. I am following the same path every person with an idea can follow on this platform: marking the trail, eating my own cooking.

## The Lemonade Stand Model: Research Infrastructure for Cooperative Economics

Here is one component that speaks directly to your work—and it started with a dream.

In 7th grade at Mead Junior High in Colorado, I played a lemonade stand simulation game on classroom computers. Most students skimmed the weather and mood data. I read everything—every variable, every signal—and then bet everything on sunny days when customers were happy. By doubling repeatedly, I made millions in the simulation.

The lesson: **information asymmetry creates advantage, and the ability to iterate without catastrophic loss enables aggressive learning.**

But when real entrepreneurship arrived, the rules changed. Real money. Real failure. Real consequences. The cooperatives you've studied face the same problem—they can't afford to experiment the way venture-backed startups can.

So I built what I call the **Lemonade Stand Model**: a platform architecture that provides real users, real market dynamics, and minimal cost of failure. Researchers and cooperative practitioners can run experiments using actual platform participants—not simulated agents—at subscription prices starting at $5/month.

The system uses **Contingency Operators**: isolated sandboxes that fork from live platform state with a single-point change (a Delta), then run forward to observe cascading effects. You get to practice with real people, but failure doesn't cost much.

The origin story is literal. On February 13, 2026, I woke at 3:39 AM and immediately transcribed what I'd been dreaming about: "What-if scenarios. Mini sandboxes with single point failures or changes to propose a potential pivot… Call it Thought Experiment, so can test nondestructively." The screenshots of those notes are preserved in our documentation. The dream became a working paper, then a SQL schema, then a piece of research infrastructure integrated into the platform.

**Why this matters for cooperative economics research:**

The platform operates on a **Zero Demographics** principle. We don't merely avoid collecting demographic data—we have **no mechanism that can collect it**. Age verification is handled by credit card payment (required by law for the $5 membership), and that's it. No age, gender, race, income, education, location, or other demographic field exists in the database.

This is not a constraint. It's a feature:

- **Ethically clean research** — Far fewer IRB concerns about protected populations
- **Bias-resistant by design** — You can't discriminate on demographics you don't track
- **Privacy-compliant by default** — GDPR, CCPA, and future regulations are addressed at the architecture layer
- **Generalizable findings** — Research speaks to behaviors and structures, not audience slices

Imagine being able to test whether the 83.3% creator share actually changes cooperative participation differently than a 70% share—with real users, real money, real decisions—at the cost of that same $5/month research subscription. That's what this enables.
