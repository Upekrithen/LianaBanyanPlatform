[SUBSTANCE PARA 1 — Founder voice. Structural argument framed in Acemoglu's institutional-economics register.]

Liana Banyan is a cooperative platform with structural choices that map directly onto your *Why Nations Fail* and *Power and Progress* frameworks: a Cost+20% margin locked structurally in the operating agreement (not by board policy that a future leadership could revise); creators retaining 83.3% of every transaction; IP allocation running 60/20/10/10 across patent buckets / Founder-creator / global sponsor pool / individual creator pedestals, with every innovation filed under the Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge — licensed for cooperative use by structure rather than gatekept by enclosure. The architectural choices are designed to prevent the slow drift from inclusive into extractive that your historical research documents in detail across two centuries of institutional case studies.

What bears stating directly because your prior published work makes it the natural debate to have: the cooperative-substrate is **not claiming to substitute for state institution-building**. *Why Nations Fail* operates at the level of *who establishes the rule of law, property rights, contract enforcement, and the foundational political-economic institutions* that allow markets to function — and the answer at that layer has been, empirically and durably, capable states. The cooperative-substrate operates at a different layer: *given that the state-level institutional foundation exists, how is the value generated downstream of that foundation distributed*. The two are architecturally complementary, not substitutional. The state establishes the foundation; the cooperative-substrate ensures the value flowing on top of that foundation lands with the workers who do the downstream work, rather than with extractive platform intermediaries that capture value without creating it.

[SUBSTANCE PARA 2 — Founder voice. Empirical anchor — the K528 cross-architecture benchmark + K533 verification harness make the architectural claim falsifiable in your specific institutional-economics methodology.]

The architectural claim is empirically falsifiable, and the harness above is how. The K528 cross-architecture benchmark (1,170 graded responses across 16 vendor and architecture conditions, 200 sealed questions across six knowledge categories, $206.33 vendor API spend) found that the same indexed-retrieval substrate, with six different language models behind it, produces accuracy clustering within three percentage points across the models at a 180-times cost spread. The architectural property: *index coverage is the binding constraint on retrieval quality, not model capability*. Same substrate, different models, equivalent retrieval — at compute-cost-per-correct-answer 76× lower than the cheapest vendor-native AI memory product. That finding is replicable on your machine, your corpus, your vendor keys, in five minutes for fifty cents (vendor API). The harness doesn't ask you to trust us; it verifies architecturally.

The reason this matters in conversation with your work: the dominant frame in AI infrastructure right now — *more compute, bigger models, frontier-tier API calls* — is itself a kind of institutional choice that benefits a small number of incumbents. The K528 finding is evidence that the architectural choice is not technically forced; it is path-dependent. Whether the frame can be displaced is the institutional-economics question. *Power and Progress* would predict it depends on whether the alternative architecture can build constituencies — workers, cooperatives, member-owners, third-party verifiers — fast enough to alter the political-economy of the choice before lock-in calcifies. The reproducibility pack is one architectural choice we made to lower the cost of constituency-building: any third party can verify the architectural-cost claim without trusting us, on consumer-scale spend, today.
