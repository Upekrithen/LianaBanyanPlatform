### WHERE THE BILL IS STRONG

The *American AI Sovereign Wealth Fund Act* gets the essential things right:

- **50% public share goal is right.** The AI wealth being generated from collective human output belongs partly to the public. Half is not too much.
- **Alaska Permanent Fund Dividend model is right.** Annual constituent-direct cash payments, not a government investment committee holding shares and voting proxies.
- **Constituent-direct payments mechanism is right.** Money to people, not to a fund manager who accumulates political power over time.
- **Naming OpenAI + Anthropic + xAI as anchor companies is right.** The IPOs of these specific companies this year are the trigger event  -  and the article correctly identifies that these three are the companies where the wealth concentration is most acute.

### WHERE THE BILL IS VULNERABLE

The *American AI Sovereign Wealth Fund Act* faces structural vulnerabilities that will be exploited in litigation and in legislative maneuvering. These are not arguments against your goal  -  they are arguments for a complementary mechanism that delivers the same goal while the bill fights through:

- **OpenAI is not profitable  -  no dividends to distribute.** Your op-ed acknowledges this directly. A sovereign wealth fund holding OpenAI equity at IPO receives shares in a company that has never posted a profit. The Alaska Permanent Fund model distributes investment returns. There are no returns yet. The fund distributes nothing to your constituents until OpenAI profitability  -  which is unresolved.
- **50% mandatory equity transfer leads to years of constitutional litigation.** The Takings Clause challenge will be filed within hours of passage. Corporate lawyers will argue this is an uncompensated taking of private property. The mechanism delivers nothing while the case winds through courts.
- **Companies will offshore IPOs or restructure to avoid equity reach.** xAI is already offshore-friendly. OpenAI's capped-profit restructuring creates ambiguity about what equity even means.
- **Board representation creates governance conflicts.** Who votes Treasury's shares in OpenAI against an acquisition that benefits public-shareholders but threatens national strategic interest? The fund manager becomes a political actor  -  exactly the centralized-power problem the Alaska Permanent Fund avoids by distributing cash rather than holding governance stakes.
- **One-time transfer doesn't capture future growth.** The bill locks public share at companies' current valuation at IPO. If these companies grow 10x over the next decade  -  which analysts project  -  the 50% stake is locked at the IPO price.
