# BST Episode — The Triple Double and the Choice to Be Motivated

## Episode Summary

This episode unpacks the Triple Double framework — three doublings from a $100/day base to $192K/year — and the deeper thesis beneath it: motivation is a choice, not something that happens to you. Covers the Babe Ruth strikeout paradox, the carnival dart game with unlimited throws, the Lottery Ticket Monkeys, and why starting a business with $2,000 beats every alternative the financial industry offers.

## Episode Outline

### Opening (5 min)
- Cold open: "Who motivates the motivator? The motivator does."
- The military version: opening a can of false enthusiasm. You're cold, you're tired, someone has to stand up first.
- The thesis: the willingness to choose today to be motivated is the single rarest thing in adults and the single most reliable predictor of who builds a life they chose on purpose.

### Act 1: The Number on the Wall (10 min)
- $100 a day. Five days. Forty-eight weeks. $24,000.
- The Triple Double: $24K → $48K → $96K → $192K.
- Why $192K? Not "I need it." It's the number where capability becomes real. Where the thing you want to work on — you can actually work on.
- Why forty-eight weeks, not fifty-two? Why five days, not seven? Because burnout is a leak. You can't climb a ladder you're too tired to grip.
- Why doubles? Psychological, not mathematical. "Do what you already did, and then do it again" is a tractable problem. Going 8× is paralyzing.

### Act 2: Swing for the Fences (15 min)
- Babe Ruth: 714 home runs. 1,330 strikeouts. Hall of Fame for the home runs. Nobody remembers the strikeouts.
- The Founder's chess stats: lost more than half. Two screenshots, months apart, same story. Kept getting better because the losing was how the learning happened.
- Ted Williams waited for the perfect pitch. But we're not playing Ted Williams' game.
- We're playing the carnival dart game. Pay once. Throw until you hit. How careful are you on throw number one?
- The attempts counter on your Helm only goes up. Header: "No Effort Is Wasted."

### Act 3: The Lottery Ticket Monkeys (15 min)
- The Indeed.com commercial (monkeys in suits, business plan = buy scratchers)
- $300/year average on lottery tickets. 1 in 300 million odds.
- The Bloom County half-price expired lottery ticket joke — the ticket was already expired when you bought it.
- Crypto is a lottery ticket wearing a lab coat.
- The stock market gives 7%. That's $140 on your $2,000 in a year. You are a passenger.
- The alternative: start a business. More leverage. In your own hands. Nothing has more upside, more dignity, than turning your own labor into something you own.

### Act 4: Earn Your Way to the Starting Line (10 min)
- The $2,000-$5,000 problem: this money is really hard to replace if the first try fails.
- That's exactly why Liana Banyan exists.
- Cold Start pathway: Cue Cards test demand in Marks. Treasure Maps find the audience. Campaign Forge builds the pitch. Red Carpet connects you to mentors. Recipe Pot assembles the team. Commerce Engine handles compliance.
- You come with your $2,000. You don't spend it. You validate in effort. Then you deploy with confidence.
- You earn your way to the starting line. Then when you step onto it, you already know you can win the race.

### Close (5 min)
- The three sayings on the office wall: Hit the Triple Double. Swing for the Fences. No Effort Is Wasted.
- "I need them too. I am not writing this from a mountaintop."
- Direct question to the listener: What would you work on if you knew the capability to do it was coming?
- Write it down. Put it somewhere you'll see it. Come back tomorrow.
- Sign off: "You build the Features. We're building the Board."
