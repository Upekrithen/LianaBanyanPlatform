## CORRECTION 1: Medallion Cascade — Language Fix

### Problem

Two phrases in the Medallion Cascade section triggered MLM and securities law exposure:

- **"genealogy chain"** — MLM downline language
- **"Early participants benefit from later participants' growth"** — Howey prong 4 (efforts of others)

### Corrected Paragraph (replace in Innovation 4)

> The Medallion Cascade is how ownership distributes. Fifty physical medallions, each worth $100, split recursively: 50 become 500 become 5,000 become 50,000. Total value is conserved. Breadth of ownership multiplies.
>
> When platform success events occur, surplus distributions are allocated proportionally across all medallion-holders — weighted by generation and contribution record. This mirrors how established worker cooperatives distribute patronage dividends: in proportion to each member's active participation in the cooperative's economic activity, not in proportion to who joined first or who recruited whom. A founding medallion-holder's larger distribution reflects their larger total contribution record, not a passive claim on others' labor.
>
> This inverts the usual dynamic of investor-owned platforms — where early equity concentrates returns at the top — by distributing ownership broadly from the outset, so that a larger, more active membership base creates more value for everyone through their own participation.

### Legal Rationale

| Risk | Original Language | Fixed Language |
|---|---|---|
| MLM / pyramid | "genealogy chain" | Removed; replaced with "contribution record" and "active participation" |
| Howey prong 4 | "Early participants benefit from later participants' growth" | "larger distribution reflects their larger total contribution record, not a passive claim on others' labor" |
| Howey prong 3 | Implied passive profit expectation | Reframed as patronage dividend — cooperative exemption applies |
| FTC pyramid concern | Recruitment-linked reward implied | Explicitly severed: "not…who recruited whom" |

## CORRECTION 2: Birthright Mechanic — Complete Rewrite

### Problem

Previous version used "redeemable equity," "claim on platform ownership," and "equity transfers permanently" — all of which create securities law exposure under Howey.

### Background: What Marks Actually Are

Marks are not a discount instrument and not freely tradeable surplus. They are:

- **Backed by real value** — either fiat Credits purchased at $1:1, OR the IP portfolio value (the patent portfolio backs the differential between what a weak-currency participant paid and what the platform received)
- **A forward obligation** — representing the holder's future participation that will make up the economic gap
- **Earned through work, not bought** — they clear through activity precisely because they represent that activity being owed

### Corrected Birthright Mechanic (replace in Innovation 1)

**The Birthright Mechanic:**

If a participant cannot or chooses not to clear their Marks obligation through participation:

- The original holder may redeem their Marks themselves at Credit rate — **1 Credit per Mark** — within **one year** of issuance
- If unredeemed after one year, the Marks **forfeit to Liana Banyan**
- Liana Banyan offers forfeited Marks to other members at the **Joules rate: 1.2 Credits per Mark**
- The purchasing member receives the Marks and clears them through their own platform participation
- Liana Banyan retains the **0.2 Credit margin** — Cost+20% is preserved at every layer

This mechanism echoes the story of Esau selling his birthright (Genesis 25:29–34). Unlike Esau, the holder has a full year and a fair buyback price. The platform neither forces nor prevents this outcome — it simply holds the obligation until the choice is made. **No equity transfers. No ownership changes hands. No profit expectation is created. The system remains closed, solvent, and whole.**

### Legal Rationale

| Old Language | Problem | Fixed Language |
|---|---|---|
| "redeemable equity" | Security / ownership claim | Removed entirely |
| "claim on platform ownership" | Howey common enterprise prong | Removed entirely |
| "equity transfers permanently" | Investment contract language | "Joules conversion transfers permanently to purchasing member" |
| Selling Marks as Joules directly | Lets holder cash out an unfulfilled obligation | Marks forfeit to LB first; LB resells at Cost+20% |

### The Economic Logic

You owed work. You didn't do it.

You had a year to buy your way out at par (1 Credit per Mark).

You didn't. The obligation reverts to the platform.

The platform resells it at Cost+20% to someone who **will** do the work.

The system is whole. The closed loop holds. No equity. No securities. No external conversion.

## CORRECTION 3: Joules — Complete Mechanics (replaces partial definition)

### Problem

Previous versions described Joules primarily as a strong-currency differential instrument. The full three-pathway issuance mechanic was not documented.

### Corrected Joules Definition (replace full Joules section in Innovation 1)

**Joules — Complete Mechanics:**

Joules are the platform's stored-value instrument. They are **never redeemable in fiat** — this is what keeps them categorically outside securities law at every issuance point.

**How Joules are issued (three pathways):**

**Pathway 1 — Standard acquisition (Cost+20%):**
A member purchases Joules at **1.2 Credits per Joule**. Each Joule retains the purchasing power of 1 Credit at the moment of acquisition — the **Forever Stamp mechanic**. The platform economy operates on a one-way ratchet: as the platform grows and Credits command more value over time, Joules purchased today retain their purchasing power at the historical rate of acquisition. They mail the letter — get the service — even when prices change.

**Pathway 2 — Volume pricing differential (automatic issuance):**
A member purchases a product at early production pricing. Production volume tiers determine Cost+20% pricing:

| Production Tier | Units | Price Example |
|---|---|---|
| Tier 1 | 50 units | $175 |
| Tier 2 | 500 units | ~$75 |
| Tier 3 | 5,000 units | ~$25 |
| Tier 4 | 15,000 units | Lower |
| Tier 5 | 50,000 units | Lower |
| Tier 6 | 500,000+ units | Extrusion-level pricing |

When production scales and the price drops — from $175 to $25, for example — the **$150 difference is not refunded in fiat**. It is issued to the original purchaser as **Joules at 1:1**. Dollar for dollar. Credit for Credit. Locked at the moment of issuance. **The early believer is honored.** The platform never loses margin — it redistributes it forward as stored platform value.

**Pathway 3 — Platform events and launches:**
During designated events (product launches, platform milestones, founding member windows), Joules may be issued at **1:1** rather than 1.2 — an earned benefit of early participation. These issuances are **bounded**, **documented**, and **tied to specific platform events**, not open-ended.

**Why all three pathways preserve integrity:**

- **Never redeemable in fiat** — in all three pathways, Joules stay inside the closed loop
- **Cost+20% holds everywhere** — even volume differential issuance is Cost+20% doing its job across production tiers
- **The Forever Stamp mechanic applies to all three** — whatever a Joule was worth at issuance, it retains that internal purchasing power regardless of what Credits do over time
- **1:1 event issuances are bounded** — they don't undermine the standard 1.2 rate because they are tied to specific, documented platform events or contractual volume pricing adjustments

The one-way ratchet, the closed loop, and the three issuance pathways together make Joules the most sophisticated instrument in the system — and the cleanest legally, precisely because they never point outward.
