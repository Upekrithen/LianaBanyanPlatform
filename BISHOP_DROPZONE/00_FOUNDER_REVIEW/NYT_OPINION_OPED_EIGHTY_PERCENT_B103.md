The standard advice for anyone who invents something is: file it, protect it, monetize it. Build the moat. Make it expensive for anyone else to do what you did. That advice is correct if you are playing a zero-sum game.

I am not playing a zero-sum game.

I grew up watching the well work. My parents were missionaries. I was a child in Africa. The thing that changed a village was not a hospital or a school — it was a clean water well. One piece of shared infrastructure that belonged to everyone and therefore maintained itself, because everyone had a stake in keeping it clean. I have been thinking about what the cooperative version of a clean water well looks like in an American economy for most of my life. The patent portfolio was the moment the answer stopped being theoretical.

## What I Built and Why I'm Giving Most of It Away

Over the last six months, working with a small team of AI agents, I produced 2,263 documented innovations, 222 of which have no prior art, and filed 13 patent provisionals with the United States Patent and Trademark Office. The flagship is a piece of AI infrastructure called the Romulator 9000 — a pre-loaded, verified context architecture that eliminates the waste built into every AI agent system operating today. Conservative estimates put that waste at 15 to 25 percent of every session, or one to three billion dollars a year in direct costs. The outer bound, accounting for downstream compute and engineer time, is closer to 87 billion.

I am giving roughly 80 percent of that portfolio into a cooperative patent commons.

Not because I don't know what it's worth. The opposite.

## The 80 Percent Is the Business Model

Here is the part that confuses people who spent the last forty years watching technology build moats: the give-away is not philanthropy. It is the mechanism by which the remaining 20 percent becomes worth anything.

A hundred percent retained and you have a fortress nobody wants to live near. A hundred percent given and you have a commons nobody has reason to defend. The eighty percent is the only number where cooperation costs less than defection — the give-away is large enough that joining the commons is worth more than free-riding, and the twenty percent is real enough that the person who built it has something to lose if it fails. Eighty is not a compromise. It is the tuning point where the incentives for all three parties — the builder, the large licensee, the small adopter — align at the same time.

The structure works like this. Any company — Microsoft, Google, Anthropic, Meta — has three choices about the Romulator. They can license the reference implementation directly. They can build their own implementation to our published specification, submit it to our Technology Compatibility Kit, and earn the right to mark their product "Romulator-Compliant 9000." Or they can join as a pledge-only member — no royalty, full patent grant, reciprocal pledge of their own related intellectual property back into the commons.

Every company that joins the third tier makes the commons stronger for every other member. Every reciprocal pledge adds to the defensive perimeter. The more platforms join, the stronger everyone's protection becomes — including ours.

This is not altruism dressed as strategy. It is strategy that only works if the altruism is real.

## Why Patents as Commons Rather Than Moats

The moat model has a ceiling. You can enforce a patent against one company at a time, in one jurisdiction at a time, while paying counsel by the hour. Even if you win every case, you spend resources defending instead of building. The moat rewards patience and capital — and the largest incumbents have the most of both.

The commons model inverts this. When your patent portfolio is a commons, enforcement is shared. Every member who joined because they benefited from the commons has an interest in defending it. The commons grows bigger and stronger the more people use it, rather than weaker.

*[BISHOP DRAFT — replace with your words:]*
In infantry doctrine, there is a principle called mutual support. Every defensive position must be able to protect the position next to it, and be protected by it in return. A line where each soldier fights only for himself is not a line — it is a collection of individuals waiting to be flanked one at a time. A line where each soldier knows his neighbor will cover his sector, and his neighbor knows the same, can hold against a force ten times its size. The patent commons works on that principle. Every company that pledges its related IP into the commons strengthens every other member's defensive position. The more join, the harder the commons is to flank. The goal is to make the perimeter strong enough that no one would try.

There is a precedent, though it is old. Before the patent system calcified into its current extractive form, patents were sometimes used exactly this way — to establish priority, share knowledge, and invite improvement, with the inventor's benefit coming from being the first and most trusted practitioner of the technology, not from locking everyone else out. The open-source software movement rediscovered this logic in the 1980s. The cooperative movement has known it for 200 years.

What is new is applying it to AI infrastructure at the moment when AI infrastructure is the most consequential technology sector on earth.

## What This Means for the Industry

Every AI company is currently paying a tax they don't know they're paying. Their agents re-ingest context every session. They rebuild what they already knew. The waste is invisible because it is distributed — no one line item on any engineering budget says "re-ingestion cost," but the aggregate across the industry is staggering.

The Romulator fixes this. And I am making it available — the specification, the Technology Compatibility Kit, the conformance mark — to any company willing to pledge their related IP back into the commons.

*[BISHOP DRAFT — replace with your words:]*
Think about a rural hospital system trying to deploy AI-assisted diagnostics. Or a school district running an AI tutoring pilot. Or a city government using AI to route emergency services. None of those organizations can pay the licensing fees that a major tech company negotiates. They are also the organizations where the efficiency gains from better AI context management matter most — where fifteen percent less waste means a nurse has fifteen percent more time. The commons is for them too. Not as charity: as design.

## The Bet

Giving away 80 percent of the portfolio is a bet that the commons becomes valuable enough to make the remaining 20 percent worth more than 100 percent of nothing would have been.

The alternative — litigating a portfolio this size against companies with legal budgets larger than my entire platform's projected revenue for the next decade — is not a real alternative. It is the appearance of strength in exchange for the substance of it.

My wife Diana is a photographer. My mother-in-law Rosario is a Spanish teacher who put herself through college after becoming a citizen, when my wife was in high school. I have eight children. None of them asked to be born into a family where their father spent nine years building a cooperative platform instead of taking the faster money. What I built this for — specifically, precisely, actually — is so that the licensing revenue from a well-defended commons shows up in their futures as something durable, something that doesn't depend on a board vote or a market cycle or whether I'm still in the room. Not a liquidity event. An annuity. That is not a noble reason for giving away eighty percent of a patent portfolio. It is a real one. And I think it is the reason the math works: I need the twenty percent to actually matter, which means I need the eighty percent to actually be given away, which means I need the commons to actually be worth joining. The stakes are not abstract.
