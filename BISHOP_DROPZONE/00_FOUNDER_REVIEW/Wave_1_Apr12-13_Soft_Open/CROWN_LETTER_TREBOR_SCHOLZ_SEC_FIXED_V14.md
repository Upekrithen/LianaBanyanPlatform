## What You Described, We Constructed — Including the AI Layer

Your SolidarityAI framework reframes AI not as artificial intelligence but as collective intelligence — acknowledging the human labor and communal knowledge that power these systems. That reframing is exactly how we built our AI governance.

I coordinate a multi-agent system — BISHOP (Anthropic Claude), KNIGHT (Cursor/OpenAI), ROOK (Google Gemini), PAWN (Perplexity) — where each agent has a defined role, a communication protocol, and a handoff mechanism. I ask the questions. They execute. I evaluate. I direct the next question. The human is the only node that reads all outputs and issues the next directive.

This is collective intelligence in the literal sense: multiple systems with different architectures, checked against each other, coordinated by a human who owns the judgment. No single AI is trusted without cross-verification. No output publishes without adversarial review across agents from different companies.

AI agents serve as temporary role-fillers in platform positions until human members take over. The AI is the glove. When a human is ready, they fill it and direct the AI, or take the glove off, at will. It's augmentation as architecture, not augmentation as marketing. The AI doesn't accumulate power. It holds a seat warm until someone earns it.

And in March 2026, the AI team built something I believe is a first: they designed and constructed their own institutional memory — a system called the Librarian that indexes every database table, every function, every page, every document, and every past conversation into a queryable knowledge base. The agents built the infrastructure that makes their own coordination automatic. No single AI holds the full picture, but together, through the Librarian, any agent can access the complete architectural state in seconds. Collective intelligence that literally built its own nervous system.

That's the solidarity stack applied to AI governance.

## The Specific Architecture Against Extraction

You've argued that cooperative AI must reject extractive logics at the infrastructure level — not patch them afterward with policy. Here's how the platform does that structurally:

**Cost+20% — Locked.** The margin is fixed in the corporate bylaws and cannot be changed by any vote, any board decision, or any future participant. On a $500 transaction, the provider keeps $416.50. Every transaction. Always. A constitutional triple-lock (3/4 member supermajority + 2/3 board supermajority + Golden Share veto) protects this provision.

**One-Way Valve.** Internal Credits enter the system through purchase at $1.00 per Credit. They do not exit as cash. The system is closed-loop by design. No secondary market exists or can be created.

**The Gleaner's Corner.** Built into the economic engine, not added as charity afterward: automated resource allocation to community organizations, modeled on ancient gleaning law. The platform cannot operate without sharing. It's structural, not optional.

**Universal Remote Work — By Design.** Every role on the platform can be performed remotely. Not "remote-friendly." Every single position — from content creation to governance to community coordination — is designed from the ground up for distributed work. A member in rural Montana has identical access to every opportunity as a member in Manhattan.

**Zero Demographics.** We don't just avoid collecting demographic data — we have no mechanism that can collect it. Age verification is handled by payment processing (required by law for membership), and that's it. No age, gender, race, income, education, or location field exists in the database. This isn't a limitation — it's a feature that enables ethically clean research without IRB concerns about protected populations.

These aren't principles posted on a wall. They're patented mechanisms. 2,224 documented innovations, approximately 2,393 formal claims across 12 provisional patent applications. The portfolio exists not to monetize cooperative economics, but to prevent anyone from strip-mining what we've built for member-participants.

The platform is live with 35 production systems deployed to lianabanyan.com — including a commerce engine, AI-powered dispute resolution (Star Chamber), cooperative vehicle sharing, housing systems, political engagement tools, a creative economy pipeline, and a measurement framework (ADAPT Score) that tracks system effectiveness across six dimensions.

## The Lemonade Stand Model: Research Infrastructure for Platform Cooperativism

Here's something I built specifically because of your work — and I think it addresses a gap in the platform cooperativism research toolkit.

The platform includes what I call the **Lemonade Stand Model**: an architecture that provides real users, real market dynamics, and minimal cost of failure. Researchers and entrepreneurs can run experiments using actual platform participants — not simulations — at membership prices starting at $5/year.

The system uses **Contingency Operators** — isolated sandboxes that fork from live platform state with a single-point change, then run forward to observe cascading effects. You get to practice with real people, but failure doesn't cost much.

**Why this matters for platform cooperativism research:**

The Zero Demographics principle creates something unusual: a platform where you can study cooperative economic behavior with real users, real transactions, and real outcomes — without the confounds of demographic targeting, and where the measurement infrastructure is built into the architecture rather than bolted on afterward.

Imagine being able to test whether the 83.3% creator share actually changes behavior differently than a 70% share — with real users, real participation, real decisions — at a cost of $5/year for membership. That's what this enables.
