Dear Mr. Doctorow,

I was a librarian in junior high, and again in college—because they couldn't find enough people who could alphabetize accurately, and it paid twenty five cents more per hour for work study.

The library was my refuge and my teacher. We didn't have television at home. I read every book in every library I could get to through grade school on different continents and in different states—every book except dictionaries and autobiographies. The librarian let me check out three to five a day because I brought them back the next day, every time. I got in trouble a lot for reading through class and for staying up all night reading at home.

This was before science fiction had its own Dewey Decimal designation. It was just "fiction," jammed in with everything else. You had to know what you were looking for, or stumble across it by accident.

I always loved science fiction because it's really about society and people and ideas, couched in stories. I read Ayn Rand and Mortimer J. Adler, but I preferred Asimov, McCaffrey, and Piers Anthony's Bio of a Space Tyrant because of the dynamics and experiments that space operas allow. When you read between the lines, science fiction tells the stories of a civilization's cultures, and you can often literally match the stories - or see where the author jumped off from - if you also read a lot of history. Science fiction is the best literature, in my book, because it encompasses all the others.

## The Word You Gave Us

You likely get written to about "enshittification" a lot. For good reason. I'm hoping this one earns a place in the stack.

The moment I read the word it resonated with what I wanted to fix. The pattern: a platform starts by being good to users, then shifts value to business customers, then extracts from everyone until the whole thing collapses under its own weight. You documented it. You named it. You made it visible.

I didn't find Pluralistic until recently, but I've been watching the same pattern destroy everything it touches since I was delivering newspapers in Montana at thirteen, walking five hours through freezing temperatures for nine cents a paper because the company set the terms and I had no leverage or other recourse. The pattern isn't new. It's just faster now.

I've been building the opposite for nine years.

## What I Built

Liana Banyan is a cooperative platform where creators keep 83.3% of every transaction—not because we're generous, but because that's what's left after Cost + 20%. The margin is fixed. It cannot change. It's in the operating agreement, with the same protection as the articles of incorporation.

The platform cannot enshittify, because the extraction mechanism is capped by what we call "testnet by design": the internal credits are non speculative, non transferable, tied to real goods and services. You can't pump and dump what you can't trade. The value stays in the work.

And we're a platform, not a fund: we coordinate, verify, record, and display. We don't hold pooled money waiting to be extracted. The money flows through to where it's going. We're infrastructure, not a toll booth.

Sixteen initiatives. 1,244 documented utility patent innovations. Seven patent applications with 210 formal claims given to the company. Built in public, documented in plain sight, designed to be copied.

## Why I'm Writing to You

You understand that the fight isn't about any single platform. It's about the pattern—the incentive structure that makes enshittification inevitable unless you design against it from the beginning, as I have.

You've also amplified digital humanities work showing that no single community can be everything to everyone—that people belong to overlapping groups because each one can only satisfy some of what they need. I've been designing for multiple overlapping initiatives under one cooperative canopy, with the economics and provenance locked in but the social spaces free to specialize and fork when they need to.

I'm not asking for money or endorsement. I need your scrutiny, please.

You've spent years documenting how platforms fail. I've spent years building one that can't fail in the same way. If I'm wrong, I want to know. If there's a flaw in the architecture, it needs to be found and fixed.

And if it holds up—if the math works, if the structure is sound, if this is actually what I think it is—then I want you to know it exists. Because you've been writing about what we need, and I believe it's been built.

## The Science Fiction Connection

In my own life, and in the lives of the children my parents fostered, I longed for dependable systemic solutions to pervasive, repeating problems. The frustration of recognizing how arbitrary, brittle interoperability created self propagating failures—with no recourse—was palpable. If only it worked this other way, the way it did in this or that book. Or if only we'd learned from this or that piece of history. But I did learn.

Some of my favorites are not popular, for good reasons, but when I was thrust into terrible knowledge—the true stories children tell each other that adults are never told—they gave me a way to understand and sympathize with violent trauma responses. In the face of that despair, hope came through Donaldson's caamora of Saltheart Foamfollower, through the "engine" in Steakley's Armor, through Heinlein's citizenship in Starship Troopers. These were heavy concepts for a ten year old, but they stuck, especially when I saw what citizenship really meant in the book, not the chapter the movie was made from: rights with responsibilities, earning your place in the system by contributing to it. Heinlein wasn't writing military propaganda. He was writing about skin in the game.

Orson Scott Card's *A Planet Called Treason* was revolutionary to me. The Black Nation built an entire civilization in the canopy because the ground was death; they didn't bother fighting the system—they built around it. We're building in that canopy. The ground—the extractive platforms, the enshittified systems—is death. We're not fighting them. We're building around them.

![A Planet Called Treason](/images/letters/a-planet-called-treason.webp)

The Haruchai in Thomas Covenant swore a Vow of eternal vigilance. They never slept. They watched. I named our moderation system after them: AI sock puppets controlled by humans, with the power to render content inert—not deleted, just frozen—pending review. Every action logged. Every decision accountable. The chief Haruchai is Bannor. He never sleeps. He's always watching.

McCaffrey's Harper Hall on Pern wasn't just a musicians' guild. It was the institution that preserved knowledge, trained talent, and gave craft its dignity in every Hold and Weyr. You earned your rank through demonstrated mastery—not purchase, not inheritance, not politics. The Guild existed to protect the work and the people who did it, just like ours.

Heinlein's citizenship model in Starship Troopers said you earn your place by contributing. Our medallion system works the same way. You don't buy status. You earn it through participation. The people who build the platform own the platform—not because we gave it to them, but because they built it. McCaffrey understood this. Heinlein understood this. The Harper Hall and the Mobile Infantry got there by different roads and arrived at the same place: skin in the game, earned belonging, no shortcuts.

Science fiction has always been about asking: what if we built it differently? I'm not asking anymore. I'm building.
