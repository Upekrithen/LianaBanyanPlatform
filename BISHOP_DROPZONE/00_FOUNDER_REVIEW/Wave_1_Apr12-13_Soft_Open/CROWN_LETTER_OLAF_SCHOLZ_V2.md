Dear Chancellor Scholz,

I am writing to you because Germany invented what I am building — and I would like to bring it back to where it started.

In 1864, Friedrich Wilhelm Raiffeisen organized the first cooperative credit unions in the Rhineland. His principle was simple: people who know each other can serve each other better than distant institutions can. One hundred sixty years later, the Raiffeisen cooperatives employ over 900,000 people across Germany. The cooperative model works. Germany proved it.

I am Jonathan Jones, founding manager of Liana Banyan Corporation, a U.S. Army National Guard veteran of no particular note, and the father of eight children. Over the past nine years, I have been building a cooperative platform that applies Raiffeisen's insight to the digital economy — a system where local businesses, makers, delivery drivers, designers, and families coordinate their economic activity through a single cooperative infrastructure, with every transaction priced at Cost+20% and every participant earning governance authority through demonstrated contribution.

**What Liana Banyan Does**

Our platform connects small-business owners, independent workers, and community members through a closed-loop economic system. A restaurant owner lists her menu. A neighbor photographs the food and delivers it to nearby offices. The cook keeps 83.3% of every sale. The photographer earns cooperative service credits. The delivery driver accumulates toward eventual vehicle ownership through our Earn-Down program. The office worker gets fresh breakfast at her desk. Nobody was rescued. Everybody benefited.

We call this Help Each Other Help Ourselves — or HEOHO. The scriptural foundation is 1 Corinthians 12:21: "The eye cannot say to the hand, 'I don't need you.'" The economic foundation is interdependence — the same principle that built Raiffeisen's credit unions.

**The Architecture**

The platform operates on three currencies — Credits (transactional), Marks (effort-tracking), and Joules (stored value) — all at permanent parity. The margin is constitutionally locked at Cost+20%, meaning no governance vote, no board decision, and no founder's whim can ever increase the platform's take. This eliminates the extraction creep that has made platforms like DoorDash and Uber progressively more extractive over time.

We have documented 1,938 innovations, filed 8 provisional patent applications encompassing 1,401 formal claims, and deployed 20 production systems to lianabanyan.com. The entire platform was built by one founder and four specialized AI agents — an organizational model we describe in our paper "How to Bake AI Cake."

**Why Germany**

Germany's social market economy — the *Soziale Marktwirtschaft* — is the closest existing national framework to what Liana Banyan proposes at the platform level. Your nation already understands that markets work best when embedded in social institutions. Your cooperative infrastructure (the Genossenschaften) already demonstrates that community-owned enterprises can operate at scale. Your *Mittelstand* already proves that locally-rooted businesses outperform extractive alternatives over generational timescales.

What Germany does not yet have is a **digital cooperative platform** that connects these traditions to the modern service economy. Liana Banyan could be that platform — not as an American import, but as a collaboration between the country that invented cooperatives and the platform that digitized them.

**What I Am Asking**

I am not asking for funding, endorsement, or political support. I am asking for connection.

Germany has institutions — the Friedrich-Ebert-Stiftung, the Deutscher Genossenschafts- und Raiffeisenverband (DGRV), university cooperative research centers — that have studied cooperative economics longer than any other country. I would welcome the opportunity to present Liana Banyan's architecture to any of these institutions for evaluation, critique, and potential partnership.

If our model holds up under German scrutiny — and I believe it will, because it follows Raiffeisen's principles with modern infrastructure — then the platform could serve as a bridge between Germany's cooperative tradition and America's gig economy. A cooperative alternative to DoorDash. A digital Genossenschaft.

We amplify you. We amplify the cooperative tradition you represent. We amplify the principle that Raiffeisen proved 160 years ago: that ordinary people, organized well, can outperform any institution that claims to serve them from above.

I would be honored to share our technical documentation, academic papers, and patent filings with anyone in your network who studies cooperative economics. The platform is live. The architecture is published. All it needs is the judgment of people who understand cooperatives at the deepest level — and nobody understands them deeper than Germany.

With respect and admiration for the tradition you represent,

**Jonathan Jones**
*Founding Manager, Liana Banyan Corporation*
*U.S. Army National Guard veteran of no particular note*
*Father of eight. Builder of cooperative infrastructure.*
