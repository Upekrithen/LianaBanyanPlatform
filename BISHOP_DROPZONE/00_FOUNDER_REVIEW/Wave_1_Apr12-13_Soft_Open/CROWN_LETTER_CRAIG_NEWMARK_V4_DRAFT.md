# Crown Letter: Craig Newmark
## Infrastructure Chancellor — Liana Banyan Corporation (Offered)

**February 2026**

---

Dear Mr. Newmark,

You accidentally killed the newspaper classified business and then spent years funding journalism to make up for it. That's either guilt or integrity — probably both — and it tells me you understand that building something useful can break something necessary, and that the builder has a responsibility to notice.

I am a veteran — Infantry and Aviation, 11B and 15A. I mention because what I'm writing to ask about affects veteran causes, civic tech, and trustworthy information infrastructure - all three of which you have put significant resources into.

## What I Am Asking

Not money. I am asking for your network — and your unvarnished judgment.

You have spent decades finding and backing people who build trustworthy things. I need introductions to operators who know how to scale community-driven solutions without breaking them: a fractional CTO who has seen what happens when good ideas meet bad architecture, someone in food security who can tell me what I do not know yet, or a veteran-serving leader who understands how fragile trust is once you take other people's stakes into your hands.

I know enough to know I don't know enough to get it right the first time. And yet I must. My military service harshly taught me: you are not a leader until you fail. In war, failure is almost always catastrophic in consequence, and yet, you remain in charge. Giving up is not an option if you care about the result of your failure — the only thing to do is use what you learned for what comes next. Humbled. Also savagely taught is to prepare in advance for predictable outcomes, as well as have contingency plans for unpredictable ones. That is why I've obsessed and over-engineered, but I still know that No Plan Survives First Contact. So my mission deserves leadership more than me, because I am unwilling to allow a single casualty, and will agonize when it happens anyway. Especially from friendly fire.

One connection from you could save a year of trial and error when people are choosing between insulin and rent.

I am prepared to hand any or all of these initiatives, and even the whole platform, to trusted, capable people. If this resonates beyond feedback and introductions, there is a seat at the table reserved for only you. I'm offering the Infrastructure Chancellor Crown — a permanent autonomous governance role overseeing platform integrity, community trust standards, and the architectural decisions that determine whether this stays trustworthy at scale. One Crown, One Offer. No one else is getting this letter for this role. We have no backup plan — because we want you to know how wanted you are.

---

## The Problem

Every community need — food, medicine, housing, legal help, childcare — has been turned into a profit extraction mechanism. The platforms that were supposed to connect people instead take 30%, 40%, 50% of every transaction, and when they IPO, the people who built the communities get nothing.

GoFundMe takes a cut of medical emergencies. Uber takes a cut of someone driving their own car. Etsy takes a cut of handmade crafts. The platform always eats first.

The little guys who started their own terrain systems, their own platforms, their own dreams — they are not enemies. They are us, from a different timeline. Traditional acquisition says, "We will buy you out for pennies, take your IP, and you will never see it again." Our philosophy is the opposite: we do not crush competitors; we invite them in and pay them what we would want to be paid.

---

## What We Built

Liana Banyan might be what Craigslist would look like if you designed it today, knowing everything you know now about what platforms become.

Cost + 20%. That is our margin on everything — not "up to 20%," exactly 20% over actual, provable cost, locked in the operating agreement. Every member can start a business for just five dollars, build their own designs or products, or get a job in another member's business — all under the same cooperative terms. Three commercial websites sustainably fund sixteen charitable initiatives through a baked-in "Cost of Doing Good" margin. The infrastructure is backed by 1,200 documented innovations — 99% utility patents, not design — eight of which survived a 130-query deep dive against the U.S. patent office with no prior art found, and are contributed to the platform under the same terms as every member.

Workers own the platform. Every person who contributes earns medallions — notarized, non-tradeable, test-net-by-design blockchain records of contribution that confer governance and revenue rights inside the cooperative.

We have built sixteen ready-to-launch initiatives across food security, healthcare access, education, financial inclusion, and crisis response — from neighbors feeding neighbors (Let's Make Dinner) to volume grocery purchasing and community buying power, to affordable prescriptions (LifeLine Medications), group legal defense, and cooperative manufacturing (Let's Make Bread).

The platform is live. The operating agreement locks the margin. The first medallions are minted. Our economics show that 500 members in any locale reach break-even and around 1,000 they are sustainably profitable. Everything is published at Cephas.LianaBanyan.org — nothing behind an NDA, nothing behind a paywall. Built in Public.

Behind all of that is an acquisition ethic I think you would recognize. When we find small creators who built something cool and never quite scaled — terrain makers, indie tool builders, solo platform founders — we do not strip-mine their work. We quantify their opportunity cost (time, money, revenue, community, IP), pay the higher of a healthy minimum or proven value, let them keep rights to their own IP, and give them Founder status and MARKS so they start inside our ecosystem with a real head start. In short: we give them the deal we wish the big guys had offered us.
