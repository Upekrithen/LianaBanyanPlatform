# Cardboard Boots: An Open Letter to MacKenzie Scott

*I don't want your money. I'm asking for something far more valuable: your reputation.*

> Publish-ready for Substack (primary) and Medium (repost). Cleanup and formatting only; the Founder's content and voice are preserved. See the "Founder review flags" at the bottom before publishing publicly.

---

Dear Ms. Scott,

I don't want your money. I'm asking for something far more valuable: your reputation.

In college, as a need-based scholarship student, I once had two roommates. One was wealthy. His family literally owned ten homes. The other was poor like me. When I needed a suit and couldn't afford one, my poor roommate immediately gave me one of the two he owned. Only afterward did my wealthy roommate offer one of his fifteen. That sacrifice thirty years ago still makes me cry, and it exemplifies the truth you wrote about: which gift means more, $100 from someone earning $50,000 or $100,000 from someone with millions. I don't have money, but I may have something more valuable.

This spring we filed another provisional patent, the Romulator 9000, a context-persistence architecture for multi-session AI agents. In controlled benchmarking it was measured at 92.7% accuracy against 3.6% for the same model without the preload, at comparable marginal cost. For a 200-person nonprofit running AI agents at production scale, the difference is hundreds of thousands of dollars a year, and better work. And under our Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge, signed into the operating agreement, the R9 is given permanently to a commons, free for every nonprofit, cooperative, and academic institution with an IRS-verified EIN. No application, no committee, no gatekeeping. Every organization your foundation has ever funded is eligible on day one. That is what I bring to your table, alongside the ask.

Over the last nine years and six months I've built a public, community-owned safety net of sixteen charitable initiatives designed to sustain themselves far beyond our lifetimes, focused on economic mobility, food security, and cooperative healthcare for people who experience the sharpest side of inequality, and I'm seeking a word from you to experienced leaders in your network who could help them succeed at scale.

I've hand-picked leaders in each field whom I'm asking (Michael Seibel for CEO, Tom Simon, a 26-year FBI forensic accountant, for CFO, Sal Khan for education, José Andrés for food security) because I pray for potatoes at the end of a hoe handle, but your endorsement would exponentially multiply every ask I make. I'm prepared to hand any or all of these initiatives, and even the entire platform, to leaders you trust. Even one connection to someone experienced in food security or cooperative healthcare, who can vet the pitfalls I don't know about yet, could prevent costly mistakes and multiply impact. I know enough to know I don't know enough.

My specific ask: if our work aligns with your values, would you point operators from your network my direction? People who excel at scaling community-driven solutions, or ethical CTO, COO, or operational candidates, even fractional, who could steward this platform beyond my limited capacity.

This isn't charity TO the People but infrastructure BY the People FOR the People, "nothing about us without us," which applies your principle that communities removing obstacles for different groups become better for everyone. It is member-owned infrastructure: the people most affected help govern and benefit from the tools built to serve them.

Every member can start a business for just $5, build their own designs or products, or get a job in another member's business, all under the same cooperative terms. Three commercial websites sustainably fund the sixteen charitable initiatives on a fourth, through a baked-in 20% "Cost of Doing Good" margin. The infrastructure is backed by 2,270 documented innovations (99% utility patents, not design) and 228 of those survived rigorous prior-art searches against the U.S. patent office with no prior art found anywhere. Twenty-one provisional patent applications have now been filed. Eighty percent of that portfolio is pledged permanently into the commons. What we need is people and leadership; the money will follow.

I'm launching the alpha by using half of my family's emergency savings to seed the launch through our medallion sponsorship program: a no-strings-attached $100 sponsorship each to ten waitresses, bus drivers, and laborers who would never otherwise see any of these opportunities to change their lives. Each recipient can share their sponsorship forward into ten $10 memberships, or more with involvement, bringing their own communities into the cooperative. One bus driver becomes ten transit workers becomes hundreds, understanding they can have their own part of what they themselves and others work to achieve, the chain reaction that can go on for years. And under the Pledge, that same cooperative architecture is available to every nonprofit your foundation has ever funded, on the same terms.

Our economics show that 500 members in any locale reach break-even, and around 1,000 they are sustainably profitable, with surplus dedicated to local initiatives. Math says it's viable; my job is to prove it works in real life so others can count on it.

If you'd like to see the architecture before making any introductions, there's a walkthrough at LianaBanyan.com/RedCarpet that will recognize your email. No scheduling, no pitch deck, no salesman.

One bounded matter, and only because the deadline is real: the provisionals must be converted to full patents by November 26, 2026, or they lapse. The prosecution funding is on course for most of the portfolio, not all. To protect the commons from enclosure if any slip, we keep an arms-length vehicle inside Upekrithen LLC: the Patent Prosecution Defense Fund. It exists solely to convert the patents; it dissolves when they convert. It accepts directed contributions. No equity, no naming, no board seat. That option is on the table and stays on the table, but it is not the ask. The ask remains the introduction.

And if this resonates beyond introductions, there's a seat at the table. I'm offering you the Board Chair Crown, a permanent governance role overseeing both the sixteen initiatives and the commons itself. One Crown, One Offer. But the introductions alone would change everything.

The medallions are minted. The platform is built. The Pledge is signed. The commons is open. The first ten members are ready. What I lack is the wisdom and experienced leadership to ensure we serve millions, not hundreds. And I have two suits.

With shared purpose,

Jonathan Jones

Founder and General Manager, Liana Banyan Corporation
406-578-1232 | Founder@LianaBanyan.com

*P.S. Naturally I'll move forward either way, but now I have asked. "I bargained with Life for a penny..." (Jessie B. Rittenhouse)*

## Founder review flags (remove before publishing)

**SECURITIES / FINANCIAL-RETURN review (this is going public, so vet these):**

1. **Patent Prosecution Defense Fund paragraph.** The original named "directed contributions at the five-to-ten-million level." I removed the specific dollar level so the published letter does not read as a solicitation of a fixed-size financial instrument. The paragraph already says "no equity, no naming, no board seat," which is good. RECOMMEND counsel confirm this reads as a charitable/directed contribution, not a securities offering, before public posting. If counsel prefers, drop this paragraph entirely for the public version and keep it only for the private send to Scott.

2. **Medallion sponsorship cascade.** "$100 sponsorship... share forward into ten $10 memberships... chain reaction." This is framed as gift-and-membership, not return-on-money, which is the right framing. But the "one becomes ten becomes hundreds" cascade can read as MLM/recruitment-for-return to an unfriendly reader. RECOMMEND a one-line clarifier that members receive cooperative membership and governance, not a financial return, and that no one earns money for recruiting.

3. **"Eighty percent of that portfolio is pledged permanently into the commons."** This 80% is the patent-to-commons pledge figure, distinct from the 83.3% creator-keep canon. Confirmed it is NOT the creator-keep number, so it is left as-is. Verify 80% is still the current pledge figure before publishing.
