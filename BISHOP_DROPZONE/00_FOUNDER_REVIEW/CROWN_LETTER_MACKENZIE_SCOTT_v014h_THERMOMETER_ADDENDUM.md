> The measurement does not require anyone to take my word for it. The R9 architecture ships as an open-source server any of your grantees can install in less than a minute, on their own computer, against whatever AI they already use. The evidence for the $115–$280 million per year is something your network produces, not something I supply.

## The quiet structural shift

With the thermometer paragraph added, the letter makes a different kind of claim. v014f says *"our measurement shows 92.7% accuracy."* v014h says *"our measurement is a tool your grantees can run; the number they produce will show up on their terminal."* The ask remains the same — introductions, not money — but the *grounds for the ask* move from **trust the Founder's benchmark** to **verify the benchmark on your own data**. For a donor whose published principles emphasize listening to grantees and letting communities define their own needs, handing the grantees a verification instrument is structurally aligned with how she already chooses to give.

This is also a partial answer to the Paper Trail question ("how do you prove the system works outside your own environment?"). The honest answer was always *"adopt it and find out."* With the thermometer, the answer becomes *"install it and watch the meter."* Neither answer demands Scott's trust beyond trying the tool.

## B111 cross-vendor finding — optional expansion of the addendum paragraph

The Eyewitness Benchmark (B111 / K423, April 20–21 2026) replicated the R9 measurement across **all four major AI vendor families: Anthropic, Google, OpenAI, and Perplexity. Eight frontier models. Twelve hundred inference calls. Single-blind. Double-graded, with inter-rater agreement of 0.883 and 0.850 — both "almost perfect."** Mean HOT accuracy: **94.8%**. Mean COLD: **8.7%**. An **86-percentage-point mean lift**, cross-vendor, including the vendor who made the grader.

**The finding most relevant to the Scott grant case** has two parts.

For **volume-heavy workloads** (case management, grant research, communications — the 80% that doesn't require canonical authority): Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash with our preload scored **94.7% accuracy at $0.0007 per question**. Cheaper than any other tier we tested. For a mid-sized Yield Giving grantee running 10,000 AI queries per day, that is roughly **$2,500 per year in total inference costs** — compared with $20,000–$40,000 per year on premium-tier options that, without the preload, deliver *lower* accuracy. The difference is one staff position the grantee gets to keep.

For **canonical-authority workloads** (legal, compliance, program reporting): Claude Haiku 4.5 with our preload scored **98.7% accuracy at $0.0066 per question — identical accuracy to Claude Opus 4.7 at one-nineteenth the cost.** A grantee running ten thousand of those queries a day lands at roughly $24,000/year on Haiku versus $464,000/year on Opus. Same accuracy. Nineteen-to-one cost ratio.

Together the two findings change the grant math from *"can this grantee afford to use AI at all"* to *"which cheap tier matches the task."* That is the shape of a preload that removes the inference-cost barrier entirely, rather than lowering it at the margin.

If Founder wants to add this to the thermometer addendum, proposed insertion after the first sentence of the proposed new paragraph (between "…not something I supply." and the next original paragraph):

> *"To be concrete about the per-grantee economics: Google's Gemini 2.5 Flash with our preload produced ninety-five percent accuracy at seven hundredths of a penny per question — a mid-sized grantee running ten thousand daily queries lands at roughly twenty-five hundred dollars a year in total inference costs. For canonical-authority work, Claude Haiku delivered ninety-nine percent — identical to Claude Opus at one-nineteenth the cost. The difference between premium-tier AI without context and cheapest-tier AI with our preload is one staff position the grantee gets to keep."*
