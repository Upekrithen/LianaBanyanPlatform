## Spoonful 182-1: The Handshake Economy

My son's car broke down. The shop sent a tow truck, bought the parts, put a tech on it, and we picked up the car the same day. No invoice. No deposit. No credit application. Just a phone call and the words "we'll work something out." That's not charity. That's a four-currency transaction settled on trust built over nine months of showing up.

## Spoonful 182-2: You Already Have a Joule Reservoir

You've helped a neighbor move. You've picked up someone's kid from school. You've had a friend fix your computer or cook for you when you were sick. Somebody has done those things for you. You already have a Joule reservoir. You've already made Marks draws on other people's reservoirs. All we did was name the pieces and build a place where it keeps working when the people change.

## Spoonful 182-3: The Four Currencies Were Already Running

Credits (cash at cost). Marks (the relationship differential). Joules (surplus effort already banked). Backed Marks (trust you can see with the naked eye). My auto shop and I built this system without vocabulary. Most of you have one too — with a neighbor, a sister-in-law, a kid down the street. The most durable economic relationships in your life don't require a credit score.

## Spoonful 182-4: Ninety-Two Years of Proof

Think mutual credit is fringe? The WIR Bank in Switzerland has been running since 1934. Ninety-two years. Tens of thousands of businesses. Billions in volume. Or Sardex in Sardinia — launched during the 2009 debt crisis when banks stopped lending. Still running. Still growing. This isn't new economics. This is how people have always traded when they trust each other.

**#LianaBanyan #WIRBank #Sardex #MutualCredit #History**

## Spoonful 182-5: The Only Thing Missing

The Amish barn-raising works because everyone knows everyone. But most Americans don't live in villages anymore. We live in cul-de-sacs surrounded by people whose names we don't know. Liana Banyan adds the part that was missing: a way to carry your reservoir with you. A way for a stranger to see that your word is good — honestly, with receipts, without having to take anyone's word for it.

My son drove his own car home today because I wired a network nine months ago. That's not a metaphor. That's a four-currency transaction settled on a handshake, closed on the exact day the essay was written. Credits for the parts. Marks for the priority. Joules for the reservoir. Time-patient settlement for the clock. That's how it works. That's the whole thing.
