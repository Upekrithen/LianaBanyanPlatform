# OPTION A — Let's Make Dinner + Family Table
## Neighbors Feeding Neighbors

---

**The one-line version:** Rosario, you know how to feed a room. This initiative makes that a business.

---

### What It Is

Let's Make Dinner is a cooperative home-kitchen network. Members cook and sell meals to their neighbors through the platform. No restaurant. No commercial kitchen required in most states. Just a home kitchen, a recipe, and people who want a real meal.

Family Table is the paired initiative — a shared meal reservation system where neighbors book a seat at each other's tables. Think of it as an open invitation to the neighborhood.

In San Antonio, this is natural. Food is how the community already works. This is just the platform that lets it pay.

### What Rosario Would Captain

- Sign up the first 10 home cooks in the neighborhood as platform members
- Host the first Family Table dinner at La Capital del Sabor (anchor partner) as the launch event
- Help cooks set up their menus and schedules
- Share the Cue Card with every neighbor who says "I can cook"

### The Economics

A home cook sells 20 meals at $15 each:
- Weekly gross: $300
- Platform fee (16.7%): $50
- Cook keeps: **$250/week → $1,000/month**

At 50 active cooks in the Node:
- Node transaction volume: $50,000/month
- Node margin (Cost+20%): flows to Node operations + LifeLine + community fund

### Who This Is For

Best fit if Rosario wants to build through the people she already knows — neighbors with kitchens, family cooks, abuelitas who have been feeding the block for free for years. This initiative turns that informal economy into a recognized, paid one.

**San Antonio advantage:** The city's food culture is deep. Heritage recipes, cultural cooking, multi-generational knowledge. The demand is already there. The platform is the distribution.

# OPTION B — Let's Get Groceries
## Cooperative Grocery Access

---

**The one-line version:** Buying together costs less. The platform organizes the buying.

---

### What It Is

Let's Get Groceries is a cooperative bulk purchasing initiative. Members pool their grocery orders — buying in bulk quantities that get wholesale pricing — and split the haul. The platform coordinates the orders, the pickup schedule, and the accounting. No one has to organize it manually.

### What Rosario Would Captain

- Identify 20–30 households willing to coordinate grocery orders
- Set up the first weekly order cycle (the platform runs the logistics)
- Partner with La Capital del Sabor as the first pickup location
- Grow to 100 households by month 3

### The Economics

A household spending $400/month on groceries saves 15–30% through bulk cooperative purchasing:
- Savings per household: **$60–$120/month**
- At 100 households: **$6,000–$12,000/month returned to members**

The platform earns Cost+20% on the coordination service, not on the groceries. Members pay less; the cooperative sustains itself.

### Who This Is For

Best fit if Rosario wants an initiative with immediate, tangible impact that every family can see in their grocery bill. No cooking required. No selling required. Just organizing people who are already buying food.

**San Antonio advantage:** Large families, multi-generational households, and tight-knit neighborhoods mean bulk ordering is culturally natural. This initiative formalizes what neighbors already do informally.

# OPTION C — LifeLine Medications *(Recommended)*
## Prescription Access at Cooperative Cost

---

**The one-line version:** Your diabetes medication is too expensive. So is everyone else's. The cooperative buys together and passes the savings through.

---

### Why This Is Personal

Rosario, this one was built partly because of your situation. When a family member's medication costs $400 a month and the insurance doesn't cover it — or covers it partially — that's not a personal problem. That's a structural one. Millions of families in San Antonio face the same math.

LifeLine Medications is the initiative that says: we are buying together, and the savings are yours.

### What It Is

A cooperative prescription purchasing network. Members pool their prescription needs. The cooperative negotiates with pharmacies, PBMs (pharmacy benefit managers), and direct manufacturers using combined purchasing power. Members pay at cost plus the platform's 20% margin — which is, in most cases, dramatically less than retail.

For common maintenance medications (diabetes, blood pressure, cholesterol, thyroid): 40–70% savings are typical when purchasing cooperatively vs. retail pharmacy without insurance.

### What Rosario Would Captain

- Sign up the first 50 member households who have regular prescription needs
- Work with the platform's pharmacy partnership network to establish the San Antonio Node's purchasing account
- Be the neighbor people call when the pharmacy bill is wrong
- Host the first community education event: "What you're actually paying for, and what you don't have to"

The platform handles the compliance, the pricing database, and the transactions. Rosario handles the community trust — which is worth more than any database.

### The Economics

At 50 member households, each saving $80/month on prescriptions:
- Monthly savings returned to members: **$4,000/month**
- Platform operational margin (Cost+20%): sustains the Node
- At 200 households: **$16,000/month in savings** flowing back to the community

This is not charity. The cooperative is buying what its members already need, at better prices, because it's buying together.

### Why LifeLine for the First Node

Of the four options, LifeLine creates the fastest emotional connection between the platform and the neighborhood. Everyone has a prescription. Everyone knows the markup. When the first member fills their medication through the Node and pays half what they paid last month, the proof is on the receipt.

**The receipt is the marketing.**

**San Antonio advantage:** Large Hispanic population with elevated rates of diabetes and hypertension — the diseases with the highest prescription burden. Rosario's own story is the Node's founding story. That's not a marketing angle. That's the truth.

# OPTION D — MSA — Mutual Shared Assets
## What the Neighborhood Owns Together

---

**The one-line version:** Not everyone needs a drill every day. The neighborhood owns one. The platform manages the schedule.

---

### What It Is

Mutual Shared Assets is a cooperative tool and equipment library. Members contribute things they own but rarely use — power tools, lawn equipment, specialty kitchen equipment, folding tables, vehicles, trailers — to the Node's shared inventory. Other members borrow them for a small Mark-denominated fee. The platform tracks availability, handles the schedule, and manages the small charges that sustain the library.

It starts with a drill. It grows to a pickup truck.

### What Rosario Would Captain

- Inventory what 20 neighbors own and would share
- Set up the first lending pool
- Establish the borrowing rules (how long, what deposit, what happens if something breaks)
- Grow the library as the Node grows

### The Economics

A member contributing a $300 power tool earns Marks every time it's borrowed:
- 20 borrows × 3 Marks each = 60 Marks/year from one tool
- At 200 items in the library: Node generates thousands of Marks in circulation monthly

The cooperative doesn't buy the assets — members contribute what they have. The platform provides the management infrastructure. The savings come from not having to buy things you use twice a year.

**San Antonio advantage:** Large households, homeowners, multi-generational family properties — there's significant existing asset wealth that sits unused most of the time. MSA captures that value and circulates it through the community.

### Who This Is For

Best fit if Rosario wants an initiative that starts small, has low friction, and can grow organically without requiring a big behavioral change from members. People already lend things to neighbors. MSA formalizes it and adds accountability.

This is the quietest of the four options — the one that builds trust through reliability rather than urgency. Different communities need different entry points.

## RECOMMENDATION: OPTION C

**LifeLine Medications** is the recommendation for First Node San Antonio because:

1. **Rosario's personal story is the founding story.** The initiative exists because of real people paying too much for real medications. She's not pitching something abstract — she's captaining something she has lived.

2. **Fastest proof of impact.** When someone's prescription bill drops by $80 in month one, the platform has proven itself in a way no marketing can match.

3. **Community trust in San Antonio.** A Spanish-speaking educator with a citizenship journey navigating the American healthcare system as Captain is the most credible possible face for this initiative in this city.

4. **Emotional hook for the Opening Gambit.** The Highway Painter Pudding, the Three-Week Synopsis, and the Romulator story are for journalists and investors. The First Node San Antonio story — with Rosario as Captain — is for everyone else. It's the human proof that all the infrastructure is real.

**She doesn't have to choose C.** The decision is hers. But if she's asking what her son's father-in-law would choose for her — it's C.
