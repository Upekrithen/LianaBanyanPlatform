## Background (one paragraph)

The Liana Banyan Cooperative has developed an empirically-validated substrate-grounded retrieval architecture (the *Cathedral Effect*, R10/R12/R13: +86.2pp mean accuracy lift across 4 commercial AI vendors at order-of-magnitude lower cost). The Cooperative proposes to license this architecture to commercial AI vendors via a Tiered Vendor Adoption Framework: identical baseline terms for all vendors, with time-window-stratified value-add tiers (Founder Vendor / Pioneer / Adopter / Cooperative), each requiring vendor signature on the Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge (#2260). Vendors at non-floor tiers contribute cash + equity to the Cooperative; receive Branded Fork rights, Preview Window access, and co-marketing; subject to a Member-Portability Covenant ensuring members retain rights to use the substrate via any vendor or local inference. Counsel review is requested on three issues before any vendor distribution.

## Issue 1 — Antitrust posture of time-window-stratified value-add

**Question**: Does the time-window-stratified structure (identical baseline terms, vendor-self-selected timing, diminishing time-locked value-add) constitute a defensible competitive practice under FTC and EU competition law?

**Context**:
- All vendor counterparties receive identical baseline terms (cash, equity, restrictions, member-portability covenant)
- The variable across the framework is **time-locked value-add**, not price or terms
- Vendors self-select the signing window — the framework does not pre-select which vendors qualify for which tier
- A perpetually-available Cooperative tier ensures no vendor is ever excluded from the substrate
- The structural analogy is patent-filing windows (Paris Convention 12-month international filing window): same right available to all, time-locked self-selection, no discrimination by counterparty identity

**Bishop's preliminary view (counsel-confirms-or-corrects)**:

The framework appears defensible because:
- Identical-terms-with-time-windows is a standard market-pricing mechanism, not discriminatory pricing
- Vendor self-selection eliminates the "LB picks winners" antitrust concern
- The perpetual Cooperative tier eliminates the exclusion concern
- Cooperative-defensive nature (rather than offensive market power) further mitigates

But counsel should evaluate:
- Whether the time-window-stratified value-add (Preview Window length differences) constitutes "tying" if Preview Window content includes future cooperative innovations
- Whether the Cooperative's accumulating equity stakes across multiple vendors creates a Section 7 (HSR) concern
- Whether the Pledge #2260 cooperative-defensive litigation joinder creates "concerted action" risk (multiple Pledge signers joining a single litigation)
- Whether the framework requires Hart-Scott-Rodino notification at execution (depends on cash consideration thresholds, $111.4M as of 2025)

**Requested counsel position**: written legal opinion that the framework is defensible under FTC + EU competition law as drafted, with any specific recommended modifications to ensure defensibility.

## Issue 2 — Securities implications of equity-accumulation

**Question**: Does the Cooperative's accumulation of equity stakes across multiple commercial AI vendors create securities-law obligations or registration requirements that affect the framework's structure?

**Context**:
- Tier 1 Founder Vendor agreement contemplates [Y]% (scaffold value 5%) of vendor's outstanding common equity flowing to LB Cooperative
- Across multiple vendors signing at multiple tiers, Cooperative could accumulate hundreds of billions of dollars in paper equity stakes (e.g., 5% of Anthropic at $60B = $3B paper; 5% of Perplexity at $9B = $450M paper; 5% of OpenAI at $300B = $15B paper)
- LB Cooperative governance under 60/20/10/10 IP allocation (60% patent buckets / 20% Founder-creator / 10% global sponsor pool / 10% individual Pedestals) distributes economic interest pro-rata to members via Pedestal Stake mechanics
- Members are members of LB Cooperative; the Cooperative is a Wyoming C-Corp (Liana Banyan CORPORATION, filed 2025-11-21)

**Bishop's preliminary view (counsel-confirms-or-corrects)**:

The structure raises questions about:
- Whether vendor-equity issuances to the Cooperative are private placements requiring Section 4(a)(2) / Reg D exemption qualification
- Whether the Cooperative's holdings cross 5% / 10% beneficial-ownership thresholds triggering Section 13(d) / 13(g) reporting
- Whether the Cooperative's pro-rata distribution of economic interest to members constitutes the offer/sale of unregistered securities (the Howey + Reves analysis already filed for the SAA — see project_b108_letters_queue and similar canonical work — applies here also)
- Whether observer-seat rights affect "control" / Section 13(d) analysis
- Whether the Cooperative requires Investment Adviser registration if managing member economic interest in vendor equities

**Requested counsel position**:
- Securities-law structural design that allows the framework to function without triggering registration requirements for the Cooperative-as-issuer of pro-rata economic interest to members
- Confirmation that Reg D + private-placement structure works for vendor-equity inflow
- Recommendation on optimal entity structure (does the Cooperative entity hold equities directly, or does a special-purpose vehicle hold them, or does the Pedestal Stake structure already filed handle this)
- Practical schedule for 13(d)/(g) reporting if/when thresholds cross

## Issue 3 — Member-substrate consent and sovereignty

**Question**: Does the framework require explicit member-side disclosure or consent regarding the commercial vendor adoption framework, given that Member Substrate remains member-owned and Cooperative is licensing the *architectural pattern* (not member content) to vendors?

**Context**:
- Members joined LB Cooperative under terms that include cooperative-economic principles (#42 *you keep what you make*; #40 *always offer what you would want*)
- The Tiered Vendor Adoption Framework licenses the *architectural pattern* (substrate-grounded retrieval producing the Cathedral Effect lift) — NOT member content — to vendors
- Member Substrate remains member-owned, member-portable, and member-sovereign per keystone #42 and the Member-Portability Covenant in the Tier 1 Term Sheet (Section 4.1)
- Vendor revenue flowing back to members via 60/20/10/10 allocation is structurally aligned with cooperative principles
- However: vendors-as-equity-holders-of-Cooperative may create a perceived conflict of interest with member-substrate sovereignty, even if no actual conflict exists

**Bishop's preliminary view (counsel-confirms-or-corrects)**:

The framework appears defensible because:
- No member content is licensed to vendors
- Members retain unconditional substrate portability
- Vendor-equity inflow benefits members pro-rata
- Member-Portability Covenant survives all other terminations

But counsel should evaluate:
- Whether the Cooperative's existing membership agreement adequately discloses commercial vendor adoption as a permitted activity, or whether amendment + member ratification is required
- Whether vendor-board-observer seat creates fiduciary-conflict concerns
- Whether the framework's value-add tier (Preview Window content) could include material derived from member substrates in a way that requires individual-member consent
- What member-disclosure language counsel recommends in framework public announcement and Cooperative-facing communications

**Requested counsel position**:
- Whether existing membership agreement supports the framework as drafted, or what amendments are required
- Recommended member-disclosure language for framework public announcement
- Recommended structural protection for member-substrate sovereignty under the Preview Window mechanism
