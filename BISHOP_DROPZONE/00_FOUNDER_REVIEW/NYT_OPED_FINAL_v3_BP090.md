# The Art of Losing

*An Op-Ed by Jonathan R. Jones*

---

> *"The art of losing isn't hard to master; so many things seem filled with the intent to be lost that their loss is no disaster."*
> — Elizabeth Bishop, "One Art" (1976)

Elizabeth Bishop mastered losing as resignation. Keys. A river. Two cities. A continent. Her villanelle builds with wry restraint until the final stanza breaks into confession. She writes "disaster" anyway, in parentheses, because the art of losing always costs more than it ought.

I do not read it as resignation.

My sister texted in the evening of June 18th, 2026; navigating a month left to find work, with time and resources both running short. I told her what I tell my children: never despair at the immediacy of your circumstance. Take a beat. No matter what, no matter when, stop. Just for a moment. And know that you've got this. Not because circumstances are kind, but simply because you decided.

You might fail. A lot, and lose a lot. But it's not over until you win: because either you win or you keep trying. That is why you will win. Because you chose to. Again.

I had just given myself the same advice earlier that same day.

On the morning of June 18th, 2026, the power was cut to my house shortly before 9 a.m. — I left at 8:45 to fix it. I had been awake for a little over twenty-four hours, deep in a technical sprint building cooperative infrastructure, when the lights cut to blackness. The bill had been paid by check a week earlier — and then reversed because the account was closed. A simple administrative consequence: the utilities were in my friend and employer's name. The cut was how I learned he had died.

His name was Alford Ray Hunter. He was my namesake (my middle name is Ray, named for him), a lifelong Minister and Chaplain, Director of the Rally Group, the best Christian example in real life I have ever personally known. His death meant losing the home where I, my wife, and four of our children still live, and the job that had sustained it. His funeral had been held more than a week before I discovered he was gone — from the utility company.

I took a beat. Prayed. Talked with my wife. Fixed the utilities, and kept working: because claiming the problem meant I could claim the solution.

My life is my fault. And that is my power. Not what happened to me, but what I have done in response. What I choose, of what I am able to choose. For circumstances to be my fault, I have to be responsible, which means I have to be capable, which means I can apply that capability to anything I choose. My safety net is me. And so is yours. But we can do better.

Babe Ruth retired holding two records simultaneously: the most home runs in MLB history (714, until Hank Aaron, 1974) and the most strikeouts in MLB history (1,330, held for decades after). Those records aren't contradictory: the home runs were enabled by the strikeouts. The same swing that produced the most strikeouts produced the most home runs.

I have played 29,100 rated games of chess on a single account — with a peak rating of 2,118 on January 14, 2026; placing me in the top 0.8% of chess players worldwide. My win rate is 45.8%. I lose more than I win. In the past year alone I played 7,825 games. I am in the top one percent of chess players on earth, and I still lose more games than I win. How? Because every loss teaches me something. The losses get harder, which means the education gets deeper.

Chess is also my daily instrument for measuring cognition. If I lose to a simple tactic I saw and dismissed from fatigue, that's my signal to stretch, breathe, have a coffee. Inherently unstable systems are flown by continuous micro-adjustment. So is a mind under load. So is a cooperative under construction.

Losing, at this scale, is not misfortune. It is curriculum.

What I built in those forty hours — another sixteen after the power cut — and in the years of losing that preceded them, is called MnemosyneC. It is the substrate memory layer of a cooperative platform called Liana Banyan: sixteen charitable initiatives, each borne of a lived and personally witnessed unfulfilled need, a fixed Cost+20% margin locked by bylaws and operating agreement, and a membership that costs $5 per year. Not five hundred dollars. Five. Because the business model of the cooperative is not extraction: it is participation. Workers, Builders, and Creators keep 83.3%. The members are not the product.

On May 30th, 2026, we ran a memory recall test across four AI flagship vendor models, seventy-five questions, single-blind graded. Without the substrate, Claude Opus 4.8 recalled 6.0% of stored information. With MnemosyneC, it recalled 89.3%. A free local model — Llama 3.1, zero dollars of inference cost — went from 6.0% to 78.0% on the shared substrate alone. The cooperative doesn't make the AI smarter. It gives the AI memory it doesn't have to pay for: the Substrate; and shares it with everyone.

The architecture of extraction is not inevitable. It is a design that can be redesigned.

For over forty years on multiple continents I have watched the game stay rigged. The correct response is not resignation; it is a free carnival. Start near zero, hoist Excalibur — especially when it's too heavy and you can barely lift it — and swing for the fences. Not just for yourself, but for all of us. Together; separately, in our own homes.

Elizabeth Bishop knew loss. She wrote its grammar with precision and restraint. I do not argue with her craft. I argue with her conclusion.

She called it an art. I call it training.
