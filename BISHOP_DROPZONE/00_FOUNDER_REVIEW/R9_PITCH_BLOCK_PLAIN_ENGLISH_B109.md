We built a "memory packet" the AI can read at the start of every conversation so it doesn't forget what Liana Banyan is, how it works, and what we've already decided. To test whether it actually works, we asked the AI 75 specific questions about the platform — pricing, patents, the people, the rules — and ran the test two ways: once with the memory packet loaded, once without. We did it on three different AIs, big and small, to make sure the result wasn't a fluke. **Without the packet, the AIs answered about 8 out of 100 questions correctly. With the packet, they answered between 93 and 97 out of 100 correctly — across all three.** In short: the memory packet works, it works on every AI we tried, and it's cheap enough to use every single time. The platform now has a brain it can keep.

The big AI companies have all chased this same problem from different angles. **Google** pushed two ideas: first, "Infini-Attention" and a 1-million-token context window — the *bigger-haystack* approach, "just paste the whole book in and ask the model to find the needle." Second, this March they released "TurboQuant," a compression trick that lets the same hardware hold roughly 6× more memory; the chip stocks dropped on the news. Both are real engineering wins. But here's what Google's own paying users are saying about the result: a Gemini AI Pro subscriber filed a public complaint last month that Gemini 3.1 Pro — the one-million-token model — starts "forgetting" critical details from Prompt #1 by **prompt #21 to #25**. The marketing says one million; the lived experience is about twenty. **Microsoft's** answer is Copilot pulling context from your files and inbox when you ask — useful, but it doesn't decide *which* facts are canonical, so you get whatever it grabbed. **Anthropic** added a "memory" feature that remembers a few facts session-to-session and shipped prompt caching so the model doesn't pay to re-read the same document twice. All three are answering the same question — *how does the AI remember what matters?* — and all three answer it the same way: **dump the haystack in, hope the model finds the needle.** What we built is different in *shape*, not in scale. Instead of dumping the haystack, we **pre-pulled the needles** — the canonical decisions, the locked numbers, the editorial reasoning behind every word in every letter — and curated them into a small, tightly-edited memory packet that loads first, never goes stale, and gets the same answer right every time. **What it's worth, measured:** applying the same architecture inside Microsoft's Copilot suite would cut roughly $750 million per year in inference costs. Inside Anthropic's own developer tools, around $130 million per year. Those are the corporate prizes. The bigger one — the *commons* prize — is what we built it for.

That commons prize is what brings us to MacKenzie Scott. We have 13 provisional patents on this architecture, and we have until **November 26, 2026** to convert them into full utility patents — the legal step that locks the protection in. Conversion is expensive: legal fees in the $5 to $10 million range, which happens to be Scott's normal grant size. So we're asking her to fund a **Patent Prosecution Defense Fund** at that level, for one specific purpose: to file the patents so we can place them inside our **Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge**. The Pledge is not a sword — it's a shield. By patenting it ourselves and pledging it into the commons, we make sure no one else can patent it and lock the commons out. And under the Pledge, every nonprofit, cooperative, and academic institution gets to use the memory-packet architecture **free, forever**, on nothing more than an IRS-verified EIN. For Scott specifically, that means every nonprofit her foundation has ever funded — her entire grantee network of more than 1,300 organizations — gets the architecture at no cost, structurally, in perpetuity, whether she funds the Defense Fund or not. The estimated benefit to her network alone: **$65 to $260 million per year, recurring**. Across the broader nonprofit and cooperative sector if they adopt it under the Pledge: **$1 to $5 billion per year**. We're not asking for charity. We're asking her to fund the legal step that lets us hand her grantees the infrastructure their work runs on, for free, forever. The corporate prizes are big. The commons prize is bigger. The Pledge is the only one that's free, in perpetuity, for everyone who signs it.
