## I. The Bicycle in 1895

Before the safety bicycle, American cities were topographically segregated by capital.

Not by law — or not entirely by law. By horse. A horse cost the equivalent of two to three months of a skilled laborer's wages to purchase, and an additional monthly wage to maintain. The result was a functional geographic cage: the working classes lived, shopped, socialized, and worshipped within walking distance of their place of employment. A distance of five miles might as well have been fifty for a worker with no horse and a twelve-hour shift the next morning.

The safety bicycle — chain-driven, equal-wheel, affordable at roughly two weeks to two months of wages for the entry model — punctured that cage without blowing it apart. By 1900, an estimated 10 million bicycles were in use in the United States. The price had collapsed from over $100 in 1890 to under $20 by 1895 as manufacturing scaled. What had been a rich man's curiosity became a working-class tool.[^1]

The political consequences were documented in real time. Susan B. Anthony called the bicycle "the freedom machine" in 1896. Frances Willard, president of the Women's Christian Temperance Union, wrote an entire book — *A Wheel Within a Wheel* — about learning to ride at age 53, and argued explicitly that the bicycle democratized not just movement but autonomy. Working-class men took factory jobs at distances they had previously been unable to commute. Young people traveled outside their birth neighborhoods for the first time in their lives.[^2]

The economic mechanism was precise: the bicycle converted modest human effort into mobility that previously required capital ownership. The mechanical advantage — gear ratio, chain drive, pneumatic tire — was a force multiplier at the physical layer. Labor × bicycle ≈ capital × horse, in terms of effective mobility. Not equality of speed in every race. Equivalence of outcome in the tasks that daily life required.

That is the shape of the thing. The bicycle is not metaphor decoration in what follows. It is the proof of concept for an economic inversion that is now repeating, in a different material substrate, at the cognitive layer.

## II. The Substrate in 2026

In 2026, the cognitive equivalent of the horse is a flagship AI API.

Anthropic's Claude, OpenAI's GPT-4 class models, Google's Gemini — these are the capital-class tools of cognitive labor. Their pricing varies by model and context window, but a serious practitioner running a flagship AI on complex multi-step reasoning tasks — the kind of tasks that previously required a research team or an expensive consultant — will spend between $50 and $5,000 per month depending on volume. A company running thousands of such queries per day will spend more. The marginal cost of each additional query is constant: pay per token, forever, to a corporation that captures the margin.

The cooperative substrate is the bicycle.

The Liana Banyan Corporation operates a peer-to-peer mesh of commodity machines — laptops, desktops, the hardware cooperative members already own. Membership costs $5/year, structured as a cooperative bylaw, not a pricing scheme. The cooperative does not run advertisements. It does not accept venture capital. It does not sell member data. The five-dollar membership is the full cost of access to the substrate.

The substrate does not merely pool machines. It organizes them. When a reasoning task arrives, the substrate's orchestrator — a distributed software layer called MnemosyneC — reads each peer's hardware specifications, selects the right AI model for that machine's capacity, and routes work proportional to what each machine can carry. A 64-gigabyte workstation runs a full 12-billion-parameter model and handles long, domain-complex reasoning. A 16-gigabyte laptop runs a 9-billion-parameter model and handles shorter questions and verification. A machine that cannot load a large local model still participates — it reads the mesh output, votes on answers, and contributes attention.

The substrate also cures what every AI — free or flagship — cannot cure alone: amnesia. Every reasoning run is receipted, indexed, and preserved as priming context for subsequent runs. The substrate is the cure to AI amnesia: the persistent cooperative mesh of what this community has already thought, tested, and proven. A flagship AI without the substrate begins every conversation from zero. The cooperative substrate carries learned context forward, compounding not just processing speed but accumulated cognitive depth.

This is not an open-source vanity project. It is a cooperative enterprise, built on software that is at its core patent-provisionally protected, with documented receipts and empirical benchmark results. The cooperative-class is not charity toward its members; it is a structurally different economic arrangement in which value is created by members and captured by members.

## III. The Lever — Physics Framing

The Founder of the Liana Banyan Corporation — who has described himself, accurately, as an amateur engineer and economist — worked out the substrate's physics in a single formula:

```
1000 × FreeAI(on-substrate)  ≈  100 × Flagship(on-substrate)  ≈  10,000 × Flagship(off-substrate)
```

The formula is Founder-direct, ratified at BP091, dated June 22, 2026. Its numbers are a hypothesis, not yet receipted — the empirical test is in progress. But the shape of the claim is empirically falsifiable, which is why it qualifies as physics rather than rhetoric.

The derivation is straightforward. Let B denote a commodity machine (bicycle-class) operating on the cooperative substrate. Let R denote a flagship AI machine (rocket-fuel-class) operating on the substrate. Let R₀ denote the same flagship machine operating alone, off any cooperative substrate.

The formula claims: 1000B ≈ 100R ≈ 10,000R₀.

From the second equivalence: a single flagship machine on the cooperative substrate produces approximately 100× the effective output of the same flagship machine operating in isolation. The substrate multiplier on flagship usage is approximately 100×.

From the first equivalence: a commodity machine on the substrate produces approximately one-tenth the per-machine output of a flagship machine on the substrate. But you can field ten commodity machines for the same capital outlay as one flagship API subscription — so their combined output is equivalent.

The physics of this is the physics of mechanical advantage. The substrate is a lever. The fulcrum is member participation. A lone person lifting a 100-pound stone needs 100 pounds of force. Ten people with levers and pulleys — the substrate — each contribute five pounds of effort, and the stone moves at ten times the speed. Same total work; distributed effort; amplified outcome. The mechanical advantage IS the substrate.

What distinguishes the cognitive case from the physical case is that cognitive work can be parallelized in ways that stone-lifting cannot. This is where the third substrate primitive enters.

## IV. The Inversion — Economics Framing

Standard AI economics have a specific shape: capital owners (the companies that built and operate the GPU farms, the training runs, the data centers) extract rent from labor (developers and practitioners who pay per token).

The cooperative substrate inverts this at five dimensions.

**Marginal cost asymptotic to zero.** A flagship API charges the same per-token rate for query number one and query number one million. The marginal cost is flat. The cooperative substrate's marginal cost structure is categorically different: one-time hardware (amortized over years), five-dollar annual membership (fixed), and electricity (approximately $0.0001 per query on commodity hardware). As member volume increases, per-member marginal cost decreases. The asymptote is near zero, not flat.

**Capital-to-labor inversion.** In the extractive-class AI model, capital — GPU farms, training infrastructure, API licensing — is the source of leverage. Labor (practitioners) pays rent for access to that capital. In the cooperative-class model, labor (participation: member machines, member attention, member governance) IS the substrate. Capital (hardware) is commoditized. The people who create the value capture the value.

**Network effects without extraction.** Standard SaaS network effects have a hidden trap: more users means more infrastructure cost means more need for capital means more extraction from users to pay for the infrastructure. The cooperative substrate avoids this trap because each member brings their own infrastructure. Every new peer adds cognitive capacity for all existing peers. Network effects compound without the extraction pressure.

**Scarcity inversion.** The conventional AI market frames compute as scarce capital — GPUs are expensive, data center power is expensive, model training is expensive. Therefore AI access is rationed by price. The cooperative substrate reframes: compute is abundant cooperative labor — your existing hardware is compute. AI access is rationed not by price but by participation. The substrate eliminates the artificial scarcity gate.

**The bicycle precedent, precisely.** Bicycles did not eliminate the horse. Horses remained faster in certain applications, more powerful in heavy hauling, better for certain displays of capital-class status. What bicycles eliminated was the *requirement* of horse ownership for effective mobility. The cooperative substrate does not eliminate flagship AI. Flagships remain faster per-machine, better for certain compute-heavy tasks. What the substrate eliminates is the requirement of flagship spending for effective cognitive labor.

## V. The Three Primitives

The substrate's multiplication effect is not magic. It operates through three distinct mechanisms — which the cooperative calls substrate primitives — each addressing a different axis of the computation problem.

**The Plow Loop (Depth).** A single question is submitted to the same model through twelve iterative passes, each pass refining the answer based on the prior pass's confidence interval. The Plow Loop is the substrate's depth primitive: it converts a single-shot AI response (which carries known hallucination and uncertainty risks) into a convergent answer that has been tested against itself. The empirical claim is that Plow Loop accuracy on hard reasoning questions significantly exceeds single-shot accuracy on the same questions.

**The Star Chamber (Consensus).** The same question is submitted simultaneously to multiple peers with different local models and different priming contexts. Each peer produces an independent answer. The answers are compared; agreement signals confidence; divergence signals hallucination risk. The Star Chamber is the substrate's consensus primitive. It is the cooperative equivalent of peer review — but automated, parallel, and continuous.

**The Posse / Army Ants (Breadth).** A complex, multi-faceted problem is decomposed into sub-problems, each sub-problem dispatched to a different peer sized to its difficulty, attacked in parallel, with results synthesized as they arrive — and the remainder recursively decomposed until the problem yields. This is the substrate's breadth primitive, named by the Founder in a single direct image: like army ants attacking a water buffalo, the buffalo will lose and be gone in literally minutes. A problem that would take a single flagship AI hours of sequential reasoning is resolved in the wall-clock time of the slowest sub-problem — typically minutes, not hours.

The three primitives compose. A research question can be Posse-decomposed at the top level (five sub-problems dispatched to five peers), Plow-Looped within each sub-problem (twelve iterations to converge each facet), and Star-Chambered on the synthesis (three peers vote on the integrated answer). No single machine — however powerful — can run all three simultaneously. The cooperative substrate can, because it IS the parallel execution environment.
