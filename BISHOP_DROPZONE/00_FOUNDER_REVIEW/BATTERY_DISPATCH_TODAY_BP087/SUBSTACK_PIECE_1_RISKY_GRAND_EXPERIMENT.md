## The Risky Grand Experiment

I live my life by taking a calculated gamble, and if you haven't yet, you should too — taking the risky grand experiment of choosing what to put your life effort into. For some that experiment is family, or career, or faith, or a venture, or something creative. Mine has been all of those things, and at this point in my life, I've put most of my eggs into the basket of what I believe can change the world.

But more importantly — and please hear me because this is the reason I'm writing — doing something risky, or something that most people won't, is a great way to find out who you are. It's the gauntlet, and if you can come out on the other side having tried something daring with everything you have, you'll have learned about yourself what no course could teach. And even if you don't get the expected outcome, you'll have more than you started with. Because you'll know what you're made of.

I want to be clear. I have no safety net. I have no one to fall back on if this doesn't work. The safety net is me, and I have to keep catching myself.

My safety net is ME and since I also have what I've been feverishly working on for a long time that is ready to be unleashed, in an unpolished and imperfect form but WORKS REALLY WELL, I choose to not build escape tunnels and focus all efforts on making more arrows and defending the KEEP. I've been in stance for months, I'm taking the stride, and SWINGING FOR THE FENCES to change the game — not just for me or my family, but for All of Us. Together; separately, in our own homes.

I believe in what I'm doing so deeply that I'm willing to risk my family's stability on it. That's not bravado. That's conviction backed by years of evidence. I've built the thing. I've tested the thing. The thing works.

And you might fail. A lot, and lose a lot, but it's not over until you win — because Either You WIN or you KEEP TRYING. And that is why you will win. Because you chose to.

## The Babe Ruth Receipt

Babe Ruth retired from baseball holding two records simultaneously: the most home runs in MLB history (714, held until Hank Aaron in 1974) and the most strikeouts in MLB history (1,330, held for decades). Those are not contradictory facts. They are the same fact. He swung for the fences every at-bat. The same swing class that produced the most strikeouts produced the most home runs.

The metaphor here is a baseball bat — not an axe, not a blade. A baseball bat. You're not cutting something down. You're making contact with something flying toward you at high speed, and you're sending it somewhere it has never been before.

I have played over 25,000 games of chess on a single account — hundreds of thousands more since 1997. My highest rating reached 2118, top 0.4% worldwide. My win rate is 46%. I lose more games than I win. This is not a confession of weakness. It is an operational receipt: losses are curriculum when you refuse to stop taking them.

One of my favorite animated films is *Megamind*, because of two lines I have carried for years:

> **Megamind:** *"There's a benefit to losing: You get to learn from your mistakes."*
>
> **Roxanne Ritchi, about him:** *"The Megamind I knew would never have run from a fight, even when he knew he had absolutely no chance of winning. It was your best quality."*

And Calvin Coolidge, who pairs with Megamind in my substrate in a way I find permanently useful:

> *"Nothing in the world can take the place of persistence. Talent will not; nothing is more common than unsuccessful men with talent. Genius will not; unrewarded genius is almost a proverb. Education will not; the world is full of educated derelicts. Persistence and determination alone are omnipotent."*

My safety net is me. I don't know where we will be in a month. All I know is that I'm an Engine. And I will not stop.

## What I've Built

What I'm swinging for, specifically, is this:

A cooperative commerce and technology platform called Liana Banyan — sixteen charitable initiatives, a fixed 20% margin locked by our operating agreement, a membership that costs five dollars a year. The cooperative's margin is its ceiling. The members are not the product. No ads. No sponsored placement. No thirty-percent extraction.

Inside Liana Banyan is a substrate — a distributed peer mesh that makes coordination between people and AI systems both durable and honest. Every member who joins carries a portion of that substrate on their own hardware, replicated across the network. Your contributions are hash-signed by you. Your cooperative-class identity is portable.

And inside that substrate is MnemosyneC — our AI orchestration layer — which on the morning of June 19th, 2026, ran its first full cooperative mesh test across five peers on five machines. Seventy questions. All five peers answered. Results confirmed.

The thing works.

We are inviting anyone who wants to help, to join. Five dollars a year. Help Each Other Help Ourselves.

## The Cooperative Catechism

If you are reading this and you are in your own grand experiment — teaching license expiring, job gone, house uncertain, the ground moving — I want to give you what I gave my sister, and what I am applying to myself:

Never despair at the immediacy of your circumstance.

The cooperative is not charity. It is reciprocity proven by construction. The equation is readable left-to-right and right-to-left identically:

**ONE OF US = ALL OF US = ONE OF US.**

When one of us is in the worst forty hours of the last decade, that IS all of us. And all of us, collectively, is why one of us can keep going.

That is the risky grand experiment. Not the technology. Not the cooperative mechanics. The experiment is: can enough of us choose to keep trying, together, that the cooperative-class answer becomes the default? Can we build the free carnival? Can we employ the world?

I believe we can.

I'm swinging.
