# The Art of Losing
### *An Op-Ed by Jonathan R. Jones*

*Founder & General Manager, Liana Banyan Corporation*
*Capitalist · Cooperative · Patriotic Interdependentalist*

---

> *"The art of losing isn't hard to master;*
> *so many things seem filled with the intent*
> *to be lost that their loss is no disaster."*
>
> - Elizabeth Bishop, "One Art" (1976)

Elizabeth Bishop mastered losing as resignation. Keys. A river. Two cities. A continent. The loved one. Her villanelle builds with wry restraint until the final stanza breaks into a confession: the mastery is incomplete. She writes "disaster" anyway, in parentheses, because the art of losing always costs more than it should.

I have read Bishop's villanelle many times. I do not read it as resignation.

The art of losing has prepared me for this moment in history. The history of my family, and the nation, and the world.

---

On the morning of June 18th, 2026, the power was cut to my house.

I had been awake for approximately twenty-four hours already. I was deep in a technical sprint - building cooperative infrastructure at a scale I had been working toward for years - when the lights went out. A simple administrative consequence: the utilities were in a friend's name. The cut was how I learned he had died.

His name was Alford Ray Hunter. He was my namesake - I am Jonathan *Ray* Jones, and the Ray comes from him, from the friendship between his family and mine that stretches back to my father's college years. He was the Director of the Rally Group, a lifelong minister, and the best Christian example in real life I ever personally knew. He had been on a mission trip. His funeral had been held more than a week before I discovered he was gone.

I sat with that. I took a beat. I prayed. I talked with my wife.

Then I fixed the utilities, and I kept working.

*My daily act of defiance is to pray for potatoes at the end of a hoe handle. Liana Banyan is my garden.* I wrote that sentence years ago as a doctrine statement - the faith that works with its hands, that prays AND lifts. What I did not know then is how precisely it would describe the hours after that power cut. Pray. Hoe handle. Fix the utilities. Write the code. Ship the build. Write the paper. Go to family lunch.

Approximately forty hours after I had first sat down to work, I went to bed. Earliest bedtime in a week.

---

The night before, I had texted my sister Abigail, who is navigating her own displacement - teaching license expiring, paid only through early August, deciding whether to stay or go after a professional difficulty that would stop most people cold.

I told her what I tell my children: *Never despair at the immediacy of your circumstance.*

I gave her the same advice I was applying to myself in real time. And when I wrote the piece that became the Substack piece that became this op-ed, I gave it to everyone I could reach.

The advice is not optimism. It is structural. It removes quitting from the option set.

*And you might fail. A lot, and lose a lot, but it's not over until you win - because Either You WIN or you KEEP TRYING. And that is why you will win. Because you chose to.*

---

Bishop's villanelle and I diverge here. She built her poem as a record of accumulation - what she has lost, in escalating weight, until the loss she cannot name arrives and breaks the meter. The restrained craft is the point. The wryness is the armor.

I am not wry about losing. I am *operational* about it.

Babe Ruth retired from baseball holding two records simultaneously: the most home runs in MLB history (714, held until Hank Aaron in 1974) and the most strikeouts in MLB history (1,330, held for decades after). Those records were not in tension. The home runs were *enabled* by the strikeouts. He swung for the fences every at-bat. The same swing class that produced the most strikeouts produced the most home runs. The statistical receipt is the proof.

I have played over 25,000 games of chess on a single account - hundreds of thousands more across other accounts since 1997. My highest rating reached 2118, placing me in the top 0.4% of chess players worldwide. I lose more games than I win. My win rate is 46%. In the past year alone I played 7,825 games: 3,910 as White (45.9% wins) and 3,915 as Black (43.7% wins) - color makes almost no difference, which is to say the losing is evenly distributed and evenly educational. I am in the top half-percent of chess players on the planet, and I lose more than I win. How? Because every loss taught me something. Because the losses get harder, which means the education gets deeper.

Chess is not just my history with losing. It is my daily instrument for measuring it. I play to stay alert and to gauge how well I am processing. If I lose to a simple tactic - or worse, one that I saw and dismissed from fatigue - it is my beacon signal to stretch and have a coffee. It is my methodology for monitoring and adjusting my behavior - like flying a helicopter. Inherently unstable systems are flown by continuous micro-adjustment. So is a mind under load. So is a cooperative under construction. So is a life.

Losing, at this scale, is not misfortune. It is curriculum.

*There's a benefit to losing: you get to learn from your mistakes.* That is Megamind, the animated supervillain whose best quality - identified by the journalist who covered his crimes - was that he never ran from a fight, even when he knew he had absolutely no chance of winning.

I have carried those two lines for years because they contain a complete epistemology: failure teaches, and persistence in the face of hopeless odds is not foolishness - it is the quality that separates the people who eventually win from the people who never throw.

*Nothing in the world can take the place of persistence. Talent will not; nothing is more common than unsuccessful men with talent. Genius will not; unrewarded genius is almost a proverb. Education will not; the world is full of educated derelicts. Persistence and determination alone are omnipotent.* - Calvin Coolidge

My safety net is me. I don't know where we will be in a month. All I know is that I'm an Engine. And I will not stop.

---

What I built in those forty hours - and in the years of losing that preceded them - is called MnemosyneC. It is the cooperative orchestration layer for a platform called Liana Banyan: a cooperative commerce and technology platform with sixteen charitable initiatives, a fixed 20% margin locked by operating agreement, and a membership that costs five dollars a year.

Not five hundred. Not a subscription that scales with your usage. Five dollars. For a full year. Because the business model of the cooperative is not extraction - it is participation. The cooperative's margin is its ceiling, not its floor. The members are not the product.

I introduce MnemosyneC and Liana Banyan here not as a pitch, but as the empirical artifact of the thesis. The art of losing has prepared me for this moment in history. MnemosyneC is what forty hours of uninterrupted work, across forty years of preparation, produces when the founder refuses to stop.

The platform is live. The cooperative mesh - four machines, one shared substrate - passed its first full test on the morning of June 19th, 2026, while I wrote the piece that became this one. Seventy questions. All answered on the anchor pass. A follow-on cooperative trial returned 81.9% accuracy across three active peers with zero disagreement on any question - 100% consensus, confirmed.

This is what preparation looks like. Not absence of loss. Preparation through loss. Bishop's "One Art" is a poem about resignation to an accumulating weight. The art of losing, practiced correctly, is not resignation. It is infrastructure.

---

The history of my family: a namesake friend who trusted me with his bills and his calls and his "dear friend" salutations, whose funeral I missed because I was building this, whose name I carry in the middle of mine, and to whom I dedicate every receipt that follows.

The history of the nation: forty years of watching the carnival game stay rigged - middle-class kids get one throw, rich kids get unlimited throws, poor kids work the carnival - and deciding that the correct response is not a shrug but a free carnival. The cooperative is that carnival.

The history of the world: the architecture of extraction is not inevitable. It is a design. Designs can be redesigned. The Substrate - the cooperative mesh, the distributed ledger, the member-owned platform - is the counter-design. Built in public. Priced at cost plus twenty percent. Owned by no one. Accountable to everyone.

Elizabeth Bishop knew loss. She wrote its grammar with precision and restraint. I do not argue with her craft. I argue with her conclusion. The art of losing is not resignation. It is preparation.

And I am prepared.
