[HOOK — Founder lede. Suggested anchor: the Ashburn imagery is yours. The aerial photo of the AWS data center in Loudoun County. The hum the residents hear at 3 a.m. The water table draw. I'm writing because last Friday, Maine's Governor Mills vetoed what would have been the country's first state-level moratorium on AI data centers. The bill had bipartisan support in the legislature. Six other states are considering similar legislation. President Trump has threatened to sue any state that "regulates AI growth." The federalism fight is being framed as *AI growth versus state sovereignty.*

It is not. It is *one specific architecture's growth* versus state sovereignty. The distinction matters more than the current debate has noticed.

The data centers being built in Ashburn, the ones proposed in Maine and Minnesota and Michigan, all run the same architectural pattern: a frontier-tier large language model hosted in a mega-data-center, drawing megawatts of power and millions of gallons of cooling water, answering each query at roughly the cost of a frontier-model API call. That pattern was selected by a small number of corporate decisions over the last five years. It is not a property of AI as a category. It is one *choice* about how to deliver AI capability.

A different choice produces a different fight. We have spent the last fourteen months at Liana Banyan empirically measuring an alternative architecture — a small, cheap language model paired with a structured memory substrate. We call it the **Cathedral Effect**. Across cross-vendor benchmarks (including sealed-against-contamination tests), this architecture lifts a low-tier model to within statistical noise of frontier-tier accuracy at roughly **nineteen times** less compute cost per correct answer. A data center running this architecture draws five to ten times less power per useful answer than the Ashburn-class facilities. There is no obvious technical reason the next wave of data center buildouts has to choose the Ashburn architecture over the substrate-amplified one. The reason is not technical. It is corporate path-dependence and a regulatory-capture story that I think you will recognize.

The reason I'm writing to you specifically is that the federalism fight is being argued on the wrong terrain. The current shape — Trump threatens to sue states that block data centers, states feel cornered, the moratorium fight gets reframed as "anti-AI" — is a fight engineered by the architecture's beneficiaries to make any state-level pushback look like Luddism. It isn't. State legislators in Maine, New York, Minnesota, and Michigan are responding to *the documented externalities of one specific architecture* — power draw, water consumption, home energy bill increases, transmission load. They are not anti-AI. They are pro-grid.

Architectural standards reframe this fight. If the next wave of data center proposals adopts the substrate-amplified pattern, there is nothing left to ban. The grid impact, the water impact, the cost-of-living impact on residents — these are artifacts of the Ashburn architecture, not properties of AI. The moratorium debate evaporates. The federalism fight evaporates. President Trump's threat to sue states becomes a fight about a problem that no longer exists.

[HOOK — Founder voice. Connect to the Oregon AI slop case ($109,700 fine, 1,300+ documented hallucination cases in courts). Same pattern: the slop is an architectural artifact (raw frontier LLM without provenance grounding), not a property of AI. Substrate-grounded architectures cannot generate citations they cannot resolve. The Authoritative-Answer-AI demonstration — *it's OK for AI to say "I don't know"* — is the legal-system version of the same Maine argument.]

I am asking for one specific thing: a Congressional working group on substrate-grounded AI infrastructure standards, before the federalism fight forces a worse outcome. The empirical evidence is reproducible by any third party. The patents are licensed cooperatively. We will publish, demonstrate, and host the architecture at any state-government scale. We are not asking Congress to legislate the architecture. We are asking Congress to *put it on the table* so that the state-level fights have a third option to consider.

The fight in Maine is winnable on terms that do not require choosing between mill town jobs and grid stability — *if* a substrate-amplified architecture is on the table as a third option. The fight against the Trump-administration threat to sue states is winnable on terms that do not require accepting the Ashburn architecture as the only AI architecture. Both fights need an architectural alternative to be visible at the federal level. That is what I am asking you to help make visible.
