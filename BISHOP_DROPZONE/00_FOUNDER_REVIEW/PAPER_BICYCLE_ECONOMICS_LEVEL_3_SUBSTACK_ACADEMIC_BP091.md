## I. Introduction — The Bicycle Precedent

### I.1 The mobility problem before 1890

The history of the bicycle is, in precise terms, the history of a mechanical-advantage inversion at the personal-mobility layer.

Before the chain-driven safety bicycle became affordable at scale in the early 1890s, American urban mobility followed a strict capital gradient. Horse ownership in 1890 required between $80 and $200 for a working animal and an additional $15–25 per month in feed, stabling, shoeing, and veterinary care.[^1] For a skilled urban laborer earning $1.50–$2.00 per day, a horse represented three to five months of gross wages to purchase and a week of wages per month to maintain — a capital burden that placed horse ownership outside the practical reach of most working families.

The alternatives to horse ownership were: walking (average 3 miles per hour, effective range approximately 2 miles for a round-trip commute), horse-car and later electric streetcar (confined to fixed routes, expensive for daily use, not available in most neighborhoods), or horse-drawn omnibus (similarly route-fixed and priced above daily worker budgets). Urban geography was, accordingly, defined by walking distance from employment. The phrase "walking neighborhood" was not metaphor. It was literal geography. A worker whose factory was four miles from a cheaper neighborhood could not, as a practical matter, take the cheaper neighborhood's housing without giving up the job.

### I.2 The bicycle as economic instrument

The safety bicycle — with equal-sized wheels, a chain drive, and pneumatic tires — became commercially practical in 1885 and collapsed in price through the following decade. By 1897, a serviceable bicycle could be purchased for $18–$35.[^2] At prevailing wages, this represented three to four weeks of earnings: a large but achievable capital expenditure, especially as installment purchase became available.

The effects were documented extensively and enthusiastically by contemporaries. Susan B. Anthony, in an 1896 interview with journalist Nellie Bly, called the bicycle "the freedom machine" and argued that it had "done more to emancipate women than anything else in the world."[^3] Frances Willard, in *A Wheel Within a Wheel* (1895), documented learning to ride at age 53 and argued explicitly that the bicycle cultivated autonomy, self-reliance, and geographic freedom that had been previously gatekept by capital-class mobility resources.[^4]

The economic mechanism is precisely expressible: the bicycle applied mechanical advantage — gear ratio (typically 2:1 to 4:1), chain drive efficiency (~95%), and pneumatic tire rolling resistance reduction — to human leg effort, converting modest caloric input into approximately 10–12 miles per hour of sustained travel speed. This is roughly 3.5× to 4× walking speed. A worker living four miles from a factory or four miles from a better-paying job could now commute in approximately 20 minutes each way rather than 80 minutes. The effective labor market radius of a working person on a bicycle was 3–4× larger than the same person on foot.

The formal relationship: let E denote human effort (constant), let M denote mechanical advantage (gear ratio × chain efficiency × tire reduction), and let V denote effective velocity. Then V = E × M. The bicycle increased M without requiring any increase in E. The same effort produced a categorically different outcome.

This is not merely technological history. It is the proof of concept for what follows.

### I.3 Why the bicycle metaphor is load-bearing

The cooperative substrate Founder J. Jones has spent three years building is not "like a bicycle" in the sense that metaphors decorate arguments. The bicycle is the architectural template because the economic relationship is identical:

- **Before bicycle / Before substrate:** mobility / cognition is gated by capital ownership (horse / flagship API)
- **After bicycle / After substrate:** the same human effort (pedaling / participating) produces outcomes equivalent to or exceeding what capital ownership previously gated, because a mechanism applies mechanical advantage to that effort
- **The mechanism IS the inversion:** the bicycle's gear-chain-tire system / the substrate's Plow Loop-Star Chamber-Posse system converts effort into amplified output
- **The economic consequence:** labor × mechanism competes with capital alone, changing who has access to the outcome class

Elizabeth Bishop's "One Art" (1976) masters losing as resignation. This paper inverts that: the art of losing has prepared the Founder for this moment in history. What was lost — access to capital, access to VC infrastructure, access to the patron-class of AI — turns out not to have been necessary. The bicycle was always cheaper than the horse.

## II. The 2026 Cognitive-Mobility Problem

### II.1 The cost of flagship cognition

The contemporary AI market has a specific pricing structure that replicates the horse-ownership problem with precision.

As of mid-2026, the major flagship AI APIs price as follows (approximate, subject to model-version variation):
- Input tokens: $0.003–$0.015 per 1,000 tokens
- Output tokens: $0.015–$0.075 per 1,000 tokens
- A complex multi-step reasoning task (500-token input, 500-token output) costs approximately $0.009–$0.045 per query

For a serious research practitioner running 2,000 such queries per day — the volume required for genuine AI-assisted research or analysis at scale — this produces a daily cost of $18–$90, or $540–$2,700 per month. ### II.2 The scarcity inversion needed

The conventional AI market frames compute as scarce capital. GPU clusters are expensive. Training runs are expensive. Model hosting is expensive. This scarcity is real at the infrastructure level — genuinely expensive to build. But it is artificially extended at the access level through API pricing that retains the scarcity premium long after the marginal cost of serving a query (for a company with infrastructure already built) has fallen near zero.

The cooperative substrate proposes to invert this scarcity. The commodity hardware that cooperative members already own — laptops, desktops, machines bought for other purposes — is, in aggregate, a substantial computing resource. A 32-gigabyte desktop machine purchased for $800 two years ago can run a 12-billion-parameter open-source AI model at approximately 15–20 tokens per second locally. This is slower than a flagship API (which can return 50–100+ tokens per second). But it is free at the marginal query level — the cost of electricity, approximately $0.0001 per query.

The scarcity, in other words, was never really about compute. It was about access to compute concentrated in capital-class data centers. The cooperative substrate disaggregates that concentration: the compute is wherever the members are. The substrate organizes it.

## III. The Cooperative Substrate — Architecture

### III.1 The peer mesh

The cooperative substrate is a peer-to-peer mesh of commodity machines connected via a relay network (relay.lianabanyan.com, live as of BP085) and organized by MnemosyneC software (version 0.5.7 as of BP091, June 22, 2026).

Each peer in the mesh contributes:
1. **Compute:** local AI model inference on the peer's hardware
2. **Attention:** participation in consensus voting and verification tasks
3. **Context:** contribution to the shared receipt and priming substrate
4. **Governance:** voting weight on cooperative decisions

Peer identity is established through a cryptographic stack that includes Ed25519 signatures, peer presence heartbeats, and MIC (Machine In Charge) rotation — the role of issuing coordinated fleet broadcasts rotates among peers per-broadcast, with no permanent tenure. No single peer is the "server."

## IV. The Three Substrate Primitives

The substrate's multiplication effect operates through three distinct algorithmic mechanisms, each addressing a different axis of the cognitive computation problem. These are the substrate primitives.

### IV.1 The Plow Loop (Depth Primitive)

**The problem it solves:** Single-shot AI responses carry known hallucination and uncertainty risks. A single answer to a hard question may be confident and wrong.

**The mechanism:** A single question is submitted through twelve iterative passes to the same model, with each pass examining the prior pass's answer for logical consistency, factual accuracy (against the priming substrate), and confidence. The model corrects itself in subsequent passes. After twelve iterations, the answer has been tested against itself twelve times — a form of internal peer review.

**The empirical claim:** Plow Loop accuracy on hard reasoning questions significantly exceeds single-shot accuracy on the same questions. The delta is the empirically measurable value of the depth primitive. Receipts documenting this delta will be published at mnemosynec.org upon M13c ship. [FOUNDER REVIEW FLAG: local path removed — replace with public URL pre-publication]

**Why this is a substrate primitive:** The Plow Loop works because the substrate maintains prior-pass context across iterations. Off-substrate, a single model can attempt iteration — but it carries no primed-context from prior community reasoning, no receipt-indexed corrections from prior errors on similar questions. The substrate's priming layer makes each iteration smarter than the last, compounding on the accumulated intelligence of the cooperative's prior work.

### IV.2 The Star Chamber (Consensus Primitive)

**The problem it solves:** Any single model — even a well-primed one — can hallucinate with high confidence. Hallucination detection requires cross-perspective comparison.

**The mechanism:** The same question is submitted simultaneously to multiple peers, each running a different local model or a different priming context (or both). Each peer produces an independent answer. The answers are aggregated: high agreement signals confidence; divergence signals hallucination risk and triggers escalation.

**The empirical claim:** The Star Chamber catches hallucinations that single-model responses miss. An empirical receipt from BP089 Plow Loop validation documented a case where one model hallucinated "digital publishing" as a specific technical term while three other peers produced responses that did not include the term — the divergence flagged the hallucination before it entered the receipt.

**Why this is a substrate primitive:** Off-substrate, a practitioner can run the same question through multiple API calls — but at full flagship API cost per call, making multi-model cross-validation prohibitively expensive for ordinary practitioners. On the cooperative substrate, the Star Chamber is nearly free: the compute is cooperative member hardware, not billable API tokens.

**The Minor Council (free-tier Star Chamber):** The cooperative runs a local-model Star Chamber — the Minor Council — using the commodity hardware of mesh peers, specifically for hallucination detection and answer validation. The Minor Council is the free equivalent of a paid multi-model consensus service; it operates on the cooperative substrate at no incremental token cost.

### IV.3 The Posse / Army Ants (Breadth Primitive)

**The problem it solves:** Complex, multi-faceted problems exceed the working memory of any single model's context window. A single machine cannot simultaneously hold all facets of an interdisciplinary problem.

**The mechanism — Posse Decomposition:**

```
Phase 1 — Decomposition:
  A HARD problem P is split into sub-problems {P₁, P₂, ..., Pₙ}
  with a dependency graph (which sub-problems block which)
  and a difficulty class per sub-problem (HARD / MEDIUM / SHORT)

Phase 2 — Tier-aware fan-out (composes with Ah Hayelped policy):
  HARD sub-problems → ULTRA peers
  MEDIUM sub-problems → FULL/CORE peers
  SHORT sub-problems → CORE/LITE peers
  Dependency-ordered: independent sub-problems dispatched immediately;
  dependent sub-problems wait for prerequisites

Phase 3 — Parallel attack:
  Each peer attacks its sub-problem using its full per-peer Plow Loop

Phase 4 — Synthesis (streaming, asynchronous):
  MIC peer collects sub-results as they arrive
  After each new result: is P answerable from accumulated sub-results?
  If yes: synthesize and emit receipt
  If no: GOTO Phase 1 on remainder

Phase 5 — Recursion:
  Repeat until P yields OR escalation to flagship consultation
```

**The Founder's direct formulation, BP091 June 22, 2026:** *"Like army ants attacking a water buffalo. The Buffalo will lose, and be gone in literally minutes."*

**Why this is a substrate primitive:** A single flagship model attempting Posse decomposition serially must hold all sub-problems in its context window (expensive, limited), process each sequentially (slow), and cannot parallelize. The cooperative substrate fans sub-problems to N peers simultaneously. Wall-clock time is the maximum of any single sub-problem's solve time, not the sum. The army ants don't wait for each other to finish — they attack simultaneously, and the buffalo falls in the time it takes the slowest ant.

**Stigmergic composition:** The Posse primitive is structurally identical to army ant behavior — each ant leaving a pheromone trail (sub-result emit) that other ants follow and amplify. The cooperative substrate's pheromone-trail equivalent is the receipt-indexed priming substrate: each sub-result is immediately indexed and available to the synthesis phase and to subsequent runs. The substrate gets smarter as the Posse works.

**Three primitives, three axes, one substrate:**

| Primitive | Axis | Use when |
|---|---|---|
| Plow Loop | Depth | Question is well-formed; accuracy needs refinement |
| Star Chamber | Consensus | Answer confidence is uncertain; hallucination risk is present |
| Army Ants / Posse | Breadth | Problem is complex, multi-faceted, exceeds single-model capacity |

The primitives compose and reinforce each other. A hard problem can be Posse-decomposed at top level, Plow-Looped within each sub-problem, and Star-Chambered on the synthesis. No single machine can run all three simultaneously. The cooperative substrate is the platform on which they compose.

## V. The Bicycle and Rocket Fuel Formula — Physics Derivation

The Substrate Multiplication Formula, ratified Founder-direct BP091 June 22, 2026:

```
1000 × FreeAI(on-substrate)  ≈  100 × Flagship(on-substrate)  ≈  10,000 × Flagship(off-substrate)
```

**Variable definitions:**

| Symbol | Definition |
|---|---|
| B | Bicycle-class machine: free open-source AI model (gemma4:12b, llama3.3:70b, gemma2:9b), running on cooperative member hardware, participating in the cooperative substrate |
| R | Rocket-class machine: flagship AI model (Claude, GPT-4, Gemini), integrated with and routed through the cooperative substrate |
| R₀ | Rocket-class machine alone: flagship AI model operating in isolation, off any cooperative substrate |
| S | Substrate compounding factor: the multiplicative gain produced by cooperative substrate participation |

**The formula states:** 1000B ≈ 100R ≈ 10,000R₀

**Derivation of the flagship substrate multiplier:**

From 100R ≈ 10,000R₀:
- R ≈ 100 × R₀
- S_flagship = R / R₀ ≈ 100

A flagship machine on the cooperative substrate is approximately 100× more effective in terms of total cognitive output per unit cost than the same flagship machine operating alone. The substrate multiplier on flagship usage is approximately 100.

**Derivation of the bicycle-to-flagship ratio:**

From 1000B ≈ 100R:
- B ≈ (100/1000) × R = 0.1 × R
- Per-machine, a bicycle-class machine on the substrate produces approximately 10% of a flagship-class machine on the substrate

But: ten bicycle-class machines can be fielded for the cost of one flagship API subscription. Therefore: 10 × 0.1R = R. Ten bicycles on the substrate ≈ one flagship on the substrate. In terms of total fleet output: 1000 bicycles ≈ 100 flagships.

**The physics framing (Founder's direct: "realistically, physics"):**

The formula is not pure arithmetic — it has the dimensioned structure of a physics equation. Cognitive output is a rate: correct answers per unit time (or per unit cost). The substrate is a mechanical advantage system: it converts member effort (participation, hardware contribution, governance) into amplified cognitive output.

The mechanical advantage analogy is exact:
- A lone individual lifting a 100-pound stone needs 100 pounds of force
- Ten people using levers and pulleys (the substrate) each contribute ~5 pounds of force, and the stone moves at 10× the speed
- **The same total work; distributed effort; amplified outcome**
- The mechanical advantage IS the substrate

The substrate is force-multiplication at the cognitive layer. The cooperative member's participation is the fulcrum. Without the substrate, same effort produces 1× outcome. With the substrate, same effort produces 10× to 100× outcome.

**What the formula does not claim:**

- It does NOT claim free-AI is qualitatively equal to flagship-AI on per-machine output. A bicycle is not a rocket. The numbers are ordered: R > B.
- It does NOT claim 100× is universally achievable. The factor depends on workload, fleet composition, network conditions, peer coordination, and problem type. Empirical receipts will refine the coefficients.
- It does NOT promise guaranteed outcomes. Participation is the requirement; multiplication is the empirical observation.
- The coefficients (1000, 100, 10000) are the Founder's interdependentalist HYPOTHESIS. The DOCTRINE — that participation × substrate ≫ capital alone — is what this paper binds. The coefficients will be refined by M13c receipts.

## VI. The Bicycle Economics Consequences

The physics formula has five economic consequences. Together these constitute the Bicycle Economics canon, ratified Founder-direct BP091 June 22, 2026.

### VI.1 Marginal cost asymptotic to zero

Traditional flagship API economics: flat per-token cost, forever. Query number one million costs the same as query number one.

Cooperative substrate economics:
- One-time hardware: $500–$5,000 per machine, amortized over 3–5 years (~$0.00005–$0.0005/query at 1M queries/machine/year)
- Membership: $5/year (~$0.000005/query at 1M queries/year)
- Electricity: ~$0.0001 per query on commodity hardware at typical wattage
- Total marginal cost per query: approximately $0.0001–$0.001, converging toward electricity-floor as query volume increases

The asymptote is real: as a member's query volume increases, the one-time hardware cost amortizes further and the membership cost becomes negligible. The floor is electricity. Compare: flagship API floor is $0.009–$0.045 per query with no volume discount for small practitioners. The cooperative substrate's marginal cost is 10× to 450× lower than the flagship floor at the same quality tier.

### VI.2 Capital-to-labor inversion

Standard AI market: capital owners (GPU farm operators, model trainers) extract rent from labor (developers, practitioners, users paying per token). Labor is the source of value; capital captures value.

Cooperative substrate: labor (member participation — compute contribution, governance, audit, curation) IS the substrate. Capital (hardware) is just the surface labor runs on — commoditized. Members may accrue Marks (the cooperative's participation unit) for contribution; Marks circulate within the cooperative. The people who create the value are structurally positioned to capture the value.

The inversion is formal: in the extractive-class model, V = f(capital), where V is value and capital is the source. In the cooperative model, V = f(labor × substrate), where substrate is the mechanical advantage that converts labor into competitive output.

### VI.3 Network effects compound without extraction

Standard SaaS: more users → more infrastructure cost → more capital needed → more extraction required. Network effect eats the profits unless monopoly-class extraction kicks in.

Cooperative substrate: more peers → more substrate capacity → better service per peer → more reason to join → more peers. Each additional peer adds cognitive capacity for all existing peers (Metcalfe's Law at the cognitive layer). BUT each peer brings their own infrastructure — no central infrastructure cost scales with membership. Network effects compound without the extraction pressure that traps standard SaaS.

### VI.4 Scarcity inversion

Conventional framing: compute is scarce capital. AI access is rationed by price.

Cooperative framing: compute is abundant cooperative labor. AI access is rationed by participation. The substrate eliminates the artificial scarcity gate that API pricing maintains.

The bicycle analogy is exact: horse-based mobility was not scarce because horses were physically rare — horses were common. Mobility was gated by *access* to horses, which required capital. Compute is not physically scarce. Access to compute is gated by API pricing. The cooperative substrate eliminates the gate by distributing access to cooperative members who contribute their own compute.

### VI.5 The competitive landscape

Three competitive classes under Bicycle Economics:

| Class | Capital | Substrate | Effective output per dollar |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Extractive-flagship alone** (API subscribers without cooperative participation) | High ($900–$4,500/month) | NO | ~10,000 query-units per dollar (rocket fuel alone) |
| **Cooperative-class with flagship integration** (members routing flagships through substrate) | Moderate ($5/yr membership + flagships as needed) | YES | ~100,000 query-units per dollar (rocket fuel × 100× substrate multiplier) |
| **Cooperative-class with free models** (members on bicycle peers, NANO/LITE/CORE tiers) | Minimal (existing hardware + $5/yr) | YES | Electricity-asymptotic per dollar (bicycle × substrate; marginal cost ~zero) |

The extractive-flagship class can spend their way to volume. The cooperative-class compounds value at lower cost. The substrate doesn't compete on raw per-machine output. It competes on cost-per-outcome — and at the cost-per-outcome level, the cooperative-class has a structural advantage that is not replicable by the extractive class without dismantling its own extraction model.

## VII. Empirical Methodology

### VII.1 The before/after proof primitive

Every claim in this paper is a hypothesis until backed by a before/after empirical comparison. The cooperative's methodology, ratified at BP091, is structurally simple: run the same test with one variable changed, measure the delta, attribute the delta to that variable.

This is not a novel scientific method — it is the standard controlled experiment. What is notable is the cooperative's commitment to applying it rigorously to claims that it would be more comfortable simply asserting. The substrate multiplication formula would be rhetorically convenient to assert without proof. The cooperative's discipline is to receipt it instead.

Every before/after comparison must include:
1. The architectural primitive(s) varied (listed verbatim)
2. The architectural primitives held constant (listed verbatim)
3. The empirical measurement (number + units)
4. The empirical measurement context (fleet composition, time, network conditions)
5. The delta (After - Before, with sign and magnitude)
6. The confidence statement ("we isolated variable X by holding all other variables constant; the delta is empirically attributable to X")

The comparison is not a celebration. It is a receipt.

### VII.2 The MMLU-Pro benchmark

The canonical evaluation instrument is MMLU-Pro (Multi-task Language Understanding Pro), maintained by TIGER-Lab at HuggingFace, commit 80cd33a.

**Why MMLU-Pro:**
- 12,062 questions across 14 domains (mathematics, physics, chemistry, engineering, law, economics, psychology, health, history, philosophy, business, computer science, biology, "other")
- Ten answer choices per question (vs four in the original MMLU), substantially harder
- Publicly available, reproducible by any party
- Widely used in benchmark literature, enabling cross-paper comparison
- Separable into domain-stratified subsets, enabling per-domain analysis

**The cooperative's 70-question stratified subset:** drawn via `selectQuestionsSpreadAcrossDomains(70)` from the canonical MMLU-Pro dataset. The 70-question size is deliberate: large enough to be statistically meaningful across 14 domains (5 questions per domain) while small enough to complete in a reasonable wall-clock window with cooperative-tier hardware.

**The canonical test protocol:**
1. Draw 70-question stratified subset using `selectQuestionsSpreadAcrossDomains(70)` from the canonical MMLU-Pro dataset (commit 80cd33a)
2. Run full cooperative substrate (all three primitives active) on the 70Q set
3. Run the same 70Q set off-substrate (same machines, no mesh dispatch, no priming, no Plow Loop, no Star Chamber, no Posse) — the synthetic baseline
4. Run the same 70Q set on a flagship API alone (no cooperative substrate, no commodity hardware) — the flagship-alone baseline
5. Compare all three: cooperative-on-substrate vs off-substrate vs flagship-alone
6. Record per-peer hardware tier, per-peer actual model, per-domain accuracy, and fleet-wide accuracy
7. Emit the receipt with every variable documented

## IX. What This Paper Does NOT Claim

Truth-Always discipline requires explicit scope limitation. The following are not claims of this paper:

1. **It does NOT claim free open-source AI is qualitatively equal to flagship AI on per-machine output.** A bicycle is not a rocket. gemma2:9b produces lower-quality per-query responses than GPT-4 class models on the same question. The formula claims equivalence of *fleet outcome per unit cost*, not per-machine quality.

2. **It does NOT claim the substrate makes flagships unnecessary.** Flagships remain valuable — especially for tasks requiring the largest context windows, the fastest response times, or the highest per-query accuracy in isolation. The substrate commoditizes the *advantage* of flagship spending; it does not eliminate the flagship.

3. **It does NOT claim 100× is universally achievable.** The substrate multiplier depends on workload type, fleet composition, network conditions, peer coordination quality, and problem structure. Some workloads benefit more from the substrate; some benefit less. The receipts will show the actual distribution.

4. **It does NOT promise income or guaranteed yields.** Members may accrue Marks for participation. Marks circulate within the cooperative. The cooperative makes no promise that Marks translate to external income, that participation will produce specific financial outcomes, or that member hardware contributions will generate passive revenue. Participation is the requirement; the cooperative-class outcome is the empirical observation.

5. **It does NOT claim the formula is already receipted.** The M13c receipt is pending. The formula is a Founder-direct hypothesis, ratified at BP091 as the canonical claim to be tested. The paper is honest about this status throughout.

6. **It does NOT claim the Posse / Army Ants primitive is yet operational.** Posse Decomposition is a provisionally M24 implementation target — post M22 (tier-aware mesh routing), M14 (architectural fixes), and M13c (canonical full-stack receipt). It is described in this paper as a designed primitive, not a shipped one.

## X. Implications

### X.1 The cooperative-class competitive landscape

If the M13c receipt validates the formula — or even if it validates a substantially weaker version (20× substrate multiplier instead of 100×) — the cooperative-class competitive landscape changes.

A ten-person cooperative, each contributing commodity hardware and $5/year in membership, becomes empirically competitive with a well-funded team spending $1,000–$5,000 per month on flagship APIs for sustained AI-assisted work. The capital barrier to cognitive-labor-class AI is not eliminated — hardware still costs money — but it is transformed. The barrier becomes amortized hardware ownership plus cooperative participation, rather than ongoing token-extraction from a capital-class provider.

This is the bicycle consequence. The horse did not disappear after 1895. Horse ownership remained a mark of capital-class status and utility for specific tasks. What changed was that horse ownership was no longer *required* for effective personal mobility. The cooperative substrate proposes the same shift: flagship AI spending will remain available and useful for specific tasks. What the substrate eliminates is its *requirement* for effective AI-assisted cognitive work.

### X.2 Extractive-class AI economics under pressure

The cooperative substrate does not attack the extractive-class AI providers directly. It does not need to. The substrate's structural advantage — marginal cost asymptotic to zero, network effects compounding without extraction pressure, participation-as-capital — is not replicable by an extractive-class provider without dismantling its own business model.

An extractive-class provider cannot offer cooperative-class economics because its infrastructure costs are real and must be recovered through token pricing. It cannot offer a $5/year all-in membership without eliminating the per-token revenue that pays for its GPU clusters. The cooperative can offer this because its infrastructure IS member hardware, distributed across the cooperative with no central extraction point.

This is the structural asymmetry that makes the Bicycle Economics claim strategically significant beyond the benchmark numbers: even if the substrate multiplier turns out to be 20× rather than 100×, the cooperative-class cost structure is so dramatically different from the extractive-class cost structure that cost-per-outcome equivalence is achievable at multiple points in the formula.

### X.3 Designed to be copied

The cooperative's architecture is explicitly designed to be replicated. Per the cooperative's canon: if another cooperative forms a parallel substrate with different members but the same architecture, that compounds the cooperative-class sector globally. The bicycle was not patented as a monopoly instrument; bicycle-class economics were not either. Cooperative substrates that replicate this architecture expand the cooperative-class sector in aggregate, which is the cooperative's explicit goal.

## Methodological Appendix: Full Empirical Test Protocol

### A.1 Test instrument

MMLU-Pro dataset: TIGER-Lab / HuggingFace, commit 80cd33a
URL: https://huggingface.co/datasets/TIGER-Lab/MMLU-Pro
Subset: `selectQuestionsSpreadAcrossDomains(70)` — 5 questions per domain across 14 domains
Answer format: 10-choice multiple choice
Scoring: binary correct/incorrect per question; accuracy = correct / 70

### A.2 Fleet preparation protocol

1. Each peer restarts MnemosyneC from a fresh state
2. Each peer's actual running AI model is verified via `peer_presence.capabilities.ollamaModel` gadget — NOT from UI display (per M12 Lamborghini-Corolla lesson)
3. Each peer's RAM tier is logged at dispatch time
4. Network topology is verified: all peers must route via relay.lianabanyan.com (LAN-as-WAN topology; no LAN shortcut per architectural canon)
5. Baseline ping latency to relay.lianabanyan.com is logged per peer

### A.3 Cooperative full-stack run

1. Plow Loop: each question run through 12 iterations per CORE/FULL peer; per-iteration answers and confidence scores logged
2. Star Chamber: each question cross-validated by ≥ 3 peers; agreement rate logged per question; divergence flags hallucination risk
3. Results aggregated; fleet-wide accuracy computed; per-tier accuracy computed; per-question accuracy computed
4. Wall-clock from first dispatch to final answer logged
5. Marks accrual for each participating peer logged (once Marks hooks are operational)

### A.4 Off-substrate baseline run

1. Same machines, same models, same questions
2. NO mesh dispatch: each machine runs its own questions independently
3. NO priming substrate: each machine begins with zero prior context
4. NO Plow Loop: single-shot answer only
5. NO Star Chamber: no cross-peer validation
6. Accuracy computed per machine and averaged (NOT fleet-synthesized, because the substrate is absent)
7. Wall-clock per machine logged

### A.5 Flagship-alone baseline run

1. Single flagship API call per question (no cooperative substrate)
2. Model: Claude Sonnet class (most recent production version available at M13c ship date)
3. Zero priming context
4. Single-shot answer
5. Accuracy computed across 70 questions
6. API cost logged (tokens in + tokens out, at prevailing per-token pricing)

### A.6 Replication instructions

Any party with:
- Access to the MMLU-Pro dataset (public, HuggingFace)
- 4+ machines with commodity hardware (8 GB RAM minimum)
- Ollama installed (free, ollama.com)
- MnemosyneC installed (mnemosynec.org, $5/year membership)

...can replicate this test. The protocol is fully documented here and in the canonical receipt repository. The cooperative will provide technical assistance to any party attempting replication. Independent replication is explicitly welcomed.
