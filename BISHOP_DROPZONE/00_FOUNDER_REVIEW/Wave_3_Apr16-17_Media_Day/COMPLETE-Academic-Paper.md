## ABSTRACT

Non-transferable, non-redeemable platform service credits that provide access to business-to-business services do not constitute securities under the *Howey* test, subsequent SEC guidance, or state securities laws. This Article establishes that such credits should be treated identically to established closed-loop systems including airline frequent flyer miles, retail loyalty points, and prepaid service cards—none of which have been classified as securities despite seventy years of regulatory practice. Through doctrinal analysis, comparative examination, and policy evaluation, this Article demonstrates that platform service credits fail all four prongs of the *Howey* investment contract test and bear strong family resemblance to enumerated categories of non-securities under *Reves v. Ernst & Young*. The Article proposes a compliance framework that permits platform cooperatives and B2B service marketplaces to operate without securities registration while maintaining robust consumer protection through existing alternative regulatory schemes including Regulation E, FinCEN closed-loop exemptions, and contract law. This analysis has urgent relevance given the SEC's aggressive enforcement posture toward digital platforms and the corresponding regulatory uncertainty facing platform cooperative models that could democratize access to business services and create alternatives to venture capital-funded platform monopolies.

**Keywords:** Securities regulation, Howey test, platform cooperatives, closed-loop systems, prepaid access, loyalty programs, digital assets, B2B marketplaces, gig economy

## APPENDIX: THE FIVE-PRINCIPLE COMPLIANCE FRAMEWORK

**For platforms seeking safe harbor from securities classification:**

### PRINCIPLE 1: NON-TRANSFERABLE
Credits cannot be transferred between users, sold on secondary markets, or traded on exchanges.

**Implementation:**
- Terms of Service explicit prohibition on transfers
- Technical controls preventing account-to-account transfers
- Monitoring for attempted transfers
- Account termination for violations
- Exception: Limited family pooling (analogous to airline miles)

### PRINCIPLE 2: NON-REDEEMABLE FOR CASH
Credits cannot be converted to fiat currency, cryptocurrency, or monetary instruments.

**Implementation:**
- Terms of Service prohibition on cash redemption
- No technical capability for credit-to-cash conversion
- No platform-facilitated secondary markets
- Clear disclosures: "Credits have no cash value"
- Exception: De minimis state law refunds ($5-$10 maximum)

### PRINCIPLE 3: SERVICE ACCESS ONLY
Credits redeemable exclusively for services, not for profit-sharing, revenue participation, or equity interests.

**Implementation:**
- Clear service menu defining redemption options
- Separate capital structure (equity ≠ credits)
- No profit-sharing language in marketing or terms
- Independent accounting: credits = service obligation liability

### PRINCIPLE 4: ACTIVE USE REQUIRED
Platform design requires active member participation to derive credit value.

**Implementation:**
- No automatic credit appreciation
- Expiration of unused credits (if disclosed)
- "Use it or lose it" policies incentivizing consumption
- Value derived from consuming services, not holding credits
- Loyalty bonuses require member activity (making purchases)

### PRINCIPLE 5: CLOSED-LOOP ECOSYSTEM
Credits function within closed ecosystem with no external market value.

**Implementation:**
- Credits valid only for platform services
- No partner network where credits function as currency
- No blockchain/distributed ledger enabling external trading
- Terms of Service: Credits are "platform-specific service access units"

**RESULT:** Credits meeting all five principles are **NOT securities**
