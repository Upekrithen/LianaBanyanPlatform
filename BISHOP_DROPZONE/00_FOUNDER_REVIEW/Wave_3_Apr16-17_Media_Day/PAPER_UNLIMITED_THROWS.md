### A response to the question entrepreneurs are afraid to answer

*Jonathan R. Jones*
*Founder & General Manager, Liana Banyan Corporation*

---

In November 2017, a user on Hacker News posted a comment that has haunted me for years. Not because it was wrong — because it was right.

> Entrepreneurship is like one of those carnival games where you throw darts. Middle class kids can afford one throw. Most miss. Rich kids can afford many throws. They try over and over until they hit something, then give speeches about meritocracy. Poor kids aren't visiting the carnival. They're the ones working it.

The thread that followed was extraordinary. Hundreds of engineers, founders, and frustrated builders wrestling with the same question: *Why isn't life fair?* And arriving, collectively, at a shrug. "Life isn't fair. We're all born into different means." "Nature and the universe aren't fair." "The default state is one of unfairness."

They're right. Life isn't fair.

But that's a description. Not a design.

## The Problem Isn't the Dart — It's the Price of Throwing

The HN commenter nailed the structural issue: rich kids get unlimited throws. The game itself — starting a business, testing an idea, iterating on a product — isn't actually that hard to learn. The hard part is surviving the learning.

Every failed startup costs money. Every pivot costs time. Every "learning experience" comes with rent due, children to feed, and the quiet erosion of confidence that happens when the people around you start suggesting you get a real job.

The dart game isn't rigged because the target is hard to hit. It's rigged because the price of each throw eliminates most players before they develop the arm.

One commenter pushed back: software developers get "unlimited free throws" by moonlighting — building side projects on nights and weekends while keeping their day jobs. And there's truth in that, for software. But what about the meal prep entrepreneur? The neighborhood mechanic? The woman who could run logistics for an entire community if someone would just let her prove it?

For them, there is no moonlighting equivalent. Every throw costs real money in a real world that doesn't care about your potential.

Until now.

## The Benefits of Losing

One of my favorite animated films is *Megamind*. Not because of the animation or the humor — because of two lines that I've carried with me for years.

The first is Megamind himself: **"There's a benefit to losing: you get to learn from your mistakes."**

The second is Roxanne Ritchi, speaking about him: **"The Megamind I knew would never have run from a fight, even when he knew he had absolutely no chance of winning. It was your best quality."**

Those two lines contain the entire philosophy of what I've built.

The benefit of losing is learning. And the willingness to fight — even hopelessly, even when the odds are absurd — is not foolishness. It's the quality that separates the people who eventually win from the people who never throw.

I know this because I've lived it in the most quantifiable arena on earth.

## 25,399 Games

I've played over 25,000 games of chess on a single account. Hundreds of thousands more across other accounts since 1997. My highest rating hit 2118 — the top 0.4% of chess players worldwide. On most days I hover in the 2080s.

Here's the part nobody talks about when they see the rating: **I've lost more games than I've won.**

50% of my games — 12,577 of them — are losses. My win rate is 46%. The rest are draws.

Read that again. I am in the top half-percent of chess players on the planet, and I lose more than I win.

How? Because every loss taught me something. Because a 2100-rated player doesn't win more often than they lose — they lose against *better and better opponents*. The losses get harder, which means the education gets deeper.

Prove it to yourself: flip a coin 100 times. You'll get roughly 50 heads and 50 tails. The law of averages guarantees that over enough games, you'll lose about as much as you win. So how do you get ahead if you lose half the time?

By playing a different game. By learning from every loss. By treating failure as tuition, not punishment.

And by throwing again.

## The Bomb Disposal Analogy

I once wrote: *"The rules of how to defuse bombs are written in blood; but there are only so many rules to learn. A brutally effective strategy, especially when the path is shared."*

Every entrepreneur who fails learns something. The problem is that most of them learn it alone, at enormous personal cost, and the lesson dies with their business. The knowledge written in their blood evaporates.

What if it didn't?

What if every failed business inside a shared ecosystem became a *documented lesson* for the next person? What if the bomb disposal rules were published — openly, transparently, free for anyone to read — so that the next person approaching the same bomb doesn't have to learn the hard way?

That's what a cooperative platform does that a VC-funded startup ecosystem cannot. In the VC model, your failure is private. Your postmortem (if you write one) gets a Medium post that nobody reads. Your lessons dissolve into "tough break, better luck next time."

In a cooperative, your failure is communal. Your lessons are shared. Your crew learns from your mistakes, and the next crew learns from theirs. The rules accumulate. The blood dries into ink.

Calvin Coolidge said it best: *"Nothing in the world can take the place of persistence. Talent will not; nothing is more common than unsuccessful men with talent. Genius will not; unrewarded genius is almost a proverb. Education will not; the world is full of educated derelicts. Persistence and determination alone are omnipotent."*

The HexIsle Business Simulator is a persistence machine. It lets you try and fail and try again, at the smallest possible cost, with the largest possible community of people doing the same thing.

## "Don't You Just Get Tired of Eking By?"

Jim Carrey says this line in *Dumb and Dumber*, and I feel it in my bones every single day. That ache — the one that makes you look at the carnival game and think *I could hit that target if they'd just let me throw* — is not laziness. It's not entitlement. It's the rational frustration of a capable person trapped inside a system that charges admission for the privilege of trying.

I've felt it for 47 years. Since a sixth-grade project on floating cities. Since my first game prototype. Since every time I looked at a problem and thought: *I could fix that, if I could just get to the workbench.*

But in forging a pathway to success, I must also in good conscience burn a permanent pathway into the jungle of life for any who dare — charting the pitfalls and resources on my treasure map.

I don't know where I'm going. But I know what I'm looking for. And in time, I will find both.
