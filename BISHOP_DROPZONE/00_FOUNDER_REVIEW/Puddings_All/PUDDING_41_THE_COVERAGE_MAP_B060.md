# Pudding #41: The Coverage Map
## How Bounty Photography Turns Local Business Visibility into Cooperative Work

---

### At a Glance (~50 words)
Most small businesses have terrible online photos — or none at all. Bounty Photography pays cooperative members to photograph local businesses, post the results on their own social media, and submit the link as proof. The platform stores no photos. The photographer keeps 83.3%. The business gets visible.

---

### More Info (~300 words)

Google a local restaurant. The photos are either from 2019, taken by a customer in bad lighting, or missing entirely. The business doesn't have time to do a photoshoot. They can't afford a professional photographer. So they stay invisible — which means they lose to the chain restaurant with a marketing department.

Bounty Photography fixes this with a simple loop. The cooperative posts a photography bounty for a local business. A member photographer claims the assignment, goes to the business, takes the required shots, and posts them on their own social media account — Instagram, TikTok, Google Business Photos, wherever they have an audience. Then they submit the post URL to the platform as proof of completion.

The platform pays the photographer. The business gets fresh, high-quality photos on social media with real engagement potential. The photographer builds their portfolio and social presence. And the cooperative stores nothing but the metadata — the URL, the timestamp, the photographer's member ID, and the payment record.

Three parties benefit. The business gets visible. The photographer gets paid and builds their brand. The cooperative gets a stronger local economy — which feeds every other cooperative function from storefronts to rideshare to housing.

The Coverage Map is what makes this feel like a mission rather than a gig. The map shows where businesses have been photographed and where gaps remain. A photographer can look at the map and see: "There are twelve businesses on Main Street with no coverage. I could fill that gap this weekend." The work isn't dispatched by an algorithm. The photographer sees the need and decides to meet it.

### Full Detail

**The zero-storage innovation**

Bounty Photography is built on Innovation #2100 — the Bounty Photography Network. Its defining technical feature is zero-storage architecture applied to creative work.

When a photographer takes photos for a bounty assignment, those photos never touch Liana Banyan's servers. Instead, the photographer posts them on their preferred social media platform — the same place they'd post their best work anyway. The platform records only:

- The social media post URL
- The platform the photo was posted on (Instagram, TikTok, Google Business, etc.)
- The timestamp of the post
- The photographer's member ID
- The business being photographed
- The bounty assignment ID
- Tags and metadata specified by the assignment
- The payment amount and completion status

Total storage per completed bounty: roughly 1 kilobyte. Compare that to hosting the actual photos: 3-10 megabytes per image, times multiple angles, times thousands of assignments. The zero-storage model keeps infrastructure costs trivial while letting the photographer retain full ownership of their creative work on their own social channels.

**The dual-channel benefit**

Every completed bounty creates value in two places simultaneously:

**Channel 1 — Social media.** The photographer's post reaches their followers, appears in local search results, shows up in hashtag feeds, and generates organic engagement. The business gets exposure not through a paid ad, but through authentic, local content created by a real person in the community.

**Channel 2 — Platform record.** The cooperative's database records that the business has been covered, the photographer has been paid, and the coverage gap has been filled. This feeds the Coverage Map, the photographer's ADAPT score, and the cooperative's understanding of local business needs.

Traditional gig photography platforms create value in only one channel — the platform's marketplace. The photographer uploads photos to the platform, the platform delivers them to the client, and the photographer's social presence gets nothing. Bounty Photography inverts this: the photographer's social media IS the delivery channel, and the platform just tracks the transaction.

**How bounties are created**

Bounties originate from three sources:

**Business request.** A local business that's a cooperative member (or a Captain managing a coalition of businesses) can request photography coverage. They specify what they need: exterior shots, interior atmosphere, food photography, product photos, team portraits. The bounty enters the system with requirements, deadline, and payout.

**Coverage gap detection.** The Coverage Map identifies businesses with no recent photography. The cooperative can automatically generate bounties for uncovered businesses, prioritized by factors like business type, location density, and time since last coverage. A restaurant that hasn't been photographed in six months gets priority over one photographed last week.

**Community initiative.** A Captain or community organizer can create a coverage campaign — "Photograph every business on Elm Street this month" — with pooled bounties and a shared deadline. This turns coverage into a community event rather than isolated gig assignments.

**The ADAPT score connection**

Photographers who complete bounties build their ADAPT reputation score across six dimensions: Effectiveness (photo quality and completion rate), Adaptability (ability to handle different assignment types), Durability (consistency over time), Alignment (adherence to assignment requirements), Participation (volume and regularity), and Transmission (whether their work generates downstream engagement and value for the business).

The ADAPT score is displayed on the photographer's profile as a professional trust signal. It is NOT a star rating. There are no 1-5 stars. The score uses horizontal bars showing relative strength across dimensions, with language like "top 15% in Effectiveness" or "room to grow in Participation." Positive framing only — the system never says "failing" or "below average."

High ADAPT scores give photographers priority access to premium bounties — assignments with higher payouts, more interesting locations, or time-sensitive urgency. The score rewards consistent quality and reliability, not volume alone. A photographer who completes 10 excellent assignments scores higher than one who rushes through 50 mediocre ones.

**What the Coverage Map shows**

The Coverage Map is a geographic visualization of where photography bounties have been completed and where gaps remain. It serves two functions:

**For photographers:** The map shows available work. Green areas have recent coverage. Amber areas are aging. Red areas have no coverage at all. A photographer can plan their week by looking at the map and choosing which neighborhoods or business clusters to focus on.

**For the cooperative:** The map shows the health of local business visibility. A community where 90% of businesses have fresh photography is a community where the cooperative is working. A community with widespread red zones needs attention — more bounties, more photographers, or outreach to businesses that haven't joined.

The map updates in real time as bounties are completed. A photographer finishing an assignment sees the business flip from red to green immediately. That feedback loop — do the work, see the impact — is what makes Bounty Photography feel like community service rather than gig labor.

**Economics**

Bounty payouts follow Cost+20%:

- The payout covers the photographer's time, travel, and equipment use (actual cost)
- Plus 20% for the cooperative's operating margin
- The photographer keeps 83.3% of the bounty value

A typical bounty might pay $30-75 depending on the scope (number of shots, location complexity, special requirements). A photographer completing 3-4 bounties in an afternoon earns $90-300 for a few hours of work they'd enjoy doing anyway — walking through their neighborhood, photographing businesses they know, posting content that builds their social following.

The business pays for the bounty through their cooperative membership or Captain account. For a small business spending $50 to get fresh professional photography with real social media distribution, the value proposition is dramatically better than hiring a freelance photographer ($200-500) or running social media ads ($50-200/month with no guarantee of authentic content).
