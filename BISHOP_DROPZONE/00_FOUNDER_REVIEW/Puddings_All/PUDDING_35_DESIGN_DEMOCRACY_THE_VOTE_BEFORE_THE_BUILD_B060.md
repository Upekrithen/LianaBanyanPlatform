# Pudding #35: Design Democracy — The Vote Before the Build
## Why Members Decide What Gets Manufactured

---

### At a Glance (~50 words)
Most platforms decide what to build in a boardroom. Liana Banyan puts product designs to a member vote before a single unit is manufactured. Design Democracy means no overproduction, no guessing, and no warehouse full of things nobody wanted. The community votes. The factory listens.

---

### More Info (~300 words)

Traditional manufacturing works backwards. A company designs a product, manufactures thousands of units, and then hopes customers buy them. When they don't, the result is markdowns, waste, and write-offs. The entire system optimizes for prediction — and predictions are usually wrong.

Design Democracy reverses the sequence. A member or team submits a product design to the platform. The community reviews it, discusses it, and votes. Designs that earn enough support enter the production pipeline. Designs that don't are archived — not deleted, not punished, just not manufactured yet.

This solves three problems simultaneously.

First, **demand is validated before production begins**. No pioneer node spends materials, energy, or labor on a product the community hasn't endorsed. Every unit produced has a constituency behind it.

Second, **creators retain authorship throughout**. The designer's name stays on the product. Their 83.3% share stays intact. The vote doesn't transfer ownership — it authorizes production. The community says "yes, build this," not "this belongs to us now."

Third, **the vote creates a natural quality filter**. Members who vote are members who use the platform, pay the $5/year membership, and understand what the cooperative needs. Their judgment reflects real demand from real participants, not market research from consultants.

The competitive reference is Snapshot — the governance tool used by DAOs. But where Snapshot decides protocol parameters, Design Democracy decides what physical products enter the world. It's governance applied to manufacturing, not finance.

Every product in the pipeline earned its place through demonstrated member interest. That's not democracy as decoration. That's demand as governance.

### Full Detail

**How it works mechanically**

The Design Battle page is an arena where product designs compete for community endorsement. A designer submits their concept with specifications, material requirements, expected Cost+20% pricing, and visual documentation. The design enters a contest window — typically 7-14 days — during which any member can review and vote.

Voting uses relative framing rather than absolute scores. Designs are described as "Leading," "Strong contender," or "Gathering support" rather than first-place / second-place rankings. This prevents early-leader runaway effects where the first design to get a few votes attracts all subsequent votes through momentum rather than merit.

When a design clears the support threshold, it moves into the Factory Pipeline — a six-stage production process: DESIGN → SLA (prototype) → VALIDATE → SLS (production body) → INJECT (if applicable) → SHIP. Each stage has its own verification checkpoint. A design can be paused or returned to an earlier stage if validation reveals issues.

**The connection to Pioneer Nodes**

Pioneer Nodes are the distributed manufacturing units that actually produce the endorsed designs. There are a maximum of 100 Pioneer Nodes in the system, each with registered equipment (SLA printers, FDM printers, CNC routers, laser cutters, injection molders, or desktop manufacturing stations).

When a design clears the vote and enters the pipeline, the system matches it to Pioneer Nodes with the right equipment. A design requiring SLA prototyping routes to nodes with SLA capability. A design requiring injection molding routes to nodes with injection equipment.

This creates a marketplace of manufacturing capacity governed by community design choices. The Pioneer Node operators don't decide what to make — the membership does. The operators provide capacity, skill, and equipment. The community provides direction.

**Why this matters for the economics**

Traditional manufacturing absorbs demand risk in the cost structure. A factory that produces 10,000 units expecting to sell 8,000 prices each unit to cover the 2,000 that won't sell. The customer pays for waste baked into the margin.

Design Democracy eliminates that waste margin. If the community votes for 500 units, the pioneer network produces 500 units. The Cost+20% calculation applies to actual demand, not speculative demand. No overproduction surplus is hidden in the price.

This is why Cost+20% works as a pricing floor. The 20% covers the cooperative's operating costs on goods the community has already endorsed. There's no speculative cushion because there's no speculative production.

**The Manufacturing Ladder**

Design Democracy doesn't require factory-scale production. The system recognizes four manufacturing levels:

1. **Kit** — Design files and instructions. The member manufactures at home with their own equipment. Cost+20% applies to the design and instruction package only.
2. **Bench** — Small-batch production from a single Pioneer Node. Typically 10-50 units. Cost+20% covers materials, labor, and the 20% operating margin.
3. **Shop** — Medium production across 2-5 Pioneer Nodes. Coordination through the platform. 50-500 units.
4. **Factory** — Full production run across the pioneer network. 500+ units. The Canister System and other dedicated manufacturing tools operate at this level.

A design that earns 20 votes might enter the pipeline at Kit level. A design that earns 2,000 votes might enter at Factory level. The manufacturing scale matches the community signal.

**What makes this different from crowdfunding**

Kickstarter validates demand through money — backers pledge dollars before the product exists. Design Democracy validates demand through participation — members vote before pledging money. The vote comes first, then the production plan, then the purchase.

This separation matters because it removes financial pressure from the evaluation process. A member voting for a design isn't committing money. They're expressing judgment about what the cooperative should produce. The financial commitment comes later, when the design enters the pipeline and units are available for purchase at the known Cost+20% price.

The vote is also not binding in the way a Kickstarter pledge is. If a design clears the vote but production costs come in higher than expected, the community can be informed and the design can be re-evaluated. There's no obligation to deliver a product that doesn't meet the Cost+20% standard just because votes were cast.

**The pipeline tracker as proof of concept**

The Design Democracy page gives the pipeline tracker prominent placement. This is deliberate. The tracker shows every design currently in the pipeline, its stage, its community support level, and its expected completion. It's the proof that votes lead to products — that the system has memory and follow-through.

A member who voted for a design three months ago can check the tracker and see it moving through SLA prototyping. A member considering a new submission can see how fast the pipeline moves and what kinds of designs succeed. The tracker closes the legitimacy loop: your vote produces a thing that exists in the world.

**The design that never gets built**

Not every design clears the threshold. The archived designs aren't failures — they're records of what the community chose not to prioritize. Some may be revisited later as the membership grows or priorities shift. The archive exists to prevent good ideas from being lost just because their moment hasn't arrived.

This is governance applied to production, not punishment. A design that doesn't clear the vote isn't rejected — it's deferred. And the designer keeps every right to resubmit, refine, or take their design elsewhere. The cooperative is a production partner, not a gatekeeper.
