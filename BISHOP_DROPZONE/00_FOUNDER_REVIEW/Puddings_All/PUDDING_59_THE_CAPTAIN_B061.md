# Pudding #59: The Captain

*Every cooperative needs someone who shows up first.*

---

## At a Glance

A Captain is not a boss. A Captain is the person who opens the doors in the morning.

In Liana Banyan, a Captain is the member who takes responsibility for making things happen in a specific place or a specific domain. They do not manage people. They do not approve transactions. They do not get paid more. They show up first, they organize, and they make sure the cooperative works where they are.

---

## More Info

### The Leadership Gap in Cooperatives

Cooperatives are democratic. Everyone gets a vote. Everyone has equal standing. This is the strength.

The weakness is: who takes out the trash?

In a traditional business, the manager assigns tasks. In a cooperative, tasks need to get done without a manager. Someone needs to launch the Wildfire Run in zip code 78207. Someone needs to organize the food network in a new neighborhood. Someone needs to review the quality of the terrain maker's output before it ships.

That someone is a Captain.

### What Captains Do

A Captain is a verified member who has earned enough Marks (reputation) to unlock the Captain role. They volunteer for a specific domain — a geographic area, a craft guild, a production line. Once accepted, they get:

- **A Captain's Dashboard.** Real-time data about their domain: active members, pending orders, quality flags, Wildfire Run status, coalition membership.
- **Organizational tools.** The ability to launch Wildfire Runs, create Crew Calls (work postings), and initiate Design Democracy votes for their domain.
- **A Priority Queue.** The first thing a Captain sees is what needs attention NOW — flagged orders, pending quality reviews, member requests.
- **Photo Coverage oversight.** For geographic Captains, the ability to coordinate Bounty Photography coverage in their area.

### What Captains Do NOT Do

- **They do not set prices.** Cost+20% is structural. Nobody adjusts it.
- **They do not approve members.** Membership is $5/year and open to everyone.
- **They do not earn more money.** Captain is a role, not a pay grade.
- **They do not make unilateral decisions.** Major decisions go through Design Democracy (community vote) or the Star Chamber (dispute resolution).
- **They do not own their domain.** A Captain who stops showing up is replaced. The domain belongs to the cooperative.

### Why Captains, Not Managers

The word "manager" implies authority over people. The word "Captain" implies responsibility for a mission. A ship's captain does not own the ship. A ship's captain is responsible for getting it where it needs to go.

In the cooperative, the Captain is responsible for getting the domain where it needs to go — more members, better quality, faster fulfillment, stronger community. They earn Marks for doing this. They earn the respect of the members they serve. But they do not earn authority over those members.

## Full Detail

### The Captain's Field Note

One of the Captain's most important tools is the Field Note — a structured observation that feeds into the cooperative's Sipping Tea intelligence system. When a Captain notices something — a new business opening in the area, a member struggling with quality, a demand pattern they recognize — they log a Field Note.

Field Notes are not reports to management. They are observations from the ground that help the cooperative understand what is happening in real places, in real time. Over time, Field Notes from hundreds of Captains across the country create a living map of cooperative activity — where things are working, where they are struggling, and where opportunities exist.

### The Captain Path

Not everyone can be a Captain immediately. The path:

1. **Join.** $5/year. Welcome to the cooperative.
2. **Participate.** Buy, sell, back projects, complete bounties. Earn Marks through contribution.
3. **Qualify.** Reach the Mark threshold for Captain eligibility (varies by domain complexity).
4. **Volunteer.** Apply for a Captain role in a specific domain. The existing Captains and the cooperative review the application.
5. **Serve.** The Captain serves for a term. At the end, the domain's members can re-elect them or choose someone new.

This path ensures that Captains have demonstrated commitment to the cooperative before they take on responsibility. You cannot buy your way into a Captain role. You cannot be appointed by management (there is no management). You earn it through contribution and you keep it through service.

### Captains and the Cold Start

In a new geographic area, the Captain is often the first active member. They launch the first Wildfire Run. They organize the first food network. They recruit the first local businesses. They are the seed crystal around which the cooperative grows.

This is the hardest work in the cooperative — building something from nothing, in a specific place, with no guarantee it will work. Captains who succeed at Cold Start earn a place on the permanent honor roll. Their names are attached to the geography they opened, forever.

### The Captain Economy

Captains do not get paid by the cooperative. But Captains who are active in their domain benefit from its growth:

- A Captain who organizes a food network buys their own groceries through it — at Cost+20%.
- A Captain who builds a maker community in their area uses the same manufacturing infrastructure.
- A Captain who grows the membership in their zip code lives in a neighborhood with better cooperative services.

The Captain's incentive is not a paycheck. It is a better version of the place where they live and work. That is the cooperative's answer to the management problem: make the person responsible for the community a member of that community.
