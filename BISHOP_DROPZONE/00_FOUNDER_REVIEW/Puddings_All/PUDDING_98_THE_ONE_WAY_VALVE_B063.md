# Pudding #98 — The One-Way Valve

*Why Credits never cash out to dollars — and why that is the most important design decision in the entire system.*

---

On every other platform with an internal currency, there is a cash-out button. Twitch bits can be converted to dollars. Roblox Robux can be converted to dollars. Even airline miles — the original platform currency — can be redeemed for cash-equivalent purchases. The conversion creates a speculative layer: people accumulate the currency not because they want to use it, but because they want to convert it later at a favorable rate.

Liana Banyan's Credits, Marks, and Joules are one-way. Dollars convert to Credits at a 1:1 rate. But Credits never convert back to dollars. Marks are earned through effort and cannot be purchased or redeemed for cash. Joules are surplus currency generated when the cooperative produces more value than it consumes — and they cannot be cashed out either.

This is not a limitation. It is the architectural decision that makes everything else work.

When a currency can be converted to dollars, it becomes a speculative instrument. People hoard it. People trade it. People build secondary markets around it. The currency stops serving its original purpose — facilitating cooperative activity — and starts serving speculators who want to profit from exchange rate fluctuations. This is exactly what happened with every cryptocurrency that promised utility and delivered speculation.

The one-way valve prevents this entirely. Credits are useful on the platform — they pay for services, products, and subscriptions from other members. Marks represent demonstrated effort and unlock governance weight. Joules represent collective surplus and fund cooperative infrastructure. None of them are attractive to speculators because none of them can be converted to the thing speculators want: dollars.

This means the cooperative's internal economy is insulated from external financial markets. A stock market crash does not affect the value of Credits. A currency speculation bubble does not affect the purchasing power of Marks. The internal economy operates on its own terms, at its own pace, for the benefit of the members who participate in it.

The one-way valve also prevents a specific regulatory risk. A currency that can be converted to dollars looks like a money transmission instrument, which triggers state-by-state licensing requirements, federal oversight, and compliance costs that would be fatal to a $5/year cooperative. Credits that cannot be converted to dollars are internal service tokens — the same legal category as arcade tokens or gift cards — and do not trigger money transmission classification.

The valve is irrevocable. It is not a policy that can be changed by a board vote. It is a structural constraint baked into the system's architecture. Credits go in. Value comes out as goods, services, and governance participation. Dollars do not come back out. Ever.
