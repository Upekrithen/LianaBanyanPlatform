# Pudding #70: The Boaz Principle

*Leave something in the field for the person who comes after you.*

---

## At a Glance

In the Book of Ruth, Boaz told his workers to leave grain at the edges of the field — deliberately, on purpose — so that anyone who needed food could come and gather it. He did not create a charity. He did not build a food bank. He redesigned the harvest so that generosity was built into the system.

Liana Banyan runs on the same idea. Every system in the cooperative is designed to leave something in the field.

---

## More Info

### The Gleaning Model

"Gleaning" is the ancient practice of allowing people to gather leftover crops after a harvest. It is not charity — the gleaner does the work of gathering. It is not theft — the farmer leaves the grain intentionally. It is a structural arrangement where the system produces surplus and the surplus is accessible to anyone willing to show up and do the work.

The cooperative applies this model to digital economics:

**Surplus food recovery (Mission ONE):** Restaurants leave unsold meals "in the field" at deep discount. Members glean them for $2-4 per meal. The restaurant gets a small return on waste. The member gets fresh food. The system produces surplus. The surplus is accessible.

**Ghost World demand signals:** Members leave "I Want This" votes on products that do not exist yet. Those votes are free — the member spends nothing. But they create a field of demand that creators can harvest. The surplus is attention and intent, left in the field for the next person who knows how to make the thing.

**The Cue Card system:** A member creates a Cue Card and shares it. Every person who sees it might not buy. But the one who does — the gleaner — finds something they needed because someone else left it in the field.

### Why "Principle," Not "Program"

The Boaz Principle is not a feature. It is a design philosophy that runs through every system:

- **Cost+20% leaves the surplus with the creator.** The platform takes only what it needs. Everything else stays in the field — 83.3% — for the person who did the work.
- **Marks leave recognition in the field.** When you contribute to the cooperative, your Marks are permanent. Other members can see what you built. Your contribution is the grain left at the edge — inspiration and proof that showing up is rewarded.
- **Cooperative Purchasing leaves price savings in the field.** When 500 families buy rice together, the wholesale discount is not captured by the platform. It is passed through at Cost+20%. The savings are left in the field for every family.

## Full Detail

### Boaz as Economic Architecture

The traditional economy extracts surplus. A corporation's job is to capture as much value as possible from every transaction. When Amazon fulfills an order, the goal is to maximize Amazon's margin — the seller gets what is left.

The Boaz Principle inverts this. The cooperative's job is to leave as much value as possible in the field. The platform takes Cost+20% — the minimum viable harvest — and everything else belongs to the members.

This is not generosity. It is architecture. The cooperative does not choose to leave surplus in the field out of moral virtue. It is *structurally required* to do so by the Cost+20% bylaw. The system cannot extract more than it needs because the system is designed to prevent it.

### The Gleaner's Dignity

Boaz did not give Ruth a handout. He gave her the right to work his field. She still had to bend over, pick up the grain, carry it home, and thresh it. The work was hers. The opportunity was his to provide.

This matters for the cooperative. Mission ONE does not give away food. It sells surplus meals at deep discount. The member still pays. The transaction still goes through Cost+20%. The member is a customer, not a recipient. Their dignity is preserved because they are participating in commerce, not accepting charity.

The same applies to the five-dollar membership. It is not free. It is affordable. The difference matters. A free platform attracts people who do not value it. A five-dollar platform attracts people who value it enough to invest — even a small amount. The five dollars is the gleaner's work: showing up, reaching down, picking up what was left for you.

### The Compound Gleaning Effect

When one member gleans (buys surplus food, backs a Ghost World product, shares a Cue Card), their action creates more surplus for the next person:

- The restaurant that sells surplus meals tonight learns there is demand, so they list more meals tomorrow.
- The Ghost World vote that pushes a product over the threshold creates a product that hundreds of members can buy.
- The Cue Card shared today brings a new member tomorrow, whose $5 membership funds the infrastructure that serves everyone.

Gleaning is not zero-sum. It is generative. The more people glean, the more grain appears at the edge of the field. The system rewards participation by producing more surplus to participate in.

Boaz left grain in the field. The cooperative leaves opportunity. And the more people harvest it, the more there is.
