# Pudding #43: The Canister
## A Desktop Factory in a Cylinder

---

### At a Glance (~50 words)
The Canister System packs screw-press injection molding, hydraulic compression, and 3D print finishing into one cylindrical housing. It operates at 5,207 PSI with an 8-inch handle. COGS: $81.46 per unit at scale. Pledge price: $149. No competitor combines all three functions. The Canister doesn't exist anywhere else.

### More Info (~300 words)

Desktop manufacturing has a gap. 3D printers make prototypes beautifully but produce finished goods slowly and expensively. Injection molding makes finished goods cheaply but requires industrial equipment costing tens of thousands of dollars. There is no tool that bridges the two — something a maker can use at home to go from prototype to production part in the same device.

The Canister System fills that gap. It's a modular, cylindrical desktop manufacturing unit with three configurations:

**Gravity ($249)** — The entry point. Gravity-fed material flow for simple casting and compression. No mechanical pressure required. Good for resin, wax, and low-viscosity materials.

**Thermoplastic ($329)** — The S piston configuration. A screw-press mechanism that generates approximately 5,207 PSI through an 8-inch handle — enough to inject LDPE, PP, HDPE, and ABS into molds. This is real injection molding, not a toy. The pressure rivals small industrial machines at a fraction of the cost.

**Complete ($499)** — The full system. Combines the gravity and thermoplastic configurations with additional tooling for compression molding and finishing. A maker with the Complete Canister can prototype in resin, injection-mold in thermoplastic, and finish parts — all from one workstation.

The body is PA-12 SLS (selective laser sintered nylon). The gaskets are TPU 95A (flexible thermoplastic polyurethane). The bill of materials costs $81.46 per unit at a production run of 5,000. Every component is sourced from standard industrial suppliers — nothing proprietary, nothing fragile, nothing that can't be replaced.

The Canister System is patented. Ten provisional applications are filed with 2,097 formal claims. No prior art anticipates the full combination of screw-press injection, hydraulic compression, and 3D print finishing in a single desktop unit.

### Full Detail

**The S piston**

The heart of the Thermoplastic configuration is the S piston — a screw-press mechanism designed for hand-operated injection molding. The "S" describes the screw thread that translates rotational force from the 8-inch handle into linear injection pressure.

At ~5,207 PSI, the S piston can process:
- **LDPE (Low-Density Polyethylene)** — melting point ~110°C, injection temp ~160-200°C. Soft, flexible parts. Bags, squeeze bottles, caps.
- **PP (Polypropylene)** — melting point ~130°C, injection temp ~200-250°C. Semi-rigid parts. Living hinges, containers, automotive trim.
- **HDPE (High-Density Polyethylene)** — melting point ~130°C, injection temp ~200-260°C. Rigid, tough parts. Cutting boards, bottles, pipes.
- **ABS (Acrylonitrile Butadiene Styrene)** — melting point ~105°C, injection temp ~220-260°C. Impact-resistant parts. Phone cases, enclosures, toys.

Each material has different temperature and pressure requirements. The Canister includes a temperature reference guide so makers know exactly what settings to use for each thermoplastic. The pressure is fixed by the screw geometry — the operator just turns the handle.

**What it makes**

Desktop injection molding at this pressure and scale produces parts that hobbyists and small manufacturers currently make on much more expensive equipment:

- Board game tokens and miniatures
- Tabletop gaming terrain
- Cosplay components
- Phone cases and small enclosures
- Keychains and promotional items
- Small mechanical parts and prototypes
- Educational models
- Replacement parts for broken household items

At 50+ units, injection molding beats 3D printing on cost-per-part. A 3D-printed miniature might cost $2-5 in filament and 2-4 hours of print time. The same miniature injection-molded in PP costs $0.10-0.30 in material and takes 30-60 seconds per shot. The economics cross over fast.

**The production pipeline**

The Canister System connects to the cooperative's manufacturing pipeline through Design Democracy and Pioneer Nodes:

1. **DESIGN** — A member creates a product design and submits it to Design Democracy for community endorsement.
2. **SLA (Prototype)** — If endorsed, a Pioneer Node with SLA capability produces a high-detail prototype.
3. **VALIDATE** — The prototype is reviewed against the design spec. Fit, function, and quality are verified.
4. **SLS (Production Body)** — For Canister System components, SLS in PA-12 produces the structural body.
5. **INJECT** — For products made WITH the Canister, the Thermoplastic configuration injection-molds the finished parts.
6. **SHIP** — Completed products ship to members who ordered them.

A member with their own Canister can skip the Pioneer Node pipeline entirely for personal use. They design, mold, and finish their own parts at home. The pipeline exists for members who want community-endorsed products manufactured at scale.

**Prior art and patents**

The Canister System's patent position is strong because no prior art anticipates the full combination. Individual elements exist separately:

- Desktop injection molders exist (Galomb Model-B100, Holipress, LNS Technologies)
- Screw-press mechanisms exist in industrial injection molding
- Modular tool systems exist in workshop equipment
- SLS-printed housings exist in prototype manufacturing

But no product combines screw-press injection molding, hydraulic compression, and 3D print finishing in a single cylindrical desktop unit designed for consumer-level operation. The combination is the innovation, and the claims require the full combination — individual elements are not claimed in isolation.

**The Kickstarter path**

The campaign serves two purposes beyond funding. First, it validates demand — not through Design Democracy (which is internal to the cooperative) but through the broader maker market. If 400+ backers pledge for a desktop injection molder at $149, the market signal is clear.

Second, it introduces Liana Banyan's cooperative model to the maker community. Every backer who receives a Canister System also receives an invitation to the cooperative. A maker who uses the Canister for personal projects might also become a Pioneer Node, a Design Democracy participant, or a member who uses other cooperative services. The Kickstarter is a cold-start pathway for the manufacturing vertical.

**Why this matters**

Desktop manufacturing is stuck in a prototype loop. 3D printers are everywhere, but they make things slowly, one at a time, in materials that often aren't suitable for finished goods. The gap between "I printed a prototype" and "I'm producing units" requires industrial equipment that costs $15,000-100,000.

The Canister System closes that gap at $149-499. A maker who prints a prototype on their FDM printer can injection-mold production copies on their Canister the same week. The transition from prototype to production happens on a desktop, not in a factory.

For the cooperative, this means Design Democracy proposals can move from "endorsed idea" to "physical product" faster and cheaper than any other path. For the maker community, it means the economic floor for manufacturing drops from tens of thousands of dollars to a few hundred. For the economy, it means more people can make things — which is the entire point.
