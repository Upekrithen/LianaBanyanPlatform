# Pudding #84 — The Coalition

*When small businesses stop competing and start combining their purchasing power.*

---

A single coffee shop buys 50 pounds of beans a month. The distributor charges full price. A coalition of twelve coffee shops buys 600 pounds a month. The distributor offers a volume discount. The savings go back to the shops, not to a middleman.

This is not a new idea. Group purchasing organizations — GPOs — have operated in healthcare, hospitality, and food service for decades. What is new is making the mechanism available to any collection of small businesses on a cooperative platform for $5 per year per member.

Liana Banyan's Coalition system lets storefronts form alliances. A coalition is not a franchise. There is no central authority dictating prices, branding, or operations. It is a voluntary association of independent businesses that pool specific purchasing needs to achieve scale advantages none of them could reach alone.

The interface shows storefront cards, not spreadsheets. A coalition looks like what it is — a network of real businesses with real products. Discount tiers appear in plain language: "At 500 units/month, supplier offers 12% discount. At 1,000 units/month, 18%." Practical outcomes before abstract rules.

Cross-promotion is built into the structure. When twelve coffee shops form a coalition, each shop's customers see the other eleven. This is mutual amplification — not paid advertising, not algorithmic recommendation, but a direct consequence of cooperation. The purchasing pipeline shows pending confirmations and bottlenecks in a single stream, so no coalition member is surprised by a delayed order or a missed threshold.

The platform takes Cost+20% on transactions that flow through the system. On a $500 coalition purchase, the platform's share is $83.33. The creator — in this case the coalition coordinator — keeps $416.67. The same transparent math that applies everywhere on the platform.

A cooperative is not a platform that connects buyers to sellers. A cooperative is a structure where the participants own the mechanism that connects them. Coalitions make that structure tangible at the local level: twelve shops, one purchase order, shared savings, no extraction.
