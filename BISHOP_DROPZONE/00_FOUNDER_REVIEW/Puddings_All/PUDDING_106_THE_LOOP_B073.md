## The Pudding

What if your marketing wrote itself?

Not in the AI-generated-content sense. Not chatbots cranking out blog posts nobody reads. Something stranger and more honest: a system where the *data from distributing content* becomes the *next piece of content to distribute*.

Liana Banyan runs three content series at the same time across six social media channels. Blood, Sweat, and Tears — hourly micro-posts from academic papers, telling the story of building a cooperative with four AI agents. Spoonfuls — bite-sized insights from 105 accessible articles, each linking to the full version. Skipping Stones — section-level entry points into deep papers, each on a physical card with a QR code.

Every single post generates a data point. Which channel. Which time of day. Which topic. Which audience responded. After 30 days, that is 720 data points — enough to answer real questions. Does financial content perform better on LinkedIn or Twitter? Do innovation topics land at 8am or 6pm? Does the founder's personal narrative outperform the technical architecture?

Here is where it gets interesting: the answers to those questions become the next chapter of the series. Chapter 4 of Blood, Sweat, and Tears opens with whatever the data from Chapter 3 revealed. The distribution system writes its own next chapter.

The Concurrent Distribution Grid — Innovation #2141, one of 182 Crown Jewels — schedules ~24 posts per day across all channels with no overlap. Each post cross-references content from another series on another channel. The Viewing Schedule is public. Anyone can see what is on where and when. Bring Popcorn.

And the ten-category Spice Rack (#2142) tags every piece of content by business skill: Salt for operations, Garlic for finance, Cinnamon for design, Paprika for leadership, and six more. Followers filter by spice. A finance person follows the Garlic. A designer follows the Cinnamon. The content finds its audience through taxonomy, not algorithms.

The result: 550 Spoonfuls, 142 BST episodes, and ~300 Skipping Stones — over 1,000 pieces of distributable content extracted from existing material. Zero new writing required to fill ~50 days of continuous multi-channel posting. The archive was already there. The Loop just woke it up.
