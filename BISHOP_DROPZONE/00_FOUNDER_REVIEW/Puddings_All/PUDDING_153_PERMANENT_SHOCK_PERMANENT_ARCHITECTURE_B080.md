## BODY

Anton Korinek said the quiet part on April 3. The safety nets we have — unemployment insurance, retraining programs, Social Security, the whole post-war scaffolding of shock absorbers — were built for a world where shocks pass. Layoffs end. Industries restructure. Workers retrain. Employment returns. The shocks were transitory, so the cushions were transitory. That design made sense for a half-century.

What Korinek named is that it may not make sense anymore. Some economists are now describing what they are watching as a **permanent** change in the labor market. Not a restructuring. Not a cycle. A durable shift in which tasks that used to sustain entry-level careers are being absorbed by systems that do not age, do not tire, do not need salaries, and do not graduate to senior roles. If that read is correct, every single assumption inside the transitory-shock framework breaks.

Korinek is leaving the University of Virginia to work at Anthropic. That is not an aside. That is someone who spent a career studying how technology reshapes income distributions deciding to go work inside the company building the technology. Academics don't usually do that without the math moving first.

Walk through what "transitory" assumes, piece by piece, and watch the failures add up.

**Unemployment insurance assumes the next job is coming.** The benefit is short because the expectation is that the worker bridges to re-employment. If the next job is not coming — or is coming at a meaningfully lower wage, or in a form that doesn't qualify — the bridge becomes a cliff.

**Retraining assumes the new skills will transfer.** Workforce-development programs were built on the premise that displaced workers could learn the next thing and step into it. If the next thing is also getting displaced, or the skill window closes faster than the training takes, retraining becomes a treadmill.

**Social Security assumes working years, retirement years, finite transition.** The contribution model is built on a full working life. If working life gets interrupted in its first decade, the contribution curve never builds.

**Wage insurance assumes employer stability.** If employment itself is what's becoming unstable, promises attached to an employer lose their collateral.

Every one of these assumes shocks pass.

If Korinek is right — and the NYT piece is the first major-paper acknowledgment that the economics profession is taking that question seriously — then what the moment calls for is not a bigger transitory shock absorber. It is a permanent one. And not permanent in the sense of a bigger welfare state. Permanent in the sense of an asset the worker owns that does not depreciate when the employer changes, the industry changes, or the shock never ends.

Liana Banyan's currency architecture was designed for the math Korinek is describing. Let me say what the parts do.

**Joules.** Joules are the surplus currency. When a member contributes surplus — excess produce, excess capacity, excess effort beyond what was needed to break even on a transaction — the surplus is recorded as a Joule. The Joule does not expire. We sometimes call it the forever stamp. Once the contribution is real, the record is real, and the record persists. A member who contributed surplus in year one still owns the Joule record in year fifteen. There is no employer who can take it back. There is no market that can inflate it away. There is no policy change that can expire it.

**Marks.** Marks are the effort-differential currency. Participate, earn Marks. Walk away from the platform for five years, come back, Marks still exist. Marks track contribution. The differential piece matters: Marks reflect the difference between what a task required and what the member put in. Going above the ask is recorded. That record compounds over time.

**Credits.** Credits are the ground-level accounting currency, pegged one-to-one with dollars, used for day-to-day transactions. They move like cash moves, but they are one-way from the cooperative side — they cannot be cashed out back to fiat, which is what keeps the system from becoming a speculative token. Credits are the plumbing. Joules and Marks are the architecture.

**Contribution history.** All three currencies plus the member's actual participation trail — guilds joined, tribes seeded, projects launched, transactions completed, stamps earned — aggregate into contribution history. That history is addressable. That history is portable across initiatives. That history is the floor a member stands on.

Here is the frame shift. The transitory-shock safety net says: *if the employer fails, the state helps you bridge to the next employer.* The permanent-shock architecture says: *the record of your contribution is the asset, and no employer ever owned it in the first place.*

That is not UBI. Universal Basic Income says the government gives you money, unconditionally, to survive. UBI is a safety net at larger scale. It is also vulnerable to exactly the political pressures that made transitory-shock nets fragile.

It is not wage insurance. Wage insurance says the employer or the state guarantees your income floor. That only works if employers and employer-states stay solvent and willing.

It is a **member-owned economic operating system** in which participation history is the asset. The worker's leverage is not a job and not a benefit. The worker's leverage is their own accumulated contribution, held in a cooperative structure the worker partly owns.

The reason this matters for Korinek's framing is that permanent change requires permanence in the response. You cannot paper a permanent shock with transitory tools. The transitory tools depreciate out from under the worker. You need an asset that does not depreciate with the shock. Joules are designed to not depreciate. Marks are designed to compound. Contribution history is designed to persist.

We built this before anyone in mainstream economics was using the word "permanent" to describe the shock. That was luck of timing as much as anything. The real work was reading what the workers were saying about what durable meant to them. Durable means not disappearing when the employer disappears. Durable means compounding, not resetting. Durable means your name stays on the door.

Korinek said it on April 3. The safety nets were built for shocks that pass. This one might not pass.

A permanent shock needs permanent architecture. Joules don't expire. Marks compound. Contribution history is the floor.

The proof is in the Pudding.
