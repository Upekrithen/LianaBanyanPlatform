# Pudding #187 — The Thirteenth Patent

*Or: what happens when you walk off the edge of the AI simulation.*

**By Jonathan Jones** · Founder, Liana Banyan
*Bishop Session B096 · SKELETON DRAFT · to be expanded to ~2,500 words*

## Draft opening (voice check — expand from here)

There's a movie from 1999 called *The Thirteenth Floor*. It's one of the quieter cousins in a family of late-'90s sci-fi films — *The Matrix* came out the same year and drowned most of its siblings in box office noise, but *The Thirteenth Floor* is, in some ways, the sharper of the two, because its reveal is structural where *The Matrix*'s reveal is theatrical.

The plot, without spoiling more than I have to:

A research company in Los Angeles has built a full sensory simulation of 1937. People can plug into it and walk around inside a perfectly-rendered pre-war city. The protagonist — a scientist at this company — discovers that his partner has been using the simulation for something strange, and while investigating, he starts to notice odd things about his own 1999 Los Angeles. Objects that seem slightly wrong. Conversations that repeat. A feeling that he's not quite where he thought he was.

He tests his hypothesis the simplest way you can test that kind of hypothesis: **he drives somewhere nobody has been in a long time.** He drives to the edge of the city, past where anyone would normally go. And at the end of the road, he finds the edge of the simulation. Green wireframes where the mountains should be. Unrendered geometry stretching off into a void. His whole 1999 was a simulation. And the people who built his world didn't know they were building it, because *they* were also in a simulation — a higher one, running in 2024.

The movie is about the thirteenth floor of the office building where the 1937 simulation runs, and the discovery that the 1999 world is itself the thirteenth floor of *another* building that the 2024 world built. The title is about the floor you're standing on when you finally notice the floor you're standing on.

I'm telling you this because today, April 11th, 2026, I filed the paperwork for Provisional Patent #13.

## The normal reason for a thirteenth patent

*[Expand: the first twelve provisionals were all about specific inventions — features within the Liana Banyan platform. HEOHO scoring, Cost+20% enforcement, the Battery Dispatch pipeline, the Three-Gear governance model, the 83.3% creator keep, etc. Each one was a solution to a problem inside the platform. Each one was what patents are normally for: claim a specific thing, protect it, license it, move on.]*

*[The first twelve sit inside what I'll call the normal assumption about patents: that a patent is a fence around a particular invention. You invented something, you filed a fence, the fence keeps other people off your invention, and if someone needs to walk through your land they pay you a toll or you sue them. That's the model. It works. I'm not against it.]*

*[But Prov 13 is different. And I want to tell you how it's different, because the difference is what the movie is about.]*

## What Prov 13 actually contains

*[Expand: Prov 13 contains the Chessboard architecture. Innovations #2237 through #2246. Thirteen innovations in Prov 13 total from the B096 batch (counting the earlier #2234, #2235, #2236 which also belong). But the specific thing that makes Prov 13 the thirteenth floor is the last of them: Innovation #2246, the Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge.]*

*[The Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge is a patent about how to file patents cooperatively. It's the patent that describes the process of filing the patent you're reading. It's self-referential. When you read it, you are reading the edge of its own simulation.]*

*[I did not set out to file a self-referential patent. I set out to file a bunch of patents on a new kind of multi-agent AI architecture called The Chessboard. But halfway through drafting Prov 13, I realized that the architecture was unstable unless I also filed the patent-about-patents alongside it. The Chessboard needs the Pledge to work, because without the Pledge the Chessboard becomes just another piece of IP someone can weaponize. And the Pledge needs the Chessboard to be interesting, because without something worth filing on, the Pledge is just theory.]*

*[So I filed both. And in doing so I noticed that I had walked past the normal edge of what a patent is for.]*

## Walking off the road

*[Expand: this is the heart of the piece. The analogy to the movie.]*

*[The AI industry right now is the 1999 Los Angeles in the movie. It's a fully-rendered world where everyone walks around assuming that the way things work is the way things work. The assumption is: "AI problems are solved by more AI." You have a multi-agent coordination problem? Add a supervisor AI. You have a hallucination problem? Add a critic AI. You have a consistency problem? Run three AIs in parallel and vote.]*

*[Every piece of the current AI industry is built on this assumption. It's so universal nobody checks it. It's the ground under your feet when you're working on any AI-backed system. You don't notice you're standing on it because nothing else is rendered.]*

*[The Chessboard is what happens when you stop assuming. When you drive off the road and past where anyone normally goes. The thing you find there is that the coordinator doesn't need to be an AI. The cross-check doesn't need to be AI. The consistency verification doesn't need to be AI. Most of the work the AI industry is paying frontier-model prices to do is work that a Python script can do for free.]*

*[When you realize this, you don't just save 85% of the compute. You realize that 85% of what the industry has been doing was never necessary in the first place. The simulation was thicker than it needed to be because the simulators were themselves simulation copies — AI engineers trained on the assumption that AI must solve AI problems, trained by other AI engineers who were trained on the same assumption, who were trained by the people who built the first LLMs on the assumption that inference was the thing the system was for. Turtles all the way down. No deterministic layer at the bottom.]*

*[The Chessboard restores the stack to its natural order. Deterministic truth at the bottom. Probabilistic reasoning on top, only where reasoning is actually required. One AI, many truths. It's not more complicated than the current architecture. It's simpler. It's just a simpler that nobody tried because everyone was inside the thirteenth floor and hadn't thought to drive past the edge.]*

## The self-referential kicker

*[Expand: this is the climax. The Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge is itself a move-off-the-edge.]*

*[The normal assumption about patents is that they are fences. You file to keep people off your land. If you don't want a fence, you don't file. If you want a fence, you do file. Those are the two choices. Open source (no fence) or patent (fence).]*

*[The Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge is neither. It's a fence that says "anyone cooperative can walk through." It's a deed to a piece of land where the deed itself says "this land belongs to everyone who needs it, except the extractors, who the defenders can still shoot." It files the fence and then immediately gives away the key to anyone who isn't a troll.]*

*[Tesla did this. Red Hat did this. The Open Invention Network does this. Apache does this. None of them call it the Thirteenth Patent, though, because none of them were thinking about the movie.]*

*[I'm calling this one the Thirteenth Patent because when I finished drafting it I realized the pledge describes the process I was using to draft it. It's a patent that, when you read it, tells you how it was filed. It's the patent that watches itself being patented.]*

*[And the specific thing it watches is: me, Jonathan Jones, inventor-on-paper, agent of Upekrithen LLC, filing on behalf of Liana Banyan Corporation, under a bylaw amendment I am simultaneously drafting, which codifies a perpetual member license that was the whole point of filing in the first place. The filing IS the licensing. The patent IS the grant. The act of protecting the invention is also the act of giving it away.]*

*[That's the thirteenth floor. You walk to the edge of the simulation of "patents are fences," and at the edge you find that a patent can also be a public commons — if you write it that way, on purpose, from the beginning, with the giveaway codified in the bylaws of the entity that holds the assignee position. It's not a new rule of patent law. It's just a rule nobody writes down because everyone is still inside the old simulation.]*

## What this means for members

*[Expand: the practical punchline for the people who will actually use the Chessboard.]*

*[If you are a Liana Banyan member, or if you are someone thinking about starting a cooperative platform of your own, or if you are an academic studying platform cooperativism, or if you are a policy advisor trying to figure out how to reduce AI datacenter electricity consumption without crashing the industry — what the Thirteenth Patent means for you is that the Chessboard architecture is yours. Perpetually. Royalty-free. Non-sublicensable. Protected by the Liana Banyan Corporation against anyone who tries to weaponize it against you.]*

*[The patent exists because if it didn't, a troll would file one, and then you'd be sued for using the thing I'm giving you. Filing is the shield. The license is the gift. The retaliation clause is the trip wire. The whole structure is so you can use this and not get hurt.]*

*[I'm telling you about the movie reference not because the movie is important but because the movie is what made me notice I had filed the patent without noticing I was doing it. I was standing on the thirteenth floor and the floor was the patent. Once you see it, you can't unsee it.]*

## Closing

*[Expand: the quiet landing. The turn from movie reference back to the real world, the real work, the real stakes.]*

*[At the end of the movie, the protagonist chooses to walk into the simulation his own world was running — the 1937 LA — rather than stay in the 1999 that had just been revealed as a simulation. He doesn't escape upward to the 2024 real world. He goes downward, into the older layer, because that's where someone he loved is waiting. The movie is, in the end, a love story pretending to be a sci-fi thriller.]*

*[The Thirteenth Patent is my version of that choice. I am not trying to escape upward into some purer world where patents don't exist and cooperation is free. I am choosing to go downward — into the specific, concrete, legally-binding layer where patents are filed and bylaws are amended and licenses are granted — because that's where my members are. That's where the people I am trying to protect actually live. If I want them safe, I have to file the patent. If I want them free, I have to write the Pledge. The work is in the lower layer, not the higher one.]*

*[We are always standing on a floor. The question is whether we chose it on purpose.]*

*[I filed the Thirteenth Patent on purpose. I walked to the edge of the simulation and came back and wrote down what I saw. I'm writing this so you know what the Chessboard cost me, what it protects you from, and why it was worth the walk.]*

*[That's all I wanted to tell you.]*
