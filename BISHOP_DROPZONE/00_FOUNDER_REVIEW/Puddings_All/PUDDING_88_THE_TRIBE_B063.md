# Pudding #88 — The Tribe

*The personal side of belonging — because not everything is about work.*

---

Most platforms organize people by what they do. LinkedIn has industry groups. Slack has workspace channels. Discord has servers built around games, projects, or professional communities. The organizing principle is function: what do you produce, what do you consume, what is your role?

Liana Banyan splits this into two structures. Guilds are professional — they organize around skills, trades, and crafts. A woodworker joins the Woodworking Guild. A photographer joins the Photography Guild. You can belong to many Guilds, and your Guild memberships signal what you can do.

Tribes are personal. They organize around who you are, where you live, and what you care about outside of work. A neighborhood group. A homeschool collective. A veterans' circle. A gardening community. A faith-based fellowship. You can belong to many Tribes, and your Tribe memberships signal where you belong.

The distinction matters because platforms that collapse personal and professional identity into one profile create perverse incentives. Your neighborhood group should not be optimized for engagement metrics. Your veterans' circle should not be monetized through targeted advertising. Your faith community should not be algorithmically ranked by activity scores.

Tribes on Liana Banyan have different design DNA than Guilds. The directory defaults to a map view — neighborhood as the primary filter — because Tribes are about proximity and shared place, not shared function. The cards are warmer and softer than Guild cards. The interface signals: this is the personal space, not the professional one.

A member might belong to one Guild (Carpentry) and three Tribes (Oak Street Neighbors, Fort Hood Veterans, Saturday Morning Gardeners). The Guild is where they find work and build reputation. The Tribes are where they find people and build relationships. Both exist on the same platform, under the same $5/year membership, but they serve fundamentally different human needs.

Family-type Tribes connect to the Family Table — the platform's system for household coordination, shared calendars, fund cards, and child-safe interfaces. A Tribe can become a family's digital infrastructure without becoming a social network that surveils them.

The platform does not rank Tribes by size, activity, or growth rate. There is no leaderboard. A Tribe of six neighbors who share a lawnmower is as valid as a Tribe of six hundred veterans who coordinate job placement. The structure serves the members, not the metrics.
