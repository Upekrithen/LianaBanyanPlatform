# Pudding #45: The Harvest and the Share
## AI Productivity Is Rising. Who Gets the Crop?

---

### At a Glance (~50 words)

### More Info (~300 words)

The AI productivity story has two chapters. Chapter one is the good news: organizations are finally seeing returns on AI investment. Productivity is up. Output per worker is climbing. The tools work.

Chapter two is the problem: the gains are concentrated. A small cohort of "power users" captures most of the productivity benefit. Workers aged 22-25 in AI-exposed occupations are experiencing significant employment declines. The technology raises the ceiling for some and lowers the floor for others.

This is not a new pattern. Every wave of automation in history has increased total output while displacing specific groups of workers. What's different about AI is the speed and the breadth — it affects knowledge work, creative work, and service work simultaneously, not just manufacturing.

Liana Banyan's architecture is a structural response to the distribution problem. Three mechanisms ensure that AI-enabled productivity gains flow to workers and creators, not to platform equity holders:

**Mechanism 1: Cost+20% caps extraction.** The platform's margin is fixed at 20% of cost. When AI makes production more efficient (lower cost), the saving passes through to the creator — the platform cannot capture the efficiency gain by raising its take rate.

**Mechanism 2: 83.3% flows to the worker.** Regardless of how much AI assistance was used in creating, producing, or delivering a good or service, the human creator keeps 83.3% of the transaction value. The AI is a tool that amplifies the creator's output. The creator keeps the amplified output's value.

**Mechanism 3: Marks recognize effort that AI doesn't replace.** Community building, governance participation, mentoring, quality review — these human contributions earn Marks even though they generate no direct transaction. AI cannot earn Marks. Only humans can. This creates an economic channel that is structurally immune to AI displacement.

### Full Detail

**The Brynjolfsson paradox**

Erik Brynjolfsson's research documents a genuine paradox. In his February 2026 Fortune analysis, he shows that U.S. productivity growth has nearly doubled — from roughly 1.4% average to approximately 2.7% — as organizations begin harvesting their AI investments. The tools are working. The economy is more productive.

But his November 2025 NBER paper ("Canaries in the Coal Mine") documents the other side: a significant decline in employment for workers aged 22-25 in occupations most exposed to AI. The youngest, most vulnerable cohort is losing ground precisely during the harvest phase.

This isn't a contradiction. It's the predictable result of an economic system where productivity gains flow to capital rather than labor. When AI makes a worker 30% more productive, the employer can serve the same demand with 23% fewer workers — and pocket the difference. The productivity gain is real. The distribution is the problem.

Daron Acemoglu's task-based framework puts numbers on this: he estimates AI may raise total factor productivity by less than 0.66% over 10 years if it only automates easily verifiable tasks rather than augmenting complex human judgment. The modest aggregate gain combined with significant displacement of specific worker cohorts is what he calls "meh automation" — not transformative for the economy, but destructive for the people it replaces.

**How Cost+20% solves the distribution problem**

In a standard platform, the operator captures efficiency gains. If AI reduces the cost of content moderation by 50%, the platform pockets the saving — neither the creator nor the user benefits. If AI reduces the cost of product listing management by 40%, the marketplace captures that margin.

In Liana Banyan, Cost+20% means the platform's margin is calculated on actual cost. When AI reduces actual cost, the 20% margin shrinks in absolute terms. A service that cost $100 to deliver (yielding a $20 platform margin) might cost $60 after AI optimization — yielding a $12 platform margin. The $40 in savings flows to the creator and consumer as lower prices, not to the platform as higher profits.

This is not altruism. It's arithmetic. Cost+20% is a formula, not a policy. The formula automatically routes efficiency gains downstream because the platform's percentage is fixed and the base it's calculated on just got smaller.

**How 83.3% keeps the creator whole**

When a creator uses AI tools to produce more content, better products, or faster services, they keep 83.3% of the increased transaction value. The AI made them more productive. They capture the productivity gain. The platform's 16.7% stays proportional.

Compare this to the standard creator economy model: a platform charges 20-45% in fees. When AI makes the creator more productive, the platform captures 20-45% of the increased output. The creator works smarter but the platform earns more from their smartness. The incentive is for the platform to push AI tools that increase volume (and therefore platform revenue) regardless of whether the creator benefits.

At 83.3%, the creator's incentive and the cooperative's incentive are aligned. More AI-enabled productivity benefits the creator directly. The cooperative benefits proportionally through the 20% margin on a growing transaction base, not by capturing a larger share of each transaction.

**How Marks protect non-automatable work**

The most vulnerable workers in the AI economy are those doing tasks that AI can automate. The most durable workers are those doing tasks that require human judgment, relationships, trust, and physical presence.

Marks are designed to recognize exactly those contributions. Community building. Governance participation. Mentoring new members. Reviewing product quality. Resolving disputes. Coordinating neighborhood coalitions. Staffing a Pioneer Node. These activities require human presence and judgment in ways that AI cannot replicate.

Marks cannot be earned by AI agents. Only human members earn Marks. This is a constitutional rule, not a technical limitation. The cooperative could, in theory, award Marks to AI for computational contributions. It explicitly does not, because Marks exist to value human effort that markets and machines undercount.

A member who spends 10 hours per week on community governance earns Marks that give weight to their Backer Election votes, build toward governance tier progression, and signal their contribution history to the cooperative. None of that can be displaced by AI because the system is designed to recognize the irreplaceably human parts of cooperative life.

**The $5/year access point**

The cohort most damaged by AI displacement — workers aged 22-25 — is also the cohort least able to afford expensive platform memberships, professional tools, or startup costs. At $5/year, Liana Banyan's barrier to entry is effectively zero.

A 23-year-old displaced from an AI-exposed content moderation job can join the cooperative for less than the cost of a coffee, access all 35 production systems, participate in governance, earn Marks through community contribution, and begin building economic activity through the platform — without needing capital, credentials, or connections.

The $5 price isn't charity. It's strategy. The cooperative needs members. Young workers need economic infrastructure. The pricing removes the barrier between the two. And once inside, every AI-enabled tool on the platform amplifies the member's capacity rather than replacing their role — because the 83.3% keep rate and Marks system ensure that the human captures the gain.

**The harvest is real. The share is the design problem.**

Brynjolfsson is right that the harvest has begun. Acemoglu is right that the distribution is failing. Both identify the problem accurately. Neither provides an institutional solution — they offer analysis and policy recommendations.

Liana Banyan is the institutional solution. Not the only possible one. Not the final one. But a running one — a system where Cost+20% routes efficiency gains downstream, 83.3% keeps creators whole, and Marks protect the irreplaceably human parts of economic life from being automated into irrelevance.

The productivity harvest is here. The question was always who gets to eat.
