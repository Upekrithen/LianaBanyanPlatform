# Pudding #188 — My Strawberries Can Only Be So Fresh

*Or: why a father of eight gave away 80% of a patent portfolio.*

**By Jonathan Jones** · Founder, Liana Banyan
*Bishop Session B100 · FULL DRAFT · ~2,200 words*

---

People are going to ask why I gave away 80% of the intellectual property.

I want to answer the question before it gets asked, because the answer is simpler than the question implies, and the question — if I let it float unanswered — will attract the wrong kind of speculation. The wrong kind of speculation is: he must be stupid, or he must be playing a longer game, or he must not understand what he has. I am none of those things. I understand exactly what I have. I am giving away 80% of it because keeping it would make me less effective, and I'd rather be capable and effective than rich.

Let me tell you what I mean.

## The math

Under the IP Load Balancing framework (Innovation #2239), every dollar that comes into the cooperative through patent licensing flows through a constitutional allocation:

- **60%** goes to Patent Buckets — funds that flow directly to cooperative members
- **20%** goes to the originating creator (that's me, for the patents I filed)
- **10%** goes to a Global Sponsor Pool
- **10%** goes to Individual Patent Pedestals

I keep 20%. The cooperative keeps 60%. The rest funds the ecosystem. This is not a pledge I can revoke. It's codified in the corporation's operating agreement. Changing it would require dissolving the corporation.

So when someone says "you gave away 80%," they're right. And when they say "why?" — here is why.

## Strawberries

I grow strawberries. Not commercially — in the backyard, for the family. Eight kids. The strawberries come in June, and when they come in, they come in all at once. You get maybe two weeks of peak strawberries, and then the season shifts and they're gone until next year.

During those two weeks, I have more strawberries than I can eat. Way more. I could eat strawberries for breakfast, lunch, and dinner, and I'd still have strawberries left over. The kids eat them. The neighbors get some. Some go into jam. Some go into the freezer. And some — because strawberries are strawberries — go soft on the counter and end up in the compost.

Here is the thing about strawberries that most people who have never grown them don't understand: **the value of a strawberry is not the strawberry. The value of a strawberry is how fresh it is when someone eats it.** A strawberry picked thirty seconds ago and eaten standing in the garden is worth ten strawberries sitting in a plastic clamshell in a grocery store refrigerator. The strawberry itself is the same plant, the same genetics, the same sun. But the fresh one is transcendently good, and the three-day-old one is merely acceptable.

Now: what happens if I try to keep all the strawberries? I can't eat them fast enough. They go soft. They rot. The value I was trying to protect disappears because the thing I was protecting has a freshness window, and I exceeded it.

**My strawberries can only be so fresh.**

Money works the same way for me. I have eight kids. I live on $30,000 a year. I've invested $5,000 a year into this platform for nine years. If the patent portfolio generates significant licensing revenue — and the ROM-First architecture alone projects ~$8.7 billion per year in industry savings at the 2024 baseline, of which Liana Banyan's licensing take would be a fraction — then 20% of that fraction is still more money than I know what to do with.

What would I do with 100%? Buy a bigger house? I have a house. Buy nicer cars? I drive what gets the job done. Send the kids to private school? They're doing fine. Travel? I've been to 47 states and a dozen countries on a military salary.

The difference between 20% of a large number and 100% of a large number is not the difference between comfortable and uncomfortable. It's the difference between comfortable and absurd. And absurdity is a tax on capability. The more you have beyond what you need, the more time you spend managing, protecting, and worrying about the excess. That time comes from somewhere. It comes from the work.

## The capability argument

Here is the part that sounds counterintuitive until you've run sixteen businesses: **giving away 80% makes me more effective, not less.**

When I walk into a room with a potential patron, a researcher, a policy advisor, or a Crown letter recipient, and I can say — truthfully, verifiably, codified in the operating agreement — that I keep 20% and the cooperative keeps 60%, the conversation changes. I am no longer a founder asking for something. I am a founder who has already given away most of what he built. That is a different kind of credibility. It is not purchased with money. It is purchased with structure.

When Trebor Scholz reads our case file and sees the 60/20/10/10 allocation, he does not see a startup founder pitching a vision. He sees a cooperative founder who has put the cooperative's interests ahead of his own in the only document that matters — the operating agreement. That changes whether he amplifies the work. It changes whether he co-authors the paper. It changes whether he sends a graduate student to investigate.

When Cory Doctorow reads about the anti-enshittification architecture and sees that the founder's take is 20% — locked, published, non-adjustable — he does not see the usual Silicon Valley game where the founder promises cooperation and then pivots to extraction at Series B. He sees a structure that can't pivot. That's the enshittification-proof architecture. Not the code. The operating agreement.

When a securities attorney reviews the Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge and sees that the perpetual member license is codified in bylaws — not in revocable policy — the attorney's opinion letter gets easier to write. The structural defense is stronger. The compliance cost is lower. The regulatory risk profile improves. All because the founder gave away 80%.

**Every dollar I give away makes the remaining 20% more likely to materialize.** The cooperative structure is what creates the licensing opportunity. The licensing opportunity is what creates the revenue. The revenue is what flows through the 60/20/10/10 allocation. My 20% exists because the other 80% exists. You can't separate them. The invention is the structure. The structure is the gift. The gift is the invention.

## What I learned from sixteen businesses

I've started sixteen businesses before this one. Some worked. Some didn't. The pattern I noticed across all of them is this: the businesses that worked were the ones where I was building something I would have built for free. And the businesses that failed were the ones where I was trying to capture value instead of create it.

This is not a moral lesson. It's an operational observation. When you're trying to capture value, you spend your time building fences, negotiating terms, protecting positions, watching competitors. When you're trying to create value, you spend your time building things. The second mode is faster, cheaper, more fun, and produces better outcomes.

The 60/20/10/10 allocation is my way of permanently locking myself into the second mode. I can't switch to value capture even if I wanted to, because the operating agreement won't let me. The structure removes the temptation, and removing the temptation frees up the cognitive bandwidth I'd otherwise spend worrying about whether I'm leaving money on the table.

I am leaving money on the table. On purpose. Because the table is in my garden and the money is strawberries and they're going to go soft anyway.

## The kids

Someone will ask: "But what about your eight kids? Don't you owe them more?"

I owe them a father who built something that works. I owe them a demonstration that you can be serious about cooperative economics without being naive about money. I owe them the lesson that keeping 20% of something real is better than keeping 100% of something imaginary.

And I owe them strawberries. Fresh ones. Picked thirty seconds ago, eaten standing in the garden, juice running down their chins. The best strawberries are the ones you share.

## The question I am answering

The question is not "why did you give away 80%?"

The question is "why would you keep 100%?"

Keeping 100% means no cooperative. No cooperative means no Living Laboratory. No Living Laboratory means no research partnerships. No research partnerships means no academic credibility. No academic credibility means no Crown letters. No Crown letters means no Scholz amplification. No Scholz amplification means no third-axis paper. No third-axis paper means no policy impact. No policy impact means no ROM-First adoption. No ROM-First adoption means no licensing revenue. No licensing revenue means 100% of nothing.

The 80% I "gave away" is the engine that makes the 20% possible. I didn't give it away. I invested it. In the only asset class I've ever found that reliably appreciates: other people's capability.
