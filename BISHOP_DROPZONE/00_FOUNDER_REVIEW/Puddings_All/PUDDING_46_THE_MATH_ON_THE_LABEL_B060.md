### More Info (~300 words)

The creator economy has a transparency problem. Not a small one. A fundamental one.

The Association of National Advertisers reports that only 51% of marketers have full clarity into what agencies pay creators. The other 49% are spending money on creator partnerships without knowing how much actually reaches the person making the content.

This opacity isn't accidental. It's the business model. When a platform stands between a buyer and a creator, the platform has every incentive to obscure its take. If the buyer knew the creator only received 55 cents of every dollar, they might pay the creator directly. If the creator knew the platform was taking 45%, they might leave. The fog protects the margin.

Liana Banyan eliminates the fog. The pricing model is one formula: Cost + 20%. The creator's product or service has a real cost (materials, labor, equipment, time). The cooperative adds 20% for operating expenses. That's the price. The creator receives the remaining 83.3% of the gross transaction value.

This is not a tier. There is no "basic plan" at 83.3% and a "premium plan" at 90%. There is no "introductory rate" that changes after the first year. There is no "processing fee" that reduces the effective rate. 83.3% is what the creator keeps. Period.

The number comes from the math: if the platform adds 20% to cost, then cost represents 83.3% of the total price (100/120 = 0.8333). This is arithmetic, not generosity. The cooperative cannot take more than 20% because Cost+20% is the pricing rule. The creator cannot receive less than 83.3% because that's what's left after 16.7%.

No negotiation. No tiers. No fine print. The math is on the label.

### Full Detail

**Why fee transparency matters more in 2026 than ever**

The creator economy is consolidating. Goldman Sachs projects $480 billion by 2027. Micro and nano creators now command 45.5% of influencer marketing spend. Creator marketing is no longer experimental — it's a permanent line item in global marketing budgets.

But the money flowing into the creator economy does not flow transparently. Three layers of opacity exist:

**Layer 1: Platform fees.** Every platform charges differently and discloses differently. YouTube takes approximately 45% of ad revenue but the creator's effective rate varies by content type, geography, and algorithmic favor. Patreon charges 5-12% in platform fees plus payment processing. Etsy stacks listing fees, transaction fees, offsite ad fees, and payment processing into a total that most sellers cannot calculate in their heads.

**Layer 2: Agency fees.** When a brand hires an agency to manage creator partnerships, the agency takes 15-30% before the creator sees anything. The ANA data showing only 51% of marketers know the creator's actual compensation means that in nearly half of all brand-creator deals, no one on the brand side knows how much the creator gets paid.

**Layer 3: Algorithmic variability.** Many platforms tie creator compensation to algorithmic metrics — views, engagement, watch time, click-through — that the creator cannot predict or control. TikTok's Creator Fund became notorious for paying fractions of a cent per view with no transparency into the calculation. A creator's income fluctuates not because their work quality changed, but because an algorithm changed.

Liana Banyan eliminates all three layers:
- **No variable platform fees.** Cost+20%. One formula. Every transaction.
- **No agency intermediation.** The cooperative connects creators directly to members. No third party takes a cut.
- **No algorithmic variability.** The creator's share is 83.3% of the transaction price. Not 83.3% of an algorithmically determined value. 83.3% of what the buyer actually paid.

**How Cost+20% is enforced**

Cost+20% is not a suggestion or a guideline. It's a pricing constraint built into the cooperative's transaction system. When a creator lists a product or service, the system calculates the price as: creator's stated cost × 1.20 = listed price.

The creator defines their cost. The system adds 20%. The buyer pays the listed price. The creator receives the cost portion (83.3% of listed). The cooperative receives the 20% portion (16.7% of listed).

No one can override this formula for a specific transaction. The cooperative cannot decide to take 25% from high-volume creators. A creator cannot offer the platform less than 20% to get preferential treatment. The formula is the same for a $5 transaction and a $50,000 transaction. Proportional, not progressive.

This means the cooperative's revenue scales exactly with transaction volume. More creators selling more products generates more 20% margins. The incentive is to grow the economy, not to squeeze individual creators. The cooperative makes more money when creators succeed, not when creators accept worse terms.

**What the number actually means for a creator**

Consider a creator selling handmade candles at a total cost of $12 per candle (materials + labor + equipment + overhead):
- Listed price: $12 × 1.20 = $14.40
- Creator receives: $12 (83.3% of $14.40)
- Cooperative receives: $2.40 (16.7% of $14.40)

On Etsy, the same candle faces:
- $0.20 listing fee
- 6.5% transaction fee = $0.94
- 3% + $0.25 payment processing = $0.68
- Optional 15% offsite advertising fee if applicable
- Total fees: $1.82 to $4.00+ depending on options
- Creator keeps: roughly 72-87% depending on which fees apply

The difference isn't just the percentage. It's the predictability. The Liana Banyan creator knows, before listing, exactly what they will keep from every sale. The Etsy seller cannot calculate their effective rate without a spreadsheet.

**Why the number can't change**

When a VC-backed platform offers attractive creator terms, those terms survive only until the next funding round demands higher revenue. Patreon started with generous economics and has progressively restructured pricing. Gumroad's fees have evolved through multiple models. Substack's 10% take rate is stable today but the company is VC-backed — the rate survives at the pleasure of the investors.

Liana Banyan's 83.3% is a constitutional feature of the cooperative. Changing it requires changing the Cost+20% formula, which requires a governance vote through Backer Elections, which requires member approval. The people who benefit from the 83.3% are the same people who would have to vote to reduce it. The ownership structure makes self-destructive fee increases nearly impossible because the owners and the affected parties are the same people.

This is the difference between a promise and a structure. A VC-backed platform promises good terms. A cooperative enforces them through architecture.

**The label**

Food products have nutrition labels. Financial products have fee disclosures. Creator economy platforms have nothing — just marketing copy that says "we put creators first" while obscuring the actual economics.

Liana Banyan's label reads:
- **Creator keeps: 83.3%**
- **Platform takes: 16.7% (Cost+20%)**
- **Advertising fee: $0**
- **Data sales revenue: $0**
- **Variable algorithmic adjustment: None**
- **Subject to change: Only by member governance vote**

The math is on the label. The label doesn't change.
