# Pudding #39: One Hundred Factories
## Why Pioneer Nodes Have a Hard Cap

---

### At a Glance (~50 words)
Liana Banyan limits its distributed manufacturing network to exactly 100 Pioneer Nodes. Not 101. Not "unlimited with quality controls." One hundred. The hard cap creates scarcity that drives quality, commitment, and a manufacturing culture where every node matters and every slot is earned.

---

### More Info (~300 words)

Platforms love unlimited scale. More sellers. More drivers. More nodes. The logic is simple: more supply means lower prices, more coverage, more revenue. It also means a race to the bottom where quality erodes, commitment vanishes, and individual participants become disposable.

Pioneer Nodes reject that logic entirely. The cooperative's distributed manufacturing network has a hard cap of 100 nodes. Each node is a registered manufacturing station — a member with verified equipment (SLA printers, FDM printers, CNC routers, laser cutters, injection molders, or desktop manufacturing stations) who can produce physical goods endorsed by the community through Design Democracy.

The hard cap does three things.

First, **it makes each slot valuable.** When there are only 100 manufacturing positions in the entire cooperative, holding one means something. You don't churn through Pioneer Nodes the way gig platforms churn through drivers. You earn your position through demonstrated capability, maintain it through consistent quality, and lose it only through sustained failure to meet standards.

Second, **it creates natural quality control.** With 100 nodes serving the entire membership, each node handles enough volume to develop real expertise. A node that produces 50 units a month builds skill that a node producing 2 units a month never will. The cap concentrates production enough to build craft.

Third, **it makes the manufacturing ladder meaningful.** The four levels — Kit, Bench, Shop, Factory — represent real progressions in capability and volume. A Pioneer Node at Bench level producing 30 units a month is genuinely different from a Kit-level member printing designs at home. The limited number of nodes means each level has economic substance, not just a badge.

### Full Detail

**The seven equipment types**

Each Pioneer Node registers with one or more of seven recognized equipment categories:

1. **SLA (Stereolithography)** — Resin-based 3D printing for high-detail prototypes and finished parts. Critical for the DESIGN → SLA → VALIDATE pipeline stage.
2. **FDM (Fused Deposition Modeling)** — Filament-based 3D printing for functional prototypes and larger structural parts.
3. **Resin** — Other resin-based processes (DLP, MSLA) beyond SLA. High detail, varied material properties.
4. **CNC** — Computer numerical control milling and routing for subtractive manufacturing. Wood, metal, plastics.
5. **Laser** — Laser cutting and engraving for flat-stock materials, signage, and precision components.
6. **Injection** — Desktop or benchtop injection molding. The Canister System's S piston configuration operates here.
7. **Desktop** — General desktop manufacturing stations. Multi-tool setups, finishing stations, assembly workbenches.

A single Pioneer Node can register multiple equipment types. A member with an SLA printer, a laser cutter, and a Canister System registers three capabilities. The platform routes production requests to nodes based on what equipment the design requires.

**The tier progression**

Pioneer Nodes aren't static. They progress through tiers based on production history, quality record, and community standing:

- **Apprentice** — New nodes. Limited production volume. Paired with experienced nodes for early production runs.
- **Journeyman** — Established nodes with a track record. Standard production volume. Eligible for most Design Democracy pipeline assignments.
- **Master** — High-volume, high-quality nodes. Priority for complex and large-run assignments. May mentor Apprentice nodes.
- **Guild Lead** — Exceptional nodes that help coordinate regional manufacturing clusters. Advisory role in production planning.

Tier progression is based on demonstrated output, not seniority. A node that produces excellent work at high volume progresses faster than one that produces adequate work at low volume. The system rewards doing the work, not waiting in line.

**Why 100 and not 50 or 500**

The number is deliberate. At 100 nodes:

- **Geographic coverage is viable.** Across the United States, 100 nodes provides roughly 2 per state on average, with natural clustering in population centers. That's enough to offer reasonable shipping distances for most members.
- **Production capacity matches expected demand.** At early-stage membership (1,000-10,000 members), 100 nodes producing 10-100 units each per month yields 1,000-10,000 units total. That matches the expected throughput of Design Democracy at those membership levels.
- **Community is maintainable.** At 100, every Pioneer Node operator can know every other operator by name. That's a community, not a marketplace. Manufacturing coordination works through trust and reputation, not anonymous dispatch.
- **The cap creates aspiration.** When slots are limited, members who want to become Pioneer Nodes must invest in equipment, demonstrate skill, and apply. The application itself is a quality filter. Unlimited nodes would attract hobbyists; 100 slots attract serious manufacturers.

If the cooperative scales to 100,000+ members and production demand exceeds what 100 nodes can handle, the cap can be revisited through the governance system (Backer Election). But it's a deliberate, governance-level decision to expand — not an automatic scaling response.

**The connection to Cost+20%**

Pioneer Node economics follow the same Cost+20% rule as the rest of the platform. When a node produces a unit, the price includes:
- **Material cost** — actual cost of materials consumed
- **Labor cost** — actual time at a fair hourly rate
- **Equipment depreciation** — amortized cost of equipment used
- **20% cooperative margin** — the platform's operating cost

The node operator keeps 83.3% of the sale price (the total minus the 20% margin that covers cooperative infrastructure). The Cost+20% floor prevents a race to the bottom. No Pioneer Node can undercut another by accepting less than cost — the system enforces the floor.

This means Pioneer Nodes compete on quality, speed, and reliability — not on price. Two nodes producing the same design charge the same base price. The member chooses based on shipping distance, production speed, and quality rating. This is how manufacturing should work: craftsmanship rewarded, price wars prevented.

**What Pioneer Nodes are not**

Pioneer Nodes are not franchises. The cooperative doesn't sell node memberships or charge franchise fees. Becoming a Pioneer Node means registering your equipment, demonstrating capability, and being accepted into a limited community of manufacturers.

Pioneer Nodes are not employees. Node operators are cooperative members who choose to participate in manufacturing. They set their own hours, manage their own equipment, and decide which production runs to accept. The cooperative provides demand (through Design Democracy), quality standards (through the tier system), and economic fairness (through Cost+20%). The operator provides skill, equipment, and labor.

Pioneer Nodes are not gig workers. The 100-node cap, the tier system, and the long-term progression model are the opposite of gig-economy disposability. A Pioneer Node is a durable manufacturing position within a cooperative — closer to a master craftsperson in a guild than a driver refreshing an app for the next delivery.

One hundred factories. Not a thousand. Not a marketplace. A network of craftspeople with limited slots, real equipment, and a community small enough to maintain trust. That's how distributed manufacturing works when you design it for quality instead of scale.
