## THE PROOF

Every messaging tool starts the same way: a blank box. Write something. Pick where it goes. Hit send.

Nobody asks why.

---

## THE PUDDING

We built a messaging system called Battery Dispatch that does something no other broadcasting tool does. Before you can type a single word, it asks you a question: *What change are you trying to make?*

That's the Intent Field. It sits above the composer. It's not optional. It's not a tooltip. It's not a "best practice" reminder that disappears after you dismiss it three times. It's a required field that gates access to the entire composition surface. You cannot draft a message on Liana Banyan without first declaring what you're trying to accomplish by sending it.

This sounds like friction. It is. That's the point.

Every multi-channel broadcasting tool — Hootsuite, Buffer, Sprout Social, Mailchimp — starts with the assumption that you know what you want to say and just need help saying it in more places. The channel comes first. The schedule comes first. The analytics dashboard comes first. The "why" is assumed.

We flipped it. The "why" comes first. The channels come last.

Here's how it works. You open the Dispatch Compose page. The Intent Field asks its question. You type: "Moving local business owners from 'curious about the platform' to 'listing their first product on the marketplace.'" Now the composer opens. You write one message — the canonical version. Not eleven versions for eleven channels. One.

Then the system shows you eleven tiles, one per distribution channel. Each tile shows only the delta — the edge modification that adapts your canonical message for that channel. Twitter gets truncated to 280 characters with a link swap. The Cephas article version gets a header and proof strip. The email version gets a subject line. The core message stays untouched. One truth, eleven surfaces.

When you're done, you don't click a checkbox that says "I confirm." You don't toggle a switch. You encounter the As You Wish stamp.

As You Wish is a solemn confirmation pattern. It takes over the screen. It shows you: the canonical message, the target channels, the intent you declared at the beginning. And it asks you to stamp it — deliberately, with a full-screen affordance that makes you pause. Because you're about to say the same thing to thousands of people across eleven channels, and that deserves more than a checkbox.

The name comes from the Founder's transaction vocabulary. "As You Wish" means: I understand what I'm doing, and I choose to do it. It appears anywhere a member takes a consequential, multi-surface action.

Why does the Intent Field matter? Because every communication tool optimizes for output. More posts. More channels. More reach. More engagement. The metrics all point outward. But the quality of communication depends on the clarity of purpose — and nobody measures that.

The Intent Field measures it. Not with analytics. With architecture. You can't send a message whose purpose you haven't articulated. And once you've articulated it, the canonical message you write is anchored to something real. The channels are projections. The intent is the source.

MoneyPenny — the platform's AI receptionist — reads the intent and offers context-aware suggestions. "You said something similar on March 12. Want to reference it?" Not auto-applied. Not auto-corrected. Offered. The member keeps agency. The AI provides memory.

One intent. One canonical message. Eleven edge variations. One solemn confirmation. Zero checkboxes.
