## What the S Piston Actually Does

The S piston is a screw-press injection mechanism. Not a plunger. Not a ram. A screw — the same principle that drives industrial injection molding machines that cost $50,000 to $500,000.

At approximately 5,207 PSI with an 8-inch manual handle, it pushes molten thermoplastic into a mold cavity. The screw action does two things a plunger cannot: it mixes the material evenly, and it maintains consistent pressure throughout the shot. That means better parts with fewer defects.

Five common thermoplastics work at this pressure: LDPE, HDPE, polypropylene, ABS, and polystyrene. That covers keychains, clips, enclosures, tokens, brackets, organizers, and most things a maker would want to produce in batches of 50 to 500.

## When Injection Beats 3D Printing

Every maker knows 3D printing. FDM printers start under $200. But 3D printing has a cost-per-part problem: the 100th print costs the same as the first.

Injection molding has a mold cost — typically $50-500 for desktop aluminum molds — but after that, each part costs pennies in material. The crossover math is simple:

With a $50 mold: injection wins at **~50 units**.
With a $200 mold: injection wins at **~200 units**.
With a $500 mold: injection wins at **~500 units**.

For a maker selling keychains at a farmer's market, board game tokens on Etsy, or cosplay clips at conventions, the 50-200 unit range is exactly where they live. That's the sweet spot where the Canister System starts saving money on every single part.
