# Pudding #82 — The Sixty-Five Dollar Moat

*Why micro-entity provisional patents are the most underrated weapon in the individual creator's arsenal.*

---

The conventional wisdom on patents is clear: they are expensive, slow, and only useful if you can afford to enforce them. A non-provisional utility patent costs $1,600 in government fees at the standard rate, plus $10,000-$30,000 in attorney fees. The process takes 2-3 years. And if someone infringes, you need litigation funding that starts at six figures.

All of this is true for the standard path. But the standard path is not the only path.

The USPTO micro-entity designation exists for individuals and small entities with gross income below a threshold (currently $228,468 — roughly 3x the median national gross income). Micro-entities pay 75% less than standard filers. A provisional patent application — which establishes a priority date, provides 12 months of "patent pending" status, and requires no formal claims — costs a micro-entity $65.

Sixty-five dollars. That is less than a month of Spotify Premium for a family plan.

What does $65 buy? Twelve months during which your idea has a documented priority date with the United States Patent and Trademark Office. Anyone who files a similar idea after your filing date is junior to your claim. You can use this period to build, test, refine, and decide whether to convert to a full non-provisional application.

Liana Banyan Corporation has filed 11 provisional applications at $65 each. Total: $715. Innovations covered: over 2,100. Crown Jewels (zero prior art): 168. Formal claims documented in the filings: 2,103.

The first application (63/925,672) was filed on November 26, 2025. Its conversion deadline is November 26, 2026. That is 12 months of protection for $65. During those 12 months, every innovation in that filing has a priority date that cannot be taken away.

This is not a substitute for formal patent prosecution. The provisionals must be converted to non-provisionals before their deadlines, and that process will require patent counsel and significantly more money. But the provisionals serve a critical function: **they buy time.** Time to build. Time to demonstrate the technology works. Time to find the right patent attorney. Time to generate revenue that can fund the conversion.

For individual creators on the platform, the same logic applies. If a member invents a new manufacturing process, a novel product design, or an original recipe system, the IP Ledger tracks it and the A&A formal process documents it. Filing a micro-entity provisional costs the member $65 and 2-3 hours of documentation work. The platform's templates and AI assistance can reduce that to under an hour.

The moat is not built by one $65 filing. It is built by systematic documentation — every innovation described, every claim articulated, every filing date established — repeated consistently over time. Eleven filings over five months. $715. That is the price of a patent portfolio that would cost a traditional startup millions to replicate at standard rates.

The sixty-five dollar moat is not about preventing competition. It is about ensuring that the people who create things have a documented, legally recognized claim to what they created. The system is designed so that any creator — not just a corporation with a legal department — can protect their ideas for the price of a dinner out.
