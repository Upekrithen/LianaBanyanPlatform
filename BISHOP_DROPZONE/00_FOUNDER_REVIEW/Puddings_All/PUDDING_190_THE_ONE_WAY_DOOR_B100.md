# Pudding #190 — The One-Way Door

*Or: why every Mark in the system has someone's skin behind it.*

**By Jonathan Jones** · Founder, Liana Banyan
*Bishop Session B100 · FULL DRAFT · ~1,800 words*

---

There's a revolving door in every financial system you've ever used. Money comes in one side. Money goes out the other. The system charges a fee for the rotation. That's how banks work, how payment processors work, how every exchange and marketplace and fintech app works. Two-way door. Spin it fast enough and the fees add up.

Liana Banyan has a one-way door.

Value goes in. It doesn't come out. Not as dollars, not as euros, not as any sovereign currency, not ever. Once a Mark enters the system, it stays in the system. It circulates inside. It does work inside. It represents real value inside. But it never converts back to cash.

People hear this and their first question is: "Then what's the point?"

The point is that the door only opens one direction because the value on the other side is more useful than the cash you traded for it.

## How value gets in

A Mark enters the system one of several ways:

You earn it through patronage. You use the cooperative's services, you participate in its programs, you contribute your labor or your expertise or your time, and the cooperative allocates Marks to you based on your participation. The 80% of the patent portfolio that belongs to the cooperative — the 60% Patent Buckets plus the 10% Global Sponsor Pool plus the 10% Individual Patent Pedestals — backs every Mark in circulation. When you hold a Mark, you hold a claim against real intellectual property that the cooperative owns and licenses.

You buy Credits and convert them. Credits are the transactional currency — you purchase them to use the platform's commerce features, and a portion converts to Marks through the patronage allocation system.

You earn Joules during events and qualifying activities. Joules are the energy currency — they accrue through engagement, decay when you stop participating, and flow into the Mark system through the governance allocation pathways.

Every path in has the same characteristic: someone put something real behind the Mark. Labor. Money. Time. Intellectual property. Demonstrated effort. There is no way to get a Mark without putting skin in the game first.

## Why the door doesn't open the other way

The obvious question: if Marks represent real value, why can't you cash them out?

Three reasons, in order of how much they matter.

**Reason one: the moment you can cash out, you have a security.**

This is the practical legal reason. The Supreme Court's Howey test asks whether an instrument involves "an expectation of profits." If you can buy Marks, hold them, and sell them for more dollars than you paid — that's an expectation of profits. That's a security. That means SEC registration, broker-dealer requirements, state blue-sky compliance, and a compliance cost that would eat the cooperative alive before it served a single member.

The one-way door eliminates the profit expectation. You can't sell Marks for dollars. You can't cash them out. There is no capital appreciation pathway. The instrument is structurally incapable of generating the thing that would make it a security. That's not a loophole. That's the architecture.

**Reason two: the moment you can cash out, you have money transmission.**

If the cooperative converts Marks to dollars, it is transmitting money. Money transmission requires a license in every state the cooperative operates in. Forty-one states have adopted the Money Transmission Modernization Act, and every one of them exempts closed-loop stored value — value that is redeemable only within the issuing system. The one-way door is what makes Marks closed-loop. Open the door and you need forty-one licenses.

**Reason three: the moment you can cash out, you have extraction.**

This is the philosophical reason, and the one that matters most to me.

Every financial system that allows cash-out eventually optimizes for cash-out. The revolving door becomes the point. People stop using the system for what it does and start using it for what it pays. Speculation replaces participation. Liquidity replaces utility. And the system — the thing that was supposed to do useful work for real people — becomes a casino.

I've watched this happen sixteen times. I've built sixteen businesses. The ones where the money flowed through and out were the ones that attracted the wrong kind of attention. The ones where the value stayed inside and did work were the ones that actually served people.

The one-way door is my structural commitment to the second model. Value stays inside. It does work inside. It builds things inside. And the people who hold Marks are people who put something real behind them — labor, money, time, demonstrated effort — not people who bought a token hoping to flip it.

## What Marks do inside the system

Inside the cooperative, Marks are extraordinarily useful. They are the unit of patronage accounting under Subchapter T of the tax code. They determine your Written Notice of Allocation — the formal document that says "this is your share of the cooperative's net patronage earnings." They influence your governance weight through the SAA system. They unlock tier-based access to cooperative services and programs.

Marks are not inert tokens sitting in a wallet. They are active instruments that do governance work, economic work, and community work inside the cooperative every day. The fact that they don't convert to cash doesn't make them less valuable. It makes them differently valuable — valuable for what they do, not for what they sell for.

## The payment fork

"But what about when I need actual dollars?"

Good question. The answer is: that's a different fork entirely.

The LB Card — the cooperative's payment instrument — runs on cash. Dollars in, dollars out. The Substitution Method handles the boundary between the cooperative's internal economy and the external fiat economy. When you need to pay rent, buy groceries, fill your gas tank, the LB Card works like any other payment card. Cash funds it. Cash only.

Marks never touch the LB Card. Credits never touch the LB Card. Joules never touch the LB Card. The two forks — the cooperative economy and the fiat economy — run on parallel tracks that never cross.

This is not a limitation. It is the design. The cooperative economy is where value does work. The fiat economy is where value buys groceries. Both are necessary. Neither replaces the other. And the wall between them is what keeps both of them honest.

## Skin in the game

When I say every Mark has someone's skin behind it, I mean it literally.

The patent portfolio — 2,262 innovations, 221 Crown Jewels, twelve provisionals filed, ~2,405 formal claims — backs the Mark system through the 60/20/10/10 allocation. When the ROM-First AI inference architecture generates licensing revenue, 80% of that revenue flows into the cooperative and backs every Mark in circulation. That's my skin. Nine years of work. $45,000 of personal investment from a $30,000 income. Sixteen businesses of experience. The patents are the backing.

When a member earns Marks through patronage, that's their skin. They used the cooperative's services. They contributed their labor. They participated in governance. The Marks they hold represent demonstrated effort, not speculative hope.

When someone buys Credits and those Credits flow into the Mark system, that's cash skin. Real dollars, exchanged for real cooperative utility, backing real patronage allocations.

There is no way to hold a Mark without having put something real behind it. And there is no way to take that something back out as cash. The one-way door guarantees both: every Mark has skin behind it, and every Mark stays in the game.

## The name

I call it the one-way door because the metaphor is structural, not decorative. Doors that open both ways are revolving doors — they spin, they extract fees on every rotation, and eventually the spinning becomes the business model. Doors that open one way are entry doors — you walk through them to get somewhere, and once you're through, you're in.

The cooperative is the room on the other side. The Marks are the currency of that room. And the door you walked through to get there only opens inward.

Welcome in. The door doesn't go back.
