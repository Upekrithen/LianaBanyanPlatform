# Pudding #40: The Earn-Down
## How Drivers Build Equity in Their Vehicles by Driving Them

---

### At a Glance (~50 words)
Local Wheels gives cooperative members access to fleet vehicles with a twist: every mile you drive earns you ownership. The platform takes 20%. The driver keeps 80%. Over time, the driver's accumulated share crosses a threshold and the vehicle transfers to them. You earn the car by using the car.

---

### More Info (~300 words)

The standard model for vehicle access has two options. Buy a car (expensive upfront, depreciating asset) or rent one (perpetual payments, zero equity). Neither works well for people with limited capital who need reliable transportation for work.

Local Wheels introduces a third option: the earn-down. A cooperative fleet vehicle is available for members to drive. Revenue from the driver's work — deliveries, rideshare, errands, whatever generates income through the platform — is split 20/80. Twenty percent goes to the cooperative to cover vehicle costs, insurance, maintenance, and the platform's operating margin. Eighty percent goes to the driver.

But the driver's 80% isn't just income. A portion accumulates as equity in the vehicle itself. As the driver works, their ownership stake grows. When it crosses the transfer threshold, the vehicle becomes theirs.

This is not a lease. A lease has fixed payments regardless of whether you work or not. The earn-down only accumulates when you're generating revenue. If you take a week off, nothing happens — you don't owe anything, and your earn-down balance stays where it is.

This is not a loan. There's no interest. There's no debt. There's no credit check. The earn-down is a cooperative benefit: you use a shared asset, you generate value with it, and over time the asset transitions to you because you've earned it through labor, not through financing.

The earn-down model also applies to other high-value equipment in the cooperative. Manufacturing equipment, professional tools, commercial kitchen equipment — anything where the upfront cost is prohibitive but the ongoing use generates revenue. The 20/80 split and the accumulation logic are the same.

### Full Detail

**How the 20/80 split works**

When a driver uses a Local Wheels fleet vehicle to generate revenue through the platform, every transaction splits the proceeds:

- **20% — Cooperative share.** Covers the vehicle's purchase cost amortization, insurance, registration, maintenance, fuel fund, and the platform's operating margin. This is the Cost+20% logic applied to vehicle operations.
- **80% — Driver share.** This is the driver's income. From this 80%, a configurable portion (set at the fleet level) accumulates toward the earn-down balance.

The earn-down percentage is transparent. If the fleet sets a 25% earn-down allocation, then 25% of the driver's 80% share goes toward their ownership stake. The driver sees exactly how much they earned, how much goes to income, and how much went toward the vehicle. No hidden fees. No variable rates.

**The transfer threshold**

Each fleet vehicle has a transfer value — the amount the cooperative needs to recover before the vehicle can transfer. This is not the sticker price. It's the cooperative's actual cost: acquisition price minus depreciation, plus maintenance investments, plus insurance costs incurred during the earn-down period.

When the driver's accumulated earn-down balance reaches the transfer value, the vehicle title transfers to the driver. The cooperative has recovered its costs. The driver has a vehicle. The transaction is complete.

If the vehicle depreciates faster than the driver accumulates, the transfer threshold drops. A vehicle that cost the cooperative $15,000 might have a transfer value of $8,000 after two years of depreciation and use. A driver accumulating $300/month in earn-down balance would cross the threshold in roughly 27 months.

**What happens after transfer**

Once the vehicle transfers, the driver owns it outright. They can continue using it for platform-generated revenue (now keeping 100% instead of 80%, since there's no cooperative vehicle cost to cover), use it personally, or sell it. The cooperative has no further claim.

The driver's relationship with the cooperative continues — they're still a member, still have access to other cooperative services — but the vehicle is theirs. Clean transfer. No strings.

**The earn-down progress display**

The Local Wheels page shows each driver their earn-down progress as a visual tracker. The display includes:

- **Total earn-down accumulated** — how much they've built toward ownership
- **Transfer threshold** — how much is needed for full transfer
- **Progress percentage** — how close they are
- **Estimated completion** — based on current earning rate
- **Monthly earn-down history** — shows the accumulation trend over time

This visibility is critical. The earn-down only works psychologically if the driver can see the progress. A number going up month after month creates motivation that a vague promise of "someday you'll own it" never can. The tracker makes the future ownership concrete and measurable.

**The Lemon Lot: peer-to-peer vehicle marketplace**

Separate from the fleet earn-down, the Lemon Lot is a peer-to-peer vehicle marketplace within the cooperative. Members can list vehicles for rent at a minimum of $15/day, with Cost+20% applied to the rental price.

The Lemon Lot serves members who own vehicles they don't use full-time. A member with a truck they only use on weekends can list it Monday through Friday. The rental income flows through the platform at Cost+20%, giving the owner 83.3% of the rental fee.

The Lemon Lot and the fleet earn-down complement each other. A driver who earns down a fleet vehicle and takes ownership might later list that vehicle on the Lemon Lot during periods when they don't need it — generating passive income from an asset they earned through work.

**Rideshare Routes: the commute match**

The third vehicle feature is Rideshare Routes — a recurring commute matching system. Members who drive the same route at the same time can share rides. The platform matches them based on origin, destination, schedule, and available days.

Rideshare Routes uses Cost+20% for the ride cost, split between fuel and vehicle wear. The driver keeps 83.3% of the ride fee. But the primary benefit isn't income — it's cost reduction. Two members splitting a 30-mile commute each save roughly half the fuel and wear cost they'd incur driving alone.

Rideshare matches are displayed as route cards showing origin area, destination area, schedule compatibility, and available days. The system matches on recurring patterns, not one-time requests. This is for the member who drives to the same job five days a week, not for the member who needs a ride to the airport once.

**Why the earn-down matters**

Vehicle ownership is one of the sharpest dividing lines in American economic life. People with cars can get to jobs, shops, schools, and healthcare. People without cars cannot — or must pay extractive rates for rideshare services that build zero equity.

The earn-down doesn't solve the entire transportation problem. But it converts a cooperative fleet vehicle from a rental expense into a wealth-building tool. Every mile driven moves the driver closer to owning the asset that makes their livelihood possible.

Traditional rideshare platforms (Uber, Lyft) require drivers to provide their own vehicles, absorb all depreciation, and accept whatever rate the algorithm sets. Drivers end up with worn-out cars and nothing to show for years of work.

The earn-down inverts this. The cooperative provides the vehicle. The platform sets fair rates via Cost+20%. The driver accumulates ownership through the work itself. After the transfer, the driver owns a vehicle that's been maintained by the cooperative throughout the earn-down period — not a car run into the ground by exploitative economics.

That's the difference between a platform that extracts value from drivers and a cooperative that builds value for them. Twenty percent goes to the commons. Eighty percent goes to the worker. And eventually, one hundred percent belongs to the person who did the work.
