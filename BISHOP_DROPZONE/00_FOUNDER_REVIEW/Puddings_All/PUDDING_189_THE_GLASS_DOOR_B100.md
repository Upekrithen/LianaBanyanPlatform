# Pudding #189 — The Glass Door

*Or: why we publish every letter before we send it.*

**By Jonathan Jones** · Founder, Liana Banyan
*Bishop Session B100 · FULL DRAFT · ~2,400 words*

---

When I was nineteen, I got called into a room at Fort Benning and told that my platoon was being reorganized. No explanation. No input. No prior notice. The decision had already been made by people I'd never spoken to, about a team I'd been training with for months, and I found out about it the same way everyone else did — by reading a piece of paper taped to a wall.

That's how most organizations communicate with the people they affect. The decisions are made in private. The people affected learn about them after the fact. And the explanation, if one comes at all, is a cleaned-up version of whatever actually happened — the version that makes the decision look inevitable instead of chosen.

I hated it then. I hate it now. And when I started building Liana Banyan, I decided that this platform would not work that way.

## What the Glass Door is

Every letter this cooperative sends to someone outside the cooperative is published on our website before it is sent.

Every letter. Every Crown letter to a business leader. Every research invitation to an academic. Every partnership pitch. Every media outreach. Every single piece of outbound communication that the Founder writes on behalf of the cooperative gets published on a member-readable page — with the recipient's name, the scheduled send date, and the full text or a substantive summary — before the letter ships.

Right now, there are 95 letters in the Glass Door. You can read every one of them.

This is not a transparency report. Transparency reports are retrospective — they tell you what happened after it happened, in the format the organization chose, omitting whatever the organization decided to omit. The Glass Door is prospective. It tells you what we are about to do, in the actual words we are about to use, before we do it.

## Why

Three reasons. In order of how much they cost me personally.

**Reason one: I can't lie to two people at the same time if both of them can read what I said to the other one.**

This sounds obvious. It is obvious. But the entire architecture of institutional communication is built on the assumption that you CAN say different things to different people, because they can't compare notes. Every startup pitch deck says something slightly different to each investor. Every corporate partnership letter emphasizes whichever priority the recipient cares about and downplays the rest. Every political campaign tailors its message to the room.

The Glass Door makes that impossible. If I tell Trebor Scholz that the Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge is about democratic IP governance, and I tell Cory Doctorow that it's about anti-enshittification architecture, and I tell Marjorie Kelly that it's about generative ownership — those three framings better be compatible, because all three letters are published on the same website and all three recipients can read all three.

They are compatible. That's the point. The Pledge IS all three of those things, and the fact that I can publish all three framings simultaneously and they don't contradict each other is the proof that the architecture is coherent. The Glass Door is not just a transparency mechanism. It's a coherence test. If you can't publish all your outbound communications simultaneously without contradiction, your story has holes in it. Ours doesn't.

**Reason two: the members should be able to see what I'm doing in their name.**

I am the Founder. I write the letters. I decide who to write to, when, and what to say. That is a significant amount of power in a cooperative where the members are supposed to govern the platform.

Most cooperatives handle this by electing a communications committee, or by having the board review outbound messaging, or by simply trusting the founder and hoping for the best. All of those approaches have the same failure mode: the members find out what was said after it was said, and by then the words are already out the door.

The Glass Door reverses this. Members see the letter before it ships. In Phase 2 — which we just deployed — members can vote on whether it ships. The dispatch is gated on a governance predicate. I cannot override a member veto by founder discretion alone.

This is not performative. This is operational. If I write a letter that the membership thinks is a mistake — wrong recipient, wrong timing, wrong framing, wrong ask — the membership can stop it. Before it goes out. Before it damages a relationship. Before it commits the cooperative to a position the members don't support.

I don't expect the members to veto many letters. Most of what I write is straightforward and the members trust me to write it. But the fact that they CAN veto — structurally, not just theoretically — changes what I write. I write more carefully. I think harder about framing. I consider how the letter will read to someone who isn't the recipient — because someone who isn't the recipient is going to read it, on the Glass Door, before it ships.

The constraint makes me better at the job.

**Reason three: the recipients deserve to know what they're walking into.**

When a Crown letter arrives in Trebor Scholz's inbox, it is not a secret pitch. It is a public communication. He can go to the Glass Door and see that the letter was published before it was sent. He can see the other letters we've sent. He can see whether we said something different to someone else.

This changes the power dynamic. In a normal outreach context, the recipient has no way to verify the sender's claims against the sender's other claims. They have to take the letter at face value. With the Glass Door, the recipient can cross-reference. They can see the full pattern of our outreach. They can decide whether we're consistent.

For an academic like Scholz, this is particularly valuable. Academics live in a world where credibility is everything and bullshit detectors are finely calibrated. Showing up with a pitch that is already public — that is already published, already readable by anyone, already cross-referenceable against every other letter we've sent — is the strongest possible credibility signal. It says: we are not hiding anything, because we literally can't.

## What it costs me

The Glass Door costs me the ability to be strategic about messaging.

I can't emphasize one thing to one recipient and a different thing to another if the two emphases are contradictory. I can't time a letter to catch someone at a vulnerable moment without that timing being visible. I can't write a letter that flatters the recipient in a way that would embarrass me if the membership read it. I can't make promises in private that I wouldn't make in public.

These are real costs. Every communications professional I've ever met would tell me that the Glass Door is a strategic disadvantage. They would say: "You need the flexibility to tailor your message. You need the ability to say different things to different audiences. You need private channels for sensitive negotiations."

They're right that I need flexibility. They're wrong that flexibility requires secrecy. The Glass Door forces me to find framings that are simultaneously true for all audiences — and framings that are simultaneously true for all audiences are, by definition, more true than framings that only work in private.

The constraint costs me tactical flexibility. It gives me structural credibility. I'll take that trade every time.

## The fourth axis

Trebor Scholz defined platform cooperativism along two axes: democratic ownership and democratic labor. The Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge (#2260) added a third axis: democratic IP governance. The Glass Door adds a fourth: **democratic communications.**

Four axes of cooperative governance:
1. Members own the platform (Scholz's first axis)
2. Members govern the labor performed on the platform (Scholz's second axis)
3. Members own the intellectual property in perpetuity via bylaw codification (#2260)
4. Members govern the platform's external communications via pre-publication and voting (#2262)

Each axis is structural, not policy. Each is codified in architecture that the founder cannot override. And each one, individually, would be unusual for a cooperative platform. Together, they are — to my knowledge — unprecedented.

I don't say that to claim credit. I say it because the next cooperative that builds on this model should know that the Glass Door exists, and that it works, and that it makes the founder better at the job instead of worse. The constraint is the feature.

## The name

I called it the Glass Door because the metaphor is obvious and that's fine. When you walk through a glass door, everyone can see you doing it. You can't sneak through a glass door. You can't pretend you didn't go through it. The door is there, the glass is clear, and the act of walking through it is visible to everyone on both sides.

Every letter we send walks through the Glass Door. The members can see it. The recipients can see it. The public can see it. And the Founder — the person walking through it — is fully visible the entire time.

Some people build walls and put doors in them and hand out keys. We built a glass door and left it open.

## What you can do with it

If you are a Liana Banyan member: go to `/outreach` and read the letters. All 95 of them. See who we're writing to, what we're asking, and when we plan to send it. If you disagree with any of it, say so. Your vote matters — in Phase 2, it gates dispatch.

If you are a researcher studying platform cooperativism: the Glass Door is a dataset. 95 outbound communications from a cooperative platform, published prospectively, with full text, recipient categories, and timestamps. Use it.

If you are a recipient of one of our letters: you already know what we said, because it was published before we sent it. If you want to know what we said to everyone else, go look. The door is glass. That's the point.

If you are building your own cooperative: steal this. The Glass Door is covered by the Cooperative Defensive Patent Pledge, which means you can use it royalty-free, in perpetuity, as long as you meet the cooperative criteria. Build your own Glass Door. Make your outbound communications public. Discover, as I did, that the constraint makes you better.
