# Pudding #182 — The Shop That Fixed My Son's Car

*A Marks story. A Joules story. A story about how most people already know how this works — they just don't have a word for it yet.*

**By Jonathan Jones** · Founder, Liana Banyan
*Bishop Session B096 · Draft 1 · NAMED-ATTRIBUTION-PENDING*

## The call

My son's car broke down a few days ago. Fuel pump, and something going on with the gas tank. He works. He needs the car to get to work. This is the thing about being an adult — the car isn't a toy, it's a *pathway*.

I called the shop I've worked with for years.

Here's what happened next. The shop sent a tow truck to pick up the car — they paid for it. They bought the parts — they paid for those too. They put a tech on it, and **today — the same day I am writing this essay — we picked up the car**. My son drove it home himself. The timing is not a rhetorical flourish. That is actually what happened, on the actual day you are reading these words for the first time.

Before we hung up, the owner and I said *"we'll work something out."*

No invoice. No credit application. No "can you put down a deposit?" The shop was **out of pocket** for the tow, the parts, and a day of skilled labor, and they fronted the whole thing on a handshake over the phone.

My son will pay them back for the tow and the parts — that's Credits, one for one, cash at cost. But the **labor** and the **priority turnaround** and the **"drive it home today"** — those are a different currency, and that's what this essay is about.

## Why would anyone do that

Reasonable question. The answer is nine months long.

Nine months ago I started overhauling that shop's technology stack. Not one piece — all of it. The shop computers were old enough that half of them couldn't run the vendor software anymore. The network was flat and wide open — if one machine got hit, the whole place went down. The security cameras were on a separate wire run from a decade-old installer who was long gone. The VoIP phone system was flaky. The diagnostic machines needed their manufacturer software kept current, and the online repair-manual subscriptions needed to actually *work* from every bay.

I recommended what to buy. I used the owner's account to purchase it. I set all of it up. I rewired. I re-planned the network so the security cameras, the shop floor, and the office were properly segmented. I got the VoIP stable. I kept the manufacturer software licenses current. I did this in bursts — some months a day here and a day there, some months I was in their office for three or four days running, because I have a personal rule: *nothing should be broken for more than two hours, ever.*

Real time, it was a nine-month project. My time in it, spread across nine months, was maybe a week each month, occasionally more.

**I never took a dollar of cash for any of it.**

What I asked for instead was this: keep my vehicles, and my family's vehicles, running. The same way I keep your shop running, you keep our wheels turning. Oil changes. Inspections. Batteries. Tires. The occasional A/C compressor or fuel pump.

That's the whole deal. It was never written down. It didn't need to be.

## What we have, without ever calling it that

The shop and I have built, over years, a **four-currency relationship**. We just didn't have the vocabulary for it. Most of you reading this have one of these too — with a neighbor, a sister-in-law, a kid down the street who mows your lawn, a friend who's a nurse and tells you whether you need to go to the ER or not. You probably have several. They are the most *durable* economic relationships in your life. They don't break when the power goes out. They don't require a credit score. They don't disappear when the app gets sold to a bigger company.

Here's the vocabulary, mapped onto the story of my son's car:

| What happened | What Liana Banyan calls it | What's really going on |
|---|---|---|
| Tow truck + parts | **Credits** | Real cost, paid in cash, one dollar for one dollar |
| Labor discount + priority turnaround + "drive it home today" | **Marks** | The *differential* — what I got *beyond* strict at-cost. This is where the relationship shows up in the economic result |
| Nine months of IT work, still ongoing | **Joules** | Surplus effort already banked. Work done ahead of need. The reservoir |
| The shop fronting everything on a phone call | **Backed Marks** | The shop knows the reservoir is real. They trusted the draw because the relationship has a credit history you can see with the naked eye |
| "We'll work something out" | **The time-patient settlement** | Marks don't have to settle today. They settle when settlement makes sense. The clock is cooperative, not adversarial |

That's it. That's the whole four-currency system, and it was already running in my life — and in yours — before Liana Banyan existed. **We didn't invent this. We just named the pieces and built a system where you can have this kind of relationship with people you've never met.**

## This isn't new. It's ancient.

If you think the Marks idea sounds too good to be true — that a whole economy could run on effort-balances instead of pure cash — look up the **WIR Bank** in Switzerland. It's been running since 1934. Ninety-two years. Swiss businesses trade with each other in a second currency called WIR, which is *not* the Swiss franc, and it settles on the books of a cooperative bank. Tens of thousands of participating businesses. Billions in annual volume. Nobody in Switzerland calls it fringe.

Or the **Sardex** system in Sardinia — launched in 2009 during the European debt crisis when small businesses there couldn't get bank credit anymore. Mutual credit, local, trust-anchored. Still running. Still growing.

Or, if you want the extreme case study, read R. A. Radford's 1945 paper *"The Economic Organisation of a P.O.W. Camp."* He was an Allied prisoner during World War II and watched his camp spontaneously develop a functioning cigarette-backed economy with prices, arbitrage, and exchange rates between currencies from Red Cross parcels. Men in a prison camp with no central authority, no written laws, and no access to banks invented an economy in weeks because that's what humans do when they have work to do and things they need.

Or, closest to home for many Americans: the **Amish barn-raising**. Twenty families show up, build your barn in a weekend, eat at your table, go home. No invoice. No contract. No tax form. When *their* barn burns down next year, you show up with a hammer. That's Joules. That's Backed Marks. That's time-patient settlement. It works because everyone in that community has a reservoir you can see.

None of this is fringe. None of it is utopian. All of it works. All of it has worked for as long as humans have had tools and each other.

**The only thing Liana Banyan adds is the ability to have this relationship with a stranger.**

## Why that matters

The problem with the Amish-barn-raising model is that it doesn't scale past people who already know each other. If you move to a new town, you don't get a barn. If your family is small, or fractured, or far away, you don't have twenty hammers on call. Most Americans don't live in villages anymore. We live in cul-de-sacs and apartment buildings surrounded by people whose names we don't know.

What we built is the part that was missing: a way to carry your reservoir with you. A way for a stranger to see — honestly, with receipts, without having to take anyone's word for it — that you've been doing the work, that your Joules are real, that your word is good. And then a set of rules (the Cost+20% floor, the one-way valve, the 83.3% creator keep, the Frame Locks, the Steward turn-keys) that keep anyone from ever converting that reservoir into extraction.

Every single piece of this platform was designed to protect the kind of relationship I have with that shop. Not replace it. Not digitize it. **Protect it, extend it, and make it available to people who don't have one yet.**

## The kicker

Here's the thing I keep running into when I explain this to people: nine out of ten of them already know how it works. They've lived it. They just didn't know what to call the pieces.

You've probably helped a neighbor move. You've probably picked up someone's kid from school when they were stuck. You've probably had a friend fix your computer, or cook for you when you were sick, or talk you off a ledge at 2 a.m. Somebody has probably done those things for you.

You already have a Joule reservoir. You've already made Marks draws on other people's reservoirs. You've already settled on time-patient terms you never spoke out loud.

All we did was notice, and write it down, and build a place where it keeps working when the people change.

My son drove his own car home today because I wired a network nine months ago. That's not a metaphor. That's a four-currency transaction settled on a handshake, closed on the exact day this essay was written.

That's Marks. That's how it works. That's the whole thing.
