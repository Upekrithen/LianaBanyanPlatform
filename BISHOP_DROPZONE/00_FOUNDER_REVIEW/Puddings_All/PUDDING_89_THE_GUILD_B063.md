# Pudding #89 — The Guild

*Professional communities where your reputation is portable and your membership costs five dollars.*

---

Trade guilds are among the oldest organizational structures in human civilization. Medieval guilds set quality standards, trained apprentices, and ensured that a master carpenter in London could be trusted by a customer who had never met them. The guild vouched for the craftsperson. The craftsperson's reputation traveled with the guild's seal.

Modern professional networks abandoned this model. LinkedIn charges $360-$720 per year for premium features. Industry associations charge $200-$2,000 in annual dues. Freelancing platforms take 10-20% of every transaction. In each case, the professional's reputation is locked inside the platform — if you leave, your reviews, your connections, and your verified skills stay behind.

Liana Banyan's Guild system restores the original model with one critical upgrade: your reputation is portable. Your ADAPT score — built from completed projects, peer feedback, and demonstrated reliability — belongs to you. It is not a platform metric. It is a personal asset that you carry across every Guild you join.

A member can belong to many Guilds simultaneously. A carpenter who also teaches woodworking and does photography on weekends joins three Guilds. Each Guild has its own community, its own skill verification, its own project board. But the ADAPT score is unified — it reflects the whole person, not a siloed version of them visible only within one professional context.

The Guild directory is search-first and category-organized. Unlike Tribe directories (which default to map view because Tribes are about place), Guild directories default to skill-first because Guilds are about function. You are looking for a plumber, not looking for your neighbor — even though they might be the same person.

Guild membership costs $5 per year. The same as everything else on the platform. There is no premium tier. There is no recruiter access fee. There is no promoted listing. The plumber with ten years of experience and a 95th-percentile ADAPT score appears in the directory on the same terms as the plumber who joined yesterday. The difference is visible in their record, not in how much they paid to be seen.

The 83.3% creator share applies to Guild-sourced work. When a customer finds a carpenter through the Woodworking Guild and pays $500 for a custom table, the carpenter keeps $416.67. The platform takes $83.33. The Guild does not take a cut — it is an organizing structure, not a toll booth.

Guilds are professional. Tribes are personal. Both are unlimited per member. Both cost $5 per year. Together they replace LinkedIn, industry associations, and freelance marketplaces — not by being cheaper (though they are), but by being structurally incapable of extracting from their members.
