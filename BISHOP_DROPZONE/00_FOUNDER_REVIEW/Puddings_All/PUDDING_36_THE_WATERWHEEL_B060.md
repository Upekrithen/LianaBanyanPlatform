# Pudding #36: The WaterWheel
## How Vacation Rentals Fund Tenant Subsidies

---

### At a Glance (~50 words)
When a cooperative housing property earns short-term rental income, the WaterWheel splits it four ways: 30% to the rental program, 40% to tenant subsidies, 15% to maintenance, and 15% to the cooperative fund. The tenants who live there year-round benefit most from the property generating income while they're away.

---

### More Info (~300 words)

Housing cooperatives have a revenue problem. Properties cost money to maintain. Tenants need affordable rent. And the gap between what maintenance costs and what affordable rent covers has to come from somewhere.

Most housing organizations solve this with grants, government subsidies, or higher rents that push the definition of "affordable." The WaterWheel solves it with a revenue split that uses short-term rental income to directly subsidize the people who live in the property full-time.

Here's how it works. A cooperative housing property has units occupied by long-term tenants and units or periods available for short-term vacation rentals. When a vacation guest books a stay, the rental income flows through the WaterWheel model:

**30% — Rental Program Share.** Covers the costs of operating the short-term rental: cleaning, turnover, listing management, guest communications, supplies.

**40% — Tenant Subsidy.** Goes directly into reducing long-term tenant costs. This is the largest share because it's the entire point of the model. The people who make the property a home benefit from the income it generates when guests visit.

**15% — Maintenance Fund.** Covers property upkeep, repairs, and capital improvements. Protects the asset that generates the revenue.

**15% — Cooperative Fund.** Flows back to the broader cooperative for shared infrastructure, insurance, and cross-property coordination.

The WaterWheel also applies a 2.23x contribution matching multiplier. When a member contributes to housing — whether through cash, labor, materials, professional services, or equipment — the cooperative matches that contribution at 2.23x in housing fund credits. The multiplier rewards participation in the physical infrastructure that makes everything else possible.

This isn't a financial product. It's a revenue allocation model. No one earns interest. No one receives dividends. The property generates income; the model determines where it goes.

---

### Full Detail

**Why the splits are hardcoded**

The WaterWheel percentages (30/40/15/15) are not configurable. They cannot be changed by property managers, cooperative administrators, or individual tenants. This is a deliberate design decision.

In traditional property management, revenue splits are negotiated — which means the party with the most leverage gets the best deal. Landlords negotiate higher management fees. Property managers negotiate performance bonuses. Tenants negotiate nothing because they have no leverage in the revenue conversation.

Hardcoding the splits removes negotiation entirely. Every property in the cooperative runs the same model. Every tenant benefits from the same 40% subsidy allocation. Every maintenance fund receives the same 15%. The rules are the same for a five-unit building and a fifty-unit complex.

This makes the system legible. A prospective tenant can look at the WaterWheel model and know exactly how vacation rental income will affect their housing costs. There's no fine print, no variable performance clause, no "up to 40% subsidy depending on occupancy." The split is structural.

**How the 40% tenant subsidy actually works**

The 40% flows into a property-level subsidy pool. That pool is distributed to long-term tenants based on their occupancy and need, not based on how much vacation rental income their specific unit generated. A tenant in Unit 3 benefits from vacation bookings in Unit 7. The model treats the property as a collective asset, not as a collection of independent revenue streams.

This means tenants don't compete with each other for subsidy dollars. It also means a property with one heavily-booked vacation unit subsidizes all tenants equally, not just the ones whose neighbors travel often.

The subsidy appears as a direct reduction in the tenant's monthly housing cost. It is visible on their housing dashboard, broken down by source month. The tenant can see: "March vacation bookings generated $1,200 in subsidy pool. Your share: $200 reduction in April rent."

That visibility is the point. The tenant doesn't just experience lower rent — they understand why their rent is lower, where the money came from, and how the model works. Financial literacy through transparent architecture.

**The 2.23x contribution multiplier**

Members who contribute to housing receive a 2.23x match in housing fund credits. The multiplier applies to all six contribution types:

- **Cash contributions** — Direct financial support to a property or the cooperative fund
- **Labor contributions** — Physical work on properties (painting, cleaning, moving, repairs)
- **Material contributions** — Donated materials (lumber, paint, fixtures, tools)
- **Professional services** — Skilled work (plumbing, electrical, legal, accounting)
- **Property contributions** — Making a property or unit available to the cooperative
- **Equipment contributions** — Tools, vehicles, or machinery for property maintenance

The multiplier exists because housing infrastructure is the physical foundation that makes every other cooperative function possible. A member who helps maintain a property is supporting the Bounty Photography program (photographers need the community to exist), the cooperative purchasing program (businesses need local customers who can afford to live nearby), and every other initiative that depends on stable community presence.

2.23x is not arbitrary. It represents the cooperative's determination that housing contributions generate roughly 2.23 times their direct value in downstream cooperative benefit. The exact number is set by the cooperative and is transparent — it appears in every contribution receipt and housing dashboard.

**The Vacation Priority System**

Not all vacation bookings are equal. The WaterWheel includes a priority system for who gets to book vacation units:

1. **Property contributors** — Members who have contributed to the specific property get first booking priority. This rewards the people who maintain the infrastructure.
2. **Any contributors** — Members who have contributed to any cooperative housing property get second priority. This rewards housing participation broadly.
3. **Members** — Standard cooperative members at $5/year get third priority. They have access, just not preferential access.
4. **Public** — Non-members can book at the standard rate. Their bookings still generate WaterWheel revenue that flows through the same 30/40/15/15 split.

Priority doesn't mean lower price. All bookings use the same Cost+20% pricing. Priority means earlier access to limited availability. A contributor who wants the beach house in July can book before the general membership. A member can book before the public.

This creates an incentive loop: contribute to housing → get booking priority → enjoy the property you helped maintain → your booking generates revenue that subsidizes tenants. The WaterWheel turns housing into a self-reinforcing cycle.

**The three-level appeal process**

Because housing affects people's lives more directly than most platform functions, the housing domain includes a three-level appeal process:

- **Level 1: Steward Review** — Property-level resolution by a designated housing steward. Most disputes resolve here.
- **Level 2: Ombudsperson Review** — Cooperative-level review by an independent ombudsperson. For disputes the steward cannot resolve.
- **Level 3: AAA Arbitration** — External, binding arbitration through the American Arbitration Association. For disputes that exhaust internal processes.

This structure applies to tenant disputes, contribution disagreements, subsidy allocation questions, and vacation booking conflicts. The three levels ensure that members always have recourse and that the cooperative's internal processes are backed by external accountability.

**What the WaterWheel is not**

The WaterWheel is not a financial product. No one holds shares in a property. No one receives interest on contributions. No one earns a return on their participation. The 40% subsidy is a cost reduction, not income. The 2.23x multiplier is a cooperative benefit, not a financial return.

The WaterWheel is a revenue allocation model for cooperative housing. It answers one question: when a property generates income, who benefits? The answer is: tenants first (40%), operations second (30%), maintenance third (15%), and the broader cooperative fourth (15%). That's not a philosophy. That's plumbing. And like plumbing, it works because the pipes don't move.
