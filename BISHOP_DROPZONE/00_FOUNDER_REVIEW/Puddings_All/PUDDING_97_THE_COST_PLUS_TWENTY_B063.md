# Pudding #97 — The Cost Plus Twenty

*The three-word pricing rule that makes extraction structurally impossible.*

---

Every platform has a take rate. Uber takes 25-40%. Etsy takes 6.5% plus listing fees plus advertising fees. Amazon takes 8-45% depending on category. DoorDash takes 15-30% from restaurants. In each case, the take rate is set by the platform, adjusted by the platform, and optimized for the platform's revenue — not the seller's earnings.

Liana Banyan has a take rate too. It is Cost+20%. Three words. One rule. Applied universally to every transaction on the platform, from a $5 subscription to a $5,000 manufacturing order.

What does Cost+20% mean in practice? The platform calculates its actual cost to facilitate a transaction — payment processing, server infrastructure, bandwidth, support overhead — and adds exactly 20% margin. On a $500 transaction, if the platform's cost is $83.33, the platform keeps $83.33. The creator keeps $416.67. That is the 83.3% creator share.

The 20% margin is not a profit-maximizing number. It is a sustainability number. It covers operational costs, generates a modest surplus for platform development, and — critically — it does not change. There is no mechanism in the system for adjusting the margin based on market conditions, competitive pressure, or revenue targets. The code enforces Cost+20%. The database enforces Cost+20%. The legal structure enforces Cost+20%.

This is what makes extraction structurally impossible. Extraction requires the ability to increase the take rate — to capture more of each transaction as the platform gains market power. When the take rate is a hardcoded constant, the platform cannot extract more as it grows. It can only process more transactions at the same margin. Growth benefits the members (more customers, more visibility, more coalition purchasing power) without costing them a higher percentage.

The conventional counterargument is that a fixed margin limits the platform's ability to invest in growth. This is true. Liana Banyan cannot spend $100 million on user acquisition, because it does not have $100 million and its margin structure cannot generate venture-scale returns. This is a feature, not a bug. The platform grows at the rate its members generate economic activity, not at the rate external capital demands.

Cost+20% is not a marketing slogan. It is an architectural constraint. It is enforced in the codebase, visible in every transaction, and documented in every Cephas article that discusses platform economics. The math is on the label because the math is the product.
