# Pudding #32: Three Currencies Walk Into a Wallet
## Why Your Cooperative Has Three Kinds of Money

---

### At a Glance (~50 words)
Liana Banyan uses three currencies: Credits (dollars you spend), Marks (effort you've contributed), and Joules (surplus the cooperative saved). They all have equal value. They serve different purposes. And together, they make it possible to run a cooperative without venture capital.

---

### More Info (~300 words)

Most platforms have one kind of money: dollars. You pay dollars, the platform takes a percentage, and the creator gets what's left. Simple. Also extractive.

Liana Banyan has three currencies because the cooperative needs to track three different things that dollars alone can't distinguish.

**Credits** are the day-to-day transactional currency. One Credit equals one dollar. When you buy something from a member's storefront, you pay in Credits. The creator keeps 83.3% — the platform takes Cost+20%, which covers the actual operational cost plus a 20% margin that funds everything else. Credits flow in and out. They're the cash register.

**Marks** are the effort-differential currency. You earn Marks by contributing to the cooperative — completing Crew Call bookings, serving as a Steward, participating in governance, building a Beacon Run. Marks are not purchased. They are earned. They carry governance weight: the more Marks you've accumulated, the more your vote matters in Backer Elections. Marks are the reputation layer.

**Joules** are the surplus currency — what the cooperative generates beyond its operating costs. Think of Joules as a "forever stamp": once created, they retain value indefinitely. When the cooperative runs profitably (which the Cost+20% model guarantees by design), the surplus converts into Joules distributed to members. Joules are the savings account.

All three currencies have equal nominal value. One Credit = one Mark = one Joule = one dollar equivalent. But they flow in different directions. Credits flow through transactions. Marks accumulate through effort. Joules crystallize from surplus.

This three-currency model means the cooperative can reward effort (Marks), facilitate commerce (Credits), and preserve surplus (Joules) without conflating them. A member who contributes heavily to governance earns Marks — even if they never buy or sell anything.

---

### Full Detail

The three-currency system is the economic spine of Liana Banyan. It solves three problems that single-currency platforms cannot:

**Problem 1: How do you reward effort that isn't a transaction?**

On Etsy, you earn money by selling things. Period. If you spend 40 hours moderating a forum, organizing a community event, or mentoring new sellers, you earn nothing. Your effort is invisible to the economic system.

On Liana Banyan, that same effort earns Marks. Completing a Crew Call booking earns Marks. Serving as a Steward in dispute resolution earns Marks. Participating in a Backer Election earns Marks. Writing a Guild charter, organizing a Tribe meetup, filing a quality report through the ADAPT system — all earn Marks.

Marks don't replace Credits. You can't pay your rent with Marks (that's what Credits are for). But Marks give your effort economic visibility. They prove you contributed. And they carry governance weight — in Backer Elections, your vote is weighted by your accumulated Marks. You can't buy governance power. You earn it.

**Problem 2: How do you preserve surplus without creating shareholders?**

Traditional cooperatives distribute surplus as patronage dividends — cash payments proportional to how much you used the cooperative. This works, but it treats surplus as something to get rid of rather than something to build with.

Joules are the cooperative's answer. When the Cost+20% model generates more margin than the cooperative needs to operate, the surplus converts into Joules. Joules are distributed to members based on participation (not purchase volume). They retain value. They can be used in transactions. And critically, they cannot be cashed out to fiat currency.

That last point matters. Joules are a one-way valve. Dollars flow in (as Credits). Surplus crystallizes (as Joules). But Joules never flow back out as dollars. This prevents the cooperative from being hollowed out by members extracting surplus as cash. The wealth stays in the system.

**Problem 3: How do you make interdependence economically rational?**

A single-currency system makes independence rational. If I can earn dollars elsewhere, why participate in a cooperative? The three-currency system changes the calculation.

Credits handle commerce — same as anywhere. But Marks give governance weight that money can't buy. And Joules give long-term value that only the cooperative generates. A member who participates deeply in Liana Banyan accumulates assets (Marks, Joules) that have no equivalent outside the cooperative. Leaving the cooperative means leaving those assets behind.

This isn't lock-in. It's alignment. The three currencies make it economically rational to invest effort in the cooperative rather than extracting value from it. The more you contribute, the more governance power you hold (Marks), the more surplus you share (Joules), and the more the cooperative works in your favor.

**The wallet view**

In the member's Wallet page, all three currencies appear in a unified view. Credits show a spendable balance with transaction history. Marks show an accumulated total with a breakdown by source (commerce, governance, community). Joules show a reserve balance with a distribution history.

The visual hierarchy was designed by Pawn (B30): Credits get the most visual weight because they're the most frequently used. Marks get a prominent secondary position because they represent the member's standing. Joules get a quieter but permanent display because they represent long-term value.

Three currencies. One wallet. One cooperative. And every dollar of surplus stays in the system.
