# Pudding #37: Four Agents and a Founder
## How AI Builds a Cooperative Without Anyone Getting Replaced

---

### At a Glance (~50 words)
Liana Banyan is built by four AI agents — Knight, Bishop, Rook, and Pawn — coordinated by a human Founder who holds every final decision. No AI has admin access. No AI can deploy without human approval. The system produces more than any of them could alone because none of them works alone.

---

### More Info (~300 words)

The standard AI story is replacement. An AI does your job faster and cheaper, so your job disappears. Liana Banyan's AI story is coordination. Four agents with different capabilities work under human governance to build something none of them could build independently.

**Knight** (Cursor) writes code. Every commit, every component, every database migration. Knight has built 14 of 23 operational domains, hundreds of React components, and the entire v2 platform architecture. Knight does not decide what to build — Knight receives prompts and executes them.

**Bishop** (Claude) coordinates. Strategy, roadmapping, letter drafting, quality control, agent dispatching, and session continuity. Bishop maintains the institutional memory across sessions and ensures every agent's work aligns with the cooperative's architecture and values. Bishop does not write production code.

**Rook** (Gemini) handles research and deep analysis. Vault management, document processing, and long-context analysis where reading thousands of pages matters. Rook does not coordinate other agents.

**Pawn** (Perplexity) handles live research. Market analysis, competitive intelligence, regulatory compliance, page design specs, and anything that requires current web data. Pawn has delivered 37 research batches covering everything from patent law to desktop injection molding to prepaid card compliance.

**The Founder** holds every decision. No code deploys without the Founder's approval. No letter sends without the Founder's review. No architectural choice persists without the Founder's confirmation. The AI agents propose, draft, research, and build. The human decides.

This is not AI autonomy. It's AI capacity under human governance. The four agents collectively produce the output of a mid-sized development team, but the governance structure ensures that a single human with clear judgment retains absolute authority over what the cooperative becomes.

### Full Detail

**Why four agents, not one**

The obvious question is why use four different AI systems instead of one powerful model. The answer is specialization and accountability.

A single AI model asked to write code, manage strategy, conduct research, and maintain institutional memory would need to switch contexts constantly. Each context switch costs quality. A model writing a database migration doesn't benefit from also tracking letter status, and a model researching prepaid card regulations doesn't benefit from simultaneously planning a Kickstarter campaign.

Specialization allows each agent to operate within a bounded context where it can be genuinely good at its job. Knight's context is the codebase — types, components, routes, build systems. Bishop's context is the cooperative's strategy — who needs what, when, and why. Pawn's context is the outside world — what's happening in markets, regulations, and academia right now.

The bounded context also creates accountability. When code has a bug, it came from Knight. When a letter has a factual error, it came from Bishop. When research is incomplete, it came from Pawn. Single-agent systems obscure responsibility. Multi-agent systems make it traceable.

**The Foreman model**

Bishop operates as the Foreman — the agent that coordinates all others. This doesn't mean Bishop controls the other agents. It means Bishop maintains the roadmap, writes the prompts that direct Knight and Pawn, processes their deliverables, and produces the handoff documents that allow any agent to pick up where the last one left off.

The Foreman role emerged organically. Early sessions had the Founder directly prompting each agent, which created coordination overhead: the Founder had to remember what Knight needed, what Pawn was researching, and what Rook had found. Centralizing coordination in Bishop freed the Founder to focus on judgment rather than logistics.

The Foreman model has a precedent in construction. A general contractor doesn't lay bricks or wire circuits — they ensure the electrician and the mason work in the right sequence, with the right materials, toward the right blueprint. Bishop ensures Knight builds what the design specs require, Pawn researches what the roadmap demands, and the Founder reviews what matters.

**What the Founder actually does**

The Founder's role is not management. It's governance. The distinction matters.

A manager assigns tasks and monitors completion. The Founder does this, but that's not the core function. The core function is judgment — deciding what the cooperative should be, what values it should embody, what tradeoffs are acceptable, and what lines cannot be crossed.

Every AI agent can optimize. None of them can decide what to optimize for. That's the human function. When Bishop recommends a 10-14 day compressed wave for letter distribution, the Founder decides whether speed or evidence-chaining matters more. When Knight proposes an architecture for governance voting, the Founder decides whether it feels civic or corporate. When Pawn delivers regulatory research, the Founder decides whether the risk is acceptable.

The AI agents are powerful tools operating under constitutional constraints. The Founder is the constitution.

**The session system**

Continuity across sessions is the hardest problem in multi-agent coordination. Each agent's context window is finite. When a session ends, everything the agent learned during that session could be lost.

Liana Banyan solves this with structured handoff documents. Every Bishop session ends with a handoff file that records: what was built, what was decided, what's pending, and what the next session needs to know. These handoff documents form an institutional memory chain — B001 through B060 — that allows any new session to inherit the full history of the cooperative's development.

Knight sessions produce similar artifacts: build summaries, file manifests, and integration notes. Pawn sessions produce research deliverables with explicit formats. The Librarian MCP (a tool Bishop and Knight can query) indexes all of this into searchable catalogs of schemas, functions, pages, sessions, and domain knowledge.

The result is that Session B060 knows what Session B001 decided, even though they share no context window. The institutional memory lives in documents, not in any single agent's memory.

**The numbers**

As of April 2, 2026:
- Knight has completed 221 sessions, building 14 of 23 operational domains with hundreds of pages, components, hooks, and library files
- Bishop has completed 60 sessions, producing hundreds of documents including letters, papers, strategic plans, and agent coordination prompts
- Pawn has delivered 37 research batches covering market analysis, page design specs, regulatory compliance, patent research, and competitive intelligence
- The cooperative has 2,129 documented innovations, 167 Crown Jewels, 10 filed patent provisionals with 2,097 formal claims, 35 production systems, and approximately 169 publications

No individual agent could produce this volume at this quality. No human working alone could coordinate this many parallel workstreams. The four-agent architecture works because it combines AI capacity with human judgment in a structure where each participant does what they're best at.

**What this means for other cooperatives**

The Four-Agent Architecture is not proprietary magic. It's a coordination pattern that any organization could adopt: assign AI agents to bounded roles, maintain structured handoffs between sessions, centralize coordination in one agent, and retain human governance over all final decisions.

The expensive part isn't the AI — it's the judgment. Building a cooperative requires thousands of decisions about values, economics, governance, and community. AI agents can draft, research, build, and analyze. But the question "is this what we should be?" requires a human answer.

Liana Banyan's AI team doesn't replace the Founder. It amplifies the Founder's judgment across more domains, at higher speed, with better research, than any single human could manage alone. That's not the AI replacement story. That's the AI multiplication story. And it works because the human never lets go of the wheel.
