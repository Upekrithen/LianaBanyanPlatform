# Pudding #38: The Roommate Contract
## How Stamps, Grace Periods, and Three Levels of Appeal Make Shared Living Accountable

---

### At a Glance (~50 words)
Liana Banyan's Roommate Accountability System turns the oldest conflict in shared living — "you never do your chores" — into a structured, evidence-based process with photo stamps, grace periods, and a three-level appeal system that goes all the way to external arbitration. No landlord needed. No shouting matches required.

---

### More Info (~300 words)

Every shared living arrangement eventually hits the same wall. Someone doesn't clean. Someone doesn't pay. And the resolution process is either passive-aggressive silence or an explosive argument. There is no system. There is no record. There is no appeal.

The Roommate Accountability System replaces all of that with structure. Here's how it works.

**Agreements come first.** Before any accountability kicks in, roommates create a shared agreement that defines expectations. What chores exist. Who does what. How often. What level of commitment applies — daily, twice weekly, weekly, or as needed. The agreement is visible to all parties. No ambiguity.

**Stamps create evidence.** When a roommate notices an issue — dishes left in the sink, trash not taken out, a mess in a shared space — they file a stamp. A stamp includes the category (cleanliness, noise, shared space, payment, or other), a description, and a photo. The photo is the evidence. The timestamp is the record.

**Grace periods prevent abuse.** A stamp doesn't immediately count against anyone. There's a grace period — typically 24-48 hours — during which the person can address the issue before it becomes part of their accountability record. The system rewards fixing problems quickly, not catching people in the act.

**Scores reflect patterns, not incidents.** Each roommate has an accountability score computed from their stamp history. A single stamp doesn't destroy a score. Repeated patterns over weeks do. The score reflects whether someone consistently meets their commitments, not whether they had one bad day.

**Three levels of appeal exist for disputes.** If someone believes a stamp was unfair, they can appeal — first to a property steward, then to a cooperative ombudsperson, and finally to AAA arbitration. The system never traps anyone in a judgment with no recourse.

### Full Detail

**Why photo evidence matters**

The single biggest problem with roommate disputes is the he-said-she-said dynamic. One person says the kitchen was dirty. The other says it wasn't. There is no record, so the dispute becomes about credibility rather than facts.

Photo stamps eliminate this entirely. A photo of dishes piled in a sink at 11 PM on a Tuesday is a fact, not an interpretation. A photo of a clean kitchen the next morning is also a fact. The system doesn't need to determine who is telling the truth because the evidence exists independently of both parties.

This is not surveillance. Stamps are filed by roommates about shared spaces, not by cameras or automated monitoring. The person filing the stamp chooses what to document and when. The person receiving the stamp can see exactly what was documented and respond.

**The commitment tier system**

Not all chores are equal, and not all roommates have the same capacity. The agreement system recognizes four commitment levels:

- **Daily** — expected every day (7 days/week). Examples: taking out trash, cleaning cooking surfaces after use.
- **Twice weekly** — expected roughly every 3-4 days. Examples: vacuuming shared areas, bathroom cleaning.
- **Weekly** — expected once per week. Examples: deep cleaning, grocery shopping for shared supplies.
- **As needed** — no fixed schedule. Examples: replacing shared supplies, fixing broken items.

The commitment level affects how stamps are weighted. A missed daily task generates a smaller per-incident weight than a missed weekly task, because daily tasks have more opportunities for recovery. Someone who misses trash duty once in seven days has a different pattern than someone who misses their weekly clean entirely.

**The accountability score**

Each roommate's score is computed from their stamp history, weighted by:
- Category severity (payment stamps weigh more than cleanliness stamps)
- Frequency (patterns weigh more than isolated incidents)
- Grace period compliance (resolving issues within the grace period reduces or eliminates the stamp's weight)
- Appeal outcomes (stamps overturned on appeal are removed from the calculation)

The score is visible to the roommate and to other parties in the agreement. It is not visible to anyone outside the agreement. A roommate's accountability score in one property does not follow them to a different property unless they choose to share it.

**The three-level appeal process**

Every stamp can be contested. The appeal process has three levels, each with increasing formality and independence:

**Level 1: Steward Review.** The property's designated housing steward reviews the stamp and the appeal. The steward is a cooperative member who volunteers for the role — they are not a landlord, manager, or authority figure. They review the photo evidence, the roommate agreement, and both parties' statements. Most disputes resolve here.

**Level 2: Ombudsperson Review.** If the steward's decision doesn't resolve the dispute, either party can escalate to a cooperative-level ombudsperson. The ombudsperson is independent — they don't live in the property and have no relationship with either party. They review the full record including the steward's decision.

**Level 3: AAA Arbitration.** If the ombudsperson's decision doesn't resolve the dispute, either party can request external arbitration through the American Arbitration Association. This is binding. It involves a professional arbitrator outside the cooperative entirely. It exists so that no one is ever trapped inside a system where the cooperative is both judge and party.

The three-level structure balances accessibility with accountability. Most disputes resolve at Level 1 because the steward review is fast and local. But the existence of Level 3 ensures that the system has external legitimacy — it cannot be captured by insiders or biased by relationships.

**What this system replaces**

In traditional shared living:
- Disputes are resolved by whoever is louder, more persistent, or more willing to escalate
- Evidence is nonexistent or anecdotal
- Appeals go to a landlord who may have their own agenda
- The tenant with less power (financial, social, linguistic) loses by default

In cooperative shared living with the Roommate Accountability System:
- Disputes are resolved through evidence and structured review
- Photo stamps create a factual record independent of personality dynamics
- Appeals go through three levels, ending in external arbitration
- The system is the same regardless of who has more power

The system doesn't pretend roommate conflicts won't happen. It ensures that when they happen, there's a fair, documented, escalating process to resolve them — one that works the same for everyone.

**The escrow dimension**

When a roommate agreement involves financial commitments — shared rent, utilities, supplies — the cooperative can hold a small escrow amount. If a roommate fails to meet financial obligations and stamps accumulate, the escrow provides a buffer. If the roommate meets their commitments, the escrow is returned in full.

The escrow amount is defined in the agreement, visible to all parties, and subject to the same three-level appeal process. It cannot be seized without due process. It exists to protect roommates from each other's defaults, not to punish anyone in advance.
