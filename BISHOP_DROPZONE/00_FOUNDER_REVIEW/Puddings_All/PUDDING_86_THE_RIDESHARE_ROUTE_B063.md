# Pudding #86 — The Rideshare Route

*What if the driver kept 83.3% and the rider knew exactly where the other 16.7% went?*

---

A rideshare driver on a conventional platform picks up a passenger for a $25 fare. The platform takes 25-40%. The driver receives $15-$19. The passenger has no idea how much the driver actually earned, and the driver has no idea how the platform calculated its cut.

On Liana Banyan's Rideshare Routes, the math is on the label. The driver enters their actual gas cost for the route. The rider sees that cost multiplied by the platform fee percentage — default 20%. On a route where the driver's gas cost is $8, the rider pays $9.60. The driver keeps $8. The platform's share is $1.60. No surge pricing. No algorithmic fare manipulation. No mystery math.

The driver keeps 83.3% of the transaction value. The platform takes Cost+20%. These are not adjustable knobs that change when demand spikes or when the platform needs to hit quarterly targets. They are structural constants — the same percentage on a Tuesday afternoon as on New Year's Eve.

This creates a different kind of rideshare. Routes are not dynamically priced based on demand. They are priced based on the actual cost of providing the ride. A driver who knows their gas cost can tell a rider exactly what the fare will be before the trip starts. No estimates. No ranges. No post-trip surprises.

The safety ledger logs every match — driver, rider, route, timestamp. Both parties have access to the record. If something goes wrong, the evidence exists. If nothing goes wrong, the record builds the driver's ADAPT score over time, which functions as a portable reputation that the driver owns and controls.

The constraint — Cost+20%, no surge, transparent math — is the competitive advantage. It is not competitive on price against a subsidized platform burning venture capital to acquire market share. It is competitive on trust. A driver who can predict their earnings and a rider who can see the math will tolerate a slightly higher fare in exchange for knowing that the system is not designed to extract from either of them.

Rideshare Routes does not try to replace Uber. It provides an alternative for communities where people would rather pay a neighbor a fair price than pay a corporation a hidden one.
