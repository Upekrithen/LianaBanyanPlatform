## Who motivates the motivator?

There's a saying I've carried with me for a long time. I don't remember exactly where I picked it up — some of it's mine, some of it was handed to me, most of it is worn smooth from use:

> **"Who motivates the motivator? The motivator does."**

In the military we called this *opening a can of false enthusiasm*. You're cold, you're tired, you haven't slept, the next twelve hours are going to be worse than the last twelve, and someone has to stand up first. So you do. You *choose* to. Not because you feel it. Because you decided to feel it.

The older I get the more I think that's most of the game. Not talent. Not luck. Not even hard work. **The willingness to choose, today, to be motivated** — when nothing around you is providing any — is the single rarest thing I've seen in adults, and the single most reliable predictor of who ends up building a life they chose on purpose.

I am writing this essay to tell you how I motivate myself. I am writing it because I'd like more people to do it. And I am writing it because Liana Banyan is, in large part, a machine for making this choice easier and more rewarding for people who've been told their whole lives that motivation is something that's supposed to *happen to them*.

It isn't. You do it. And here's the math I use.

## The Triple Double

I have a number taped up in my office. You can see it in the video of my one-take office tour if you ever watch it — right there on the wall while Brucklyn's song is playing. The number is this:

> **$100 a day, five days a week, forty-eight weeks a year.**

Do the math. That's **$24,000 a year**. Not a fortune. Not "retire on it" money. But: concretely imaginable. Anyone reading this can picture how $100 in a day happens. One oil change referred. Four Cue Cards sold. A single small repair. A morning of tutoring. An afternoon of photos. It's not a wish. It's a *task*.

That's the base. Then I double it three times.

| Rung | Math | Annual | What it means |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Rung 0** | $100/day × 5 × 48 | **$24,000** | The ladder begins. Concretely imaginable. Reachable by anyone willing to choose. |
| **Rung 1** | 2× | **$48,000** | A real living. Around the median household income of many American families. |
| **Rung 2** | 2× | **$96,000** | Well above median. Savings actually happen. Choices open up. |
| **Rung 3** | 2× | **$192,000** | The capability to act in the real world. Not rich, exactly — but *capable*. |

That's the **Triple Double.** Three doublings from a concretely imaginable base. The top rung isn't a million dollars. The top rung is **$192,000 a year**, and I want to be honest with you about what that number is and isn't.

It isn't "I need it." I don't need $192,000 a year to live. Most people don't. You can live a genuinely good life on a lot less.

It is **the number where ambition stops being cosplay and starts having consequences.** At $192K a year, the thing I want to work on — I can work on. The person I want to help — I can help. The problem I want to take a swing at — I can actually swing at it. Not think about it, not talk about it, *do it*. Not because I'm rich, but because I am no longer *fragile*. That's what that number means to me. It is the rung where capability becomes real.

Also: notice what I did with the weeks. **Forty-eight.** Not fifty-two. I built four weeks of rest into the plan at the bottom rung and every rung above it. And five days, not seven. Because burnout isn't a badge, it's a leak. You can't climb a ladder you're too tired to grip. I want you to hit these numbers and still be alive enough to enjoy what comes next.

## Why doubles, not addition

I get asked sometimes why the ladder works by doubling instead of adding. The reason is psychological, not mathematical.

When you ask someone to go from $24K to $192K directly, their brain says *"that's eight times what I make now and I don't see the path."* They're right to shut down. The gap is paralyzing.

When you ask someone to *double* what they already did last year, their brain says *"I already did half of that. I know how. I just need more of it, or a slightly better version of it."* That's a tractable problem. That's a Monday-morning problem.

Every rung is just asking *"do what you already did, and then do it again."* Compound thinking. Same math Warren Buffett has been preaching for sixty years. Same math the retirement calculators use. Same math the universe actually runs on.

The lottery tells you to jump eight rungs in one buy. The Triple Double tells you to step onto the next rung once you're standing on the one below. One of these works. The other one doesn't.

## Swing for the Fences

Next to my $100/day sign there's another saying. Same office, same wall. You can see it in the video too:

> **Swing for the Fences.**

Here's the thing about Babe Ruth. He hit **714 home runs**, a record that stood for nearly forty years. He is one of the most iconic home-run hitters in the history of the sport. Everyone knows this.

Here is what fewer people know: Babe Ruth also **struck out 1,330 times.** When he retired, he held the career strikeout record too. The guy whose name is synonymous with the home run was also the guy who missed more than anyone had ever missed before.

Does anyone care about the strikeouts? No. We put him in the Hall of Fame for the home runs. The strikeouts were the *cost of being willing to swing hard*.

I have the same pattern in my own life. My chess stats tell me I've lost **more than half** the games I've played. Two screenshots, months apart, both say the same thing. I lose more than I win. And I have kept getting better, and I mostly hit home runs now on the stuff that matters, because **I did all the losing first**, and the losing was how I learned.

Warren Buffett tells a story about Ted Williams waiting for the perfect pitch — and that's good advice if you're being paid to be cautious. But there's another story, and it's the one we published a whole paper on in the Liana Banyan library: *"What If You Had Unlimited Throws?"* You're at the carnival dart game. You pay once. The guy running the booth tells you to throw as many darts as you want until you hit the target. **How careful are you going to be on throw number one?**

That's the game we're actually playing. Not Ted Williams in the batter's box waiting for the one pitch of a lifetime — but the carnival dart game with unlimited throws. Every Cue Card you send is a throw. Every Treasure Map you scatter is a throw. Every project you pitch is a throw. Every Pudding you write is a throw. The only way to guarantee you never hit is to stop throwing.

## No Effort Is Wasted

Above the Swing for the Fences sign, on the Helm dashboard we're building, there's going to be a third line. It's a quote from Calvin's dad — from the Calvin and Hobbes comic strip my father used to read to me out of the Sunday paper. Calvin is upset about something not going the way he planned, and his dad says, more or less:

> **"When you don't get what you want, you get experience."**

We have a whole Pudding on this already — **Pudding #24, "No Effort Is Wasted."** I'm not going to re-litigate it here. The short version is: every throw that misses is teaching your arm how not to miss that way again. The strikeouts are paying for the home runs in knowledge, not in dollars. You don't get to skip the strikeouts. You do get to count them, and look at the pile of them, and know that each one bought you something.

On the Triple Double panel on your Helm, there's going to be a counter. It counts your **attempts this month** — not your wins. Cue Cards sent. Projects launched. Campaigns fired. Throws taken. It only goes up. The header on the counter is *No Effort Is Wasted.* Click the header and Pudding #24 opens.

You don't need to hit every throw. You need to keep throwing. The ladder takes care of the rest.

## The Lottery Ticket Monkeys

Now here's where I want to talk about the thing most people do instead of this, and why it breaks my heart a little every time I see it.

> *"When I win the lottery, I won't tell anyone — but there will be signs."*
>
> *I'll go to the dentist.*
> *I'll pay off the car.*
> *I'll make the house payment on time.*
> *I'll go to the doctor for that thing I've been ignoring.*
> *I'll get my kid's glasses updated.*
> *I'll finally fix the roof.*

You've seen this joke in twenty variations. It's funny because it's true, and it's true because **most people aren't trying to be billionaires. They're trying to live a good life.** Nobody in that list of signs is buying a yacht. Nobody is buying a private island. Nobody is buying a sports team. They're buying *a dentist appointment they've been putting off*.

And here's the hard fact:

> **The average American spends about $300 a year on lottery tickets.** For the bottom third of earners — the people who can least afford it — it's closer to $400 or more. The odds of winning a major jackpot are roughly **1 in 300 million**. You are more likely to be struck by lightning twice.

Years ago there was a commercial I've never been able to forget. I believe it was for Indeed.com, the job site, though I'd welcome corrections from anyone with a sharper memory. The commercial showed a group of monkeys in suits sitting around a corporate boardroom table. The voiceover explained that their entire business plan was *to buy lottery tickets*. The joke was obvious. You do not run a company on lottery tickets. No serious person would. No investor would put a dime into a business whose quarterly report was *"we bought scratchers."*

So why do we accept it as a personal financial plan?

There's an old comic strip — I'm almost sure it was **Bloom County**, from Berkeley Breathed, in the early 1980s, though it might have been Calvin and Hobbes and my memory is lying to me — where one of the characters was selling *day-old lottery tickets at half price*. The joke landed because the odds of winning had **only slightly decreased** from what they were before the drawing was held. You're selling a near-identical product the day after at a discount because functionally nothing changed — it was worthless in both states, just more formally worthless the day after.

That joke is about thirty years old and it is still the single sharpest thing anyone has ever said about the lottery. *The ticket was already expired when you bought it.*

## What to do with $2,000–$5,000

Here is a question I get asked a lot by people I know in real life. They've managed to save up something. Two thousand dollars. Sometimes five thousand. Sometimes a little more. They want to know **what to do with it.**

The options they've been told about are:

1. **Put it in the stock market** — historical returns around 7% real per year, so your $5,000 becomes about $350 in a year, assuming no crash. You have no lever over the outcome. You are a passenger.
2. **Buy a lottery ticket** — see above. Monkeys.
3. **Buy crypto** — a lottery ticket wearing a lab coat.
4. **Leave it in a savings account** — watch inflation eat it slowly.
5. **"Invest in yourself"** — which sounds great but is meaningless without a specific plan.

Every single one of those options turns you into a *consumer of returns*. You put capital in. You wait. Something happens to the capital without you. Maybe it grows, maybe it shrinks, you don't really get to affect it either way.

Here's what I tell them instead. Every single time:

> **Start a business.**

Not because it's guaranteed to work — nothing is. Because it has **more leverage than any other thing you can do with that money**, and the leverage is in *your own hands*. You get to do what you already do for a living, *and* more of what you actually want to do, and you get to see the effect of your effort immediately. A good month and you know it was a good month by Friday, not by the next quarterly statement.

Nothing I have ever seen has more upside, more satisfaction, more *dignity* than turning your own labor into something you own. Index funds give you 7% a year. A business you run gives you **a life you chose on purpose.** Those are not comparable products.

But here's the problem — and it's the reason most people don't take my advice — **that $2,000 to $5,000 is really, really hard for most people to replace if they lose it.** If your first try at the business fails (and most first tries do fail), you may not get a second try for a long time. The lottery tickets are cheaper per attempt. The stock market gives you diversification. Starting a business is all-in, and the all-in is scary in direct proportion to how much you actually need the money.

**That is exactly why Liana Banyan exists.**

Our whole Cold Start pathway is designed to let you **perfect the business one piece at a time** *before* you put your capital to work:

- **Cue Cards** let you test demand in Marks — effort only — before you spend a dollar on inventory. You find out if people actually want the thing before you make the thing.
- **Treasure Maps** let you crowdsource discovery of whether your idea already has a waiting audience somewhere.
- **Campaign Forge** lets you build the pitch and the materials using shared platform tools instead of paying an agency.
- **The Red Carpet** connects you to people who've already done what you're trying to do, so you stop making the expensive mistakes they already made.
- **The Recipe Pot** lets you assemble a team with the exact skills you're missing, paying in Marks first.
- **The Commerce Engine** handles the boring stuff — payments, ledger, compliance — so you can focus on the actual work.

You come to Liana Banyan with your $2,000. You **don't spend it.** You use the platform to validate the idea, find the first buyers, line up the production, and stress-test the whole thing — all in Marks, all in effort, all before a dollar of your real money is at risk. And *then*, when the platform's tools have told you honestly whether the idea actually works, you deploy the capital with confidence.

You earn your way to the starting line. And then when you step onto it, **you already know you can win the race.**

That's not a new kind of economics. That's just how people who start businesses the old way have always worked — except the old way required a network, a mentor, and a lot of luck to access. We made it a public utility. Anyone can walk in the door.

## The choice

So here's where this all lands.

You can play the lottery — and the overwhelming math says you will lose $300 a year forever and your dentist appointment will not happen.

You can buy the index fund — and the math says you will get about 7% a year from something you have no control over, and you will never, not once, get to say *"I built that."*

Or you can **choose, today, to be the kind of person who throws darts at the carnival target all day.** Not because you feel like it. Not because you're inspired. Because **you decided to**. Because who motivates the motivator? The motivator does.

You pick your base. $100 a day if that's right for you; $50 if that's more honest; $200 if you're already working at a pace that justifies it. You double it three times in your head and you know what the top of the ladder looks like. You Swing for the Fences every day and you watch the attempts counter climb even on the days nothing lands. You remember that **no effort is wasted**, because the strikeouts are paying for the home runs in the only currency that matters — *what you learned*. And when you have a little capital, you come to Liana Banyan and you use the Cold Start tools to make sure the first swing with your real money is one you already know how to take.

That is the whole program.

**Hit the Triple Double. Swing for the Fences. No effort is wasted.**

I have those three sayings taped up in my office because I need them too. I am not writing this from a mountaintop. I am writing this from the same place you are reading it — somebody trying, most days choosing to, some days not, always going again.

If you got this far, I want to ask you something directly. **What would you work on, if you knew the capability to do it was coming?** Write it down. Put it somewhere you'll see it. Come back to Liana Banyan tomorrow and we'll both work on it together.

That's what the ladder is for. That's what the Board is for. That's what we're building.
