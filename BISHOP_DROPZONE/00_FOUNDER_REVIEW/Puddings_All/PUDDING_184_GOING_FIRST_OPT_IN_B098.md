## I. The Promise First, Then the Explanation

Before anything else I want to make a promise, in writing, that I intend to keep: **I will not ask a single member of this platform to document the origin of their own contribution until I have finished documenting the origin of all 2,248 of mine.**

Not the cleaned-up version. Not the pitch-deck version. The real version — the one with the specific Tuesday, the specific kitchen, the specific person at the counter, the specific frustration that made me sit down and write the first version of what eventually became the innovation.

I am going first. I am going first on all of them. And I am going to be very clear about what that does and does not mean for you.

It does not mean that when you join Liana Banyan, you will be handed a form and told to fill it out. It does not mean that contributions will be held up at some gate while you prove you can tell a story about where the contribution came from. It does not mean there is a rule, a requirement, a compliance step, or a hoop.

It means exactly one thing: **if you ever choose to document the moment behind one of your contributions, the platform will be ready for it, because I will have already built the road and walked it first with all of mine.**

Everything else in this essay is an explanation of why I am doing this, how it works, who benefits, and what the opt-in choice looks like if you want it. If the short version is enough, you can stop reading here. The long version is for people who want to understand the mechanism.

## II. The Problem Platforms Have With Their Own Origins

Every platform eventually becomes a museum.

Not intentionally. Nobody sits down and says, let's build something hollow. It happens gradually, the way a kitchen drawer fills with batteries you're not sure are dead and menus from restaurants that closed. Features accumulate. The original urgency fades. The people who remembered why a thing was built move on, and what's left is the feature itself — orphaned from its origin, defended by inertia.

I have watched this happen to platforms I admired. I have watched communities rally around tools they no longer understand, maintaining them out of habit rather than conviction. I have watched founders give interviews about their "vision" in language so abstracted from lived experience that you would never guess the thing was once a solution to a real, specific, human problem.

I do not want that for this platform.

Liana Banyan was not born in a conference room. It was not born in a pitch deck or a whiteboard session or a strategic offsite. It was born in a series of moments — frustrating, clarifying, sometimes embarrassing moments — that I experienced in kitchens, hardware stores, community centers, ravines with bad cell service, and rooms with more open journals than any reasonable person would keep on a single desk. Every innovation on this platform traces back to a real situation, a real friction, a real need that I or someone I was standing next to actually felt.

But here is the thing I have come to understand about origin stories: they do not preserve themselves.

Unless you pin them down, they drift. They get smoothed out in the retelling, simplified for the pitch, romanticized for the press release. The specific becomes the general. The real becomes the mythic. And once that happens, you have lost the most important thing a founder can offer a platform — the unedited truth of why it exists.

This essay is about the mechanism I am building to prevent that, and about the invitation that sits at the end of it for anyone who wants to participate.

## III. What I Mean by "Going First"

"Going First" is not a branding exercise and it is not a membership requirement. It is a personal commitment with a platform-level benefit.

Here is what the personal commitment looks like in practice:

For every innovation currently on this platform — and we are currently tracking 2,248 distinct innovations across sixteen active initiatives — I am mapping each one back to the specific moment it came from, writing down the anecdote in plain first-person language, finding or taking the photograph that anchors the moment, and publishing all of it to the platform's public record.

Not a stock photo. Not an illustration. A real photograph, timestamped where possible, from the actual context that gave rise to the need. In many cases the photograph is already in one of the eighteen open physical journals on my desk right now. In a few cases the photograph is a new one I'm taking deliberately in the room where the original thing happened. In the cases where no photograph exists and no honest retrospective image can be made, I am writing down that absence and explaining it — because the absence is also a kind of truth.

Why a photograph? Because photographs are hard to retcon.

You can revise a written anecdote. You can soften the edges, add detail you didn't notice at the time, reframe the protagonist. Memory is famously plastic. But a photograph taken in the moment — a cluttered counter, a broken tool, a handwritten list, a crowded community meeting — is a commitment device. It says: this is what it actually looked like. This is the real thing.

The reason the commitment is mine and not everyone's is that I am the one who built the platform. It is my responsibility to document what I built and why. It is nobody else's.

## IV. Why I Don't Want Anyone Else To Be Required To Do This

Let me say this as plainly as I can.

**If you join Liana Banyan, contribute to Liana Banyan, build a business on Liana Banyan, earn Credits or Marks or Joules on Liana Banyan, or become a Patron on Liana Banyan, you will never be required to document the origin of any of it.**

I want that on the record because I have watched, in other places, how "documentation" turns into a mechanism for control. A documentation requirement gets added with good intentions, grows in scope, becomes a filter, becomes a compliance layer, becomes a way for the platform to deny service to people who didn't have the time or the interest to write an essay about their own work. I will not build that, and I will not let anyone build that inside this platform on my watch.

There are people who want to document the story of their contribution — because they love telling it, because they think it might help someone else, because they want the record preserved, because the moment mattered to them and they want it seen. **For those people, this essay is the beginning of an invitation.** I am going first so that when they want to go second, third, four thousandth, the road exists.

There are also people who do not want to document any of that. Who just want to run their food truck, post their listings, cash their Marks, sleep in a real bed, and live their life. **For those people, nothing about what I am doing changes anything.** They are full cooperative members in every sense that matters. Their contributions count exactly the same. Their earnings count exactly the same. Their votes count exactly the same. Their place at the Family Table counts exactly the same. The documentation layer is transparent to them — it does not exist in their experience of the platform unless they choose to open that door.

This is the shape of a cooperative. The cooperative owes each member the same rights and the same floor of respect regardless of whether they want to be a storyteller, an operator, a craftsman, a ghost, or any combination. Not a two-tier system where documented members are superior and undocumented members are second class. No. **Full membership. No asterisk.**

## V. What You Get If You Do Opt In

If you decide, at some point, that you want to document the origin of one of your contributions the way I am documenting mine, here is what the platform will give you in return. These are benefits, not obligations. They do not activate unless you choose to walk through the door.

**First, named attribution.** Your anecdote, your photograph, and your name stay attached to the innovation, the business, the pudding, or whatever the artifact is. For as long as the platform exists, the record says: this is what this person noticed, this is where it came from, this is what it became. That is a form of persistent credit that no extractive platform has ever offered an operator at your scale.

**Second, co-authorship on anything Liana Banyan builds on top of your contribution.** If Bishop writes a Pudding that draws on your documented anecdote, you are credited as a co-source. If a paper gets written that cites the pattern you documented, you are cited in the reference list. If a Cue Card, a Cold Start Pathway, a Family Table configuration, or a Captain System template traces back to your moment, the record connects you to it. You built the thing. The platform's record should say so, and when you opt in, it does.

**Third, Service Allocation Authority accrual for the contribution itself.** SAA is the way the cooperative recognizes a member's demonstrated judgment and gives them more ability to direct cooperative resources over time. Documented contributions are legibly trackable, which means they can accrue SAA in ways that undocumented contributions cannot — not because undocumented work is worth less, but because there is no public evidence trail for the cooperative's governance mechanisms to work from. Opting in is how you make yourself *legible* to the governance layer. That is a concrete material benefit, denominated in allocation authority, that can compound over the life of your participation.

**Fourth, eligibility to be named in academic research as a case study subject.** Liana Banyan is building itself as a living laboratory for research on cooperative economics, platform governance, peer-proximity expertise, and every topic a consented cooperative population can inform. Researchers from the outside — Scholz, Brynjolfsson, Scott, the INDL network, and anyone else we open the door to — are going to want to cite real members doing real work. If you have opted in, you are in the pool from which those citations are drawn, and you are named, attributed, and (where appropriate) co-authored. If you have not opted in, you are invisible to the research layer, which is exactly how you should be if that is your preference.

**Fifth, the thing that is harder to quantify but I think matters most: your story becomes part of the cooperative's memory.** Ten years from now, when a new member is trying to figure out how someone at their rung made the leap to the next one, they will be able to read your documented moment instead of a sanitized case study written by a consultant who never met you. That's the real gift. The new member isn't the beneficiary alone — you are too. Your own memory of what happened is preserved in a form that survives you. I cannot offer you immortality. I can offer you a durable, searchable, attributable record of what you did and why, which is the nearest thing any platform has ever been able to honestly promise.

Those are the benefits. They are real. They are available to anyone who wants them. They activate on an individual, per-contribution basis — you can opt in for some contributions and not others, you can change your mind later, you can request aggregation instead of attribution, and you can withdraw any specific anecdote from the research-eligibility pool without losing the other benefits. The consent is granular because the choice is yours.

## VI. Why I Am Going First, Specifically

I think there are three reasons a founder should go first on something like this, and I want to name all three because each one is load-bearing on its own.

**The first reason is pedagogical.** If I want members to feel safe opting in — if I want them to trust that documentation is a gift and not a trap — I have to show them what it looks like when somebody already did it. 2,248 documented anecdotes, each one paired with a photograph or an honest note about the photograph's absence, is a visible corpus they can look at before they decide whether to join it. The first person who opts in after me gets to see a walked road. Not a proposed road. An actual, publicly-readable corpus of one Founder's full documented origin, in his own voice, with his own photographs, published and in place. The pedagogy is the corpus itself, not the lecture about the corpus.

**The second reason is epistemological.** Every paper I write — and at the time of this essay I have thirty-nine papers in the corpus, with two more in draft and several more queued — makes claims about how cooperative economics work, how peer-proximity expertise flows, how platform architecture shapes outcomes. Those claims need to be falsifiable. If I cannot point to the real moments that informed them, the papers are just a founder telling stories into a microphone. **If I can point to the real moments, in public, with photographs and anecdotes that anyone can check, the papers become something closer to preliminary hypothesis documents with live empirical tests attached.** That is a materially different epistemic status than "founder whitepaper." Going first is the work that earns the right to make the claims the papers make.

**The third reason is moral.** I am asking a lot of the people who join this platform. I am asking them to trust a cooperative economy that their neighbors still find confusing. I am asking them to trade the VC-backed convenience they are used to for a member-owned architecture that they have to learn. I am asking them to show up, operate real businesses, earn real money, feed real families, under a structure they have had very little time to test against their own experience. In exchange for all that, the least I can do is show them that when I ask them to do something, I am already doing it myself, at scale, in public, without flinching. I am not asking them to be vulnerable in a way I am unwilling to be vulnerable. I am just going first.

## VII. What "Already Doing It" Looks Like Right Now

You don't have to take my word for the claim that I am already doing this. If you visit the Liana Banyan site, there is a three-minute unedited video on the site called *Founder's Office One-Take Unedited.* It is the room I work in. The camera walks through it in a single unbroken take, no cuts, no retakes, no polish. You can count approximately eighteen open physical journals visible on the desk and shelves as the camera moves. Each one is a different era or purpose. Each one has anecdotes, rough notes, sketches, and the specific dated moments the 2,248 innovations in this platform trace back to.

Those journals were not staged for the video. The video exists because the journals already existed. The anti-garage-myth commitment device built into this platform is not theoretical. It is a physical room with physical journals on physical shelves, documented on a physical video that you can watch right now, and any honest observer can see that the source material is real before I have even written the polished version.

That is what I mean when I say I am already going first. I started going first before I announced I was going first, because the actual work of documentation is not something you can announce into existence. It has to be on the desk before the essay makes sense.

## VIII. The Invitation, Explicit

So here is the invitation, stated cleanly and without pressure.

If you want to document the origin of something you contributed — your business, your Cue Card, your Pudding, your Service Node, your skill declaration, your lemon lot vehicle refurbishment, your family recipe, your guide, your anything — the platform has a place for it and the platform will give you the benefits listed above in exchange for the opt-in.

If you don't want to document any of it, you are still welcome. You are still a full member. Your contributions still count. Nobody will ask you twice. Nobody will pester you about it. Nobody will tier you below members who opted in. The record will say you contributed what you contributed, and that is enough.

The point of going first is not to make documentation universal. **The point of going first is to make documentation available.** The difference between those two statements is the whole difference between a cooperative and a compliance regime, and I intend to land on the cooperative side of that line every single time.

I am going first. I will finish all 2,248 before I publish the sign-up flow for the first member to go second. When that sign-up flow does go live, nobody will be rounded into it. But if you want a road, there will be one, and I will have walked it first with all of mine, so you know exactly what you are looking at before you decide whether to walk it with yours.

Watch me. Or don't. Either way, welcome to the platform.

— Liana Banyan, Founder
San Antonio, Texas | April 2026
