## I. The Problem Nobody Talks About

Every platform eventually becomes a museum.

Not intentionally. Nobody sits down and says, let's build something hollow. It happens gradually, the way a kitchen drawer fills with batteries you're not sure are dead and menus from restaurants that closed. Features accumulate. The original urgency fades. The people who remembered why a thing was built move on, and what's left is the feature itself — orphaned from its origin, defended by inertia.

I have watched this happen to platforms I admired. I have watched communities rally around tools they no longer understand, maintaining them out of habit rather than conviction. I have watched founders give interviews about their "vision" in language so abstracted from lived experience that you would never guess the thing was once a solution to a real, specific, human problem.

I do not want that for this platform.

Liana Banyan was not born in a conference room. It was not born in a pitch deck or a whiteboard session or a strategic offsite. It was born in a series of moments — frustrating, clarifying, sometimes embarrassing moments — that I experienced in kitchens, hardware stores, community centers, and living rooms. Every single innovation on this platform traces back to a real situation, a real friction, a real need that I or someone I was standing next to actually felt.

But here is the thing I have come to understand about origin stories: they do not preserve themselves.

Unless you pin them down, they drift. They get smoothed out in the retelling, simplified for the pitch, romanticized for the press release. The specific becomes the general. The real becomes the mythic. And once that happens, you have lost the most important thing a founder can offer a platform: the unedited truth of why it exists.

This essay is about the system I am building to prevent that — and why I am the one going first.

## II. What "Founder-First" Actually Means

"Founder-First Anecdote Mapping" is not a branding exercise. It is a structural commitment.

Here is what it means in practice:

Every innovation on this platform — and we are currently tracking 2,233+ distinct innovation concepts across sixteen active initiatives — must be mapped to a first-person anecdote before it ships. That anecdote must be documented. And that documentation must include a photograph.

Not a stock photo. Not an illustration. A real photograph, timestamped, from the actual context that gave rise to the need.

Why a photograph? Because photographs are hard to retcon.

You can revise a written anecdote. You can soften the edges, add detail you didn't notice at the time, reframe the protagonist. Memory is famously plastic. But a photograph taken in the moment — a cluttered counter, a broken tool, a handwritten list, a crowded community meeting — is a commitment device. It says: this is what it actually looked like. This is the real thing.

The "Founder-First" part of the name means that I am not asking members to do something I am unwilling to do myself. Before anyone else on this platform is asked to map their contributions to lived experience, I am going through every original concept — every pudding, every initiative, every structural innovation — and documenting the anecdote and photograph that gave rise to it.

This is not a small undertaking. It is, in fact, an enormous one. But I believe it is the most important thing I can do right now for the long-term integrity of this platform.

## III. The Garage Myth and Why It Matters

In 2014, Steve Wozniak gave an interview in which he clarified something that had been bothering him for years. The famous Apple garage — the one that appears in documentaries, on pilgrimage maps in Palo Alto, in countless "humble beginnings" narratives — was not, he said, where the actual work happened. The real design and engineering was done elsewhere. The garage was where they occasionally met. It was a symbol, not a site.

This is not a criticism of Apple. It is an illustration of a universal pattern.

Origin stories get simplified because simplification serves a purpose. The garage is a better story than "we worked at various kitchen tables and in a spare bedroom and sometimes at a friend's place." The garage is iconic. It is photographable. It is a location you can return to and point at.

But the simplification has a cost. When the origin story becomes myth, it stops being useful as a guide. Nobody can learn from a myth the way they can learn from a real, messy, documented process. The myth says: great things begin in garages. The real story says: great things begin wherever you are, with whatever you have, if you pay close enough attention to what's actually broken.

This platform is built on the real story version.

Every pudding — our term for a documented platform innovation — is meant to be a case study in paying attention. Not in having a clever idea, but in noticing a real friction, sitting with it long enough to understand it, and then building something specific enough to address it. That process cannot be reconstructed after the fact. It has to be captured in the moment, or as close to the moment as possible.

The photograph requirement is what keeps us honest. It is the anti-garage-myth mechanism built into the platform's architecture.

## IV. Oral History as Infrastructure

In 1974, Studs Terkel published *Working*, a collection of oral histories from Americans across the full spectrum of labor — steelworkers, domestics, farmers, corporate executives, gravediggers. The methodology was simple: Terkel showed up, turned on a tape recorder, and listened. He asked real questions and sat with real answers, however uncomfortable or contradictory or long.

What he produced was not journalism in the conventional sense. It was infrastructure — a record of what work actually felt like from the inside, at a specific moment in American history, told by the people doing it rather than the people observing it.

StoryCorps, founded in 2003 by Dave Isay, applied a similar principle at scale. By 2026, StoryCorps has recorded nearly 370,000 interviews, building what is now one of the largest archives of human voice in existence. The methodology is again simple: two people, a booth or a phone, a facilitator, and the radical premise that ordinary people's experiences are worth preserving with institutional rigor.

What both Terkel and Isay understood is that lived experience is primary data. It is not a soft supplement to the "real" research. It *is* the research. Everything else — the analysis, the framework, the policy recommendation — is downstream of what actually happened to real people in real circumstances.

Liana Banyan's anecdote-mapping system is an application of this principle to platform architecture. We are treating the founders' and members' lived experiences not as marketing copy but as primary data. The anecdote is the unit of analysis. The photograph is the citation. The pudding is the finding.

This is a different epistemology than most platforms operate under. Most platforms treat user experience as something to be measured — click rates, retention curves, NPS scores. We treat it as something to be documented and understood on its own terms, in its own language, before any quantification happens.

## V. Ostrom's Lesson: Go to the Thing Itself

In 1990, Elinor Ostrom published *Governing the Commons*, a landmark work in political economy that would eventually earn her the Nobel Prize. Her central argument was methodological before it was theoretical: if you want to understand how communities manage shared resources, you have to go look at how communities actually manage shared resources. You cannot derive it from first principles. You cannot model it from the outside.

What she found, when she went and looked, confounded the dominant theory of the time. The "tragedy of the commons" — the idea that shared resources inevitably get depleted by self-interested individuals — turned out to be a story about specific conditions, not a universal law. Communities that developed their own informal rules, monitoring systems, and graduated sanctions managed commons successfully for generations. Maine's lobster fishing communities. Alpine Swiss villages managing shared meadows. Irrigation systems in Spain and the Philippines.

None of these systems looked like what economic theory would have predicted. They were idiosyncratic, locally adapted, built from the texture of specific relationships and specific landscapes. They could not have been designed from the outside. They emerged from the inside, from people paying close attention to their own specific situation.

Ostrom's Nobel lecture is, among other things, a methodological manifesto: go to the thing itself. Document what you see. Build your framework from the ground up, not from the top down.

This is what Founder-First Anecdote Mapping asks of us. Before we theorize about what members need, we document what we actually observed. Before we build a system to address a friction, we pin down the specific moment we felt that friction. Before we generalize, we particularize.

The photograph is the act of going to the thing itself.

## VI. The Commitment Device

Behavioral economists use the term "commitment device" to describe a mechanism that locks you into a future behavior by making deviation costly or impossible. Odysseus asking to be tied to the mast is the classical example. Paying a deposit on a gym membership is the mundane one.

The photograph requirement in our anecdote-mapping system is a commitment device in a specific sense: it makes it impossible to retroactively clean up the origin story.

Here is what I mean. If the only requirement for mapping an innovation to an anecdote were a written description, I could go back six months from now — or six years — and write a tidier version. I could unconsciously incorporate things I learned later. I could smooth out the part where I was confused or wrong. I could make myself sound more prescient than I was.

The photograph prevents this. Not entirely — photographs can be staged, and I acknowledge that — but as a practical matter, a timestamped photograph of a real situation creates an accountability structure that a written description alone does not. It anchors the anecdote to a specific moment in a way that is difficult to undo.

It also creates accountability in a second sense: it is public. When I post the anecdote and photograph for a pudding, I am not just creating a private record. I am making a public claim about the origin of that innovation. Members can see it. Future members can see it. Researchers, if this platform becomes what I believe it can become, can see it.

That public visibility is part of the mechanism. It is much harder to drift from your origins when your origins are documented and visible.

## VII. The Pedagogy of Going First

There is a third reason for the Founder-First requirement that is less about mechanism and more about culture.

I want members of this platform to contribute their own anecdotes. I want the platform's map of innovations to grow from a single founder's documented experience into a dense, multi-authored record of real human needs and real human solutions. That is the long-term vision.

But I cannot ask members to do something I have not demonstrated myself.

If I want members to be vulnerable enough to share the real, unpolished moment that gave rise to their contribution — not the cleaned-up version, not the flattering version, but the version where they were frustrated or confused or embarrassed — I have to show them what that looks like. I have to go first.

This is, I think, the most underrated responsibility of a founder. Not the vision, not the strategy, not the fundraising — the willingness to be the first person to do the uncomfortable thing you are asking everyone else to do.

Going first with the anecdote mapping is my attempt to fulfill that responsibility. By the time the first member is asked to map their contribution, they will be able to look at 2,233+ examples of what that looks like — complete with photographs, messy contexts, and honest accounts of the specific moment the need became visible.

That is a pedagogy. Not a lecture, not a tutorial, not a style guide — a lived demonstration of the practice, at scale, done by the person asking others to adopt it.

## VIII. What This Looks Like in Practice

Let me be concrete about the process.

For each of the 2,233+ innovations currently in the platform's documentation system, the Founder-First Anecdote Mapping process involves the following steps:

**Step 1 — Identify the Origin Moment.** I go back through my notes, photographs, journals, and memory to identify the specific situation that gave rise to the need this innovation addresses. Not the general problem category — the specific moment. The Tuesday in March when the thing broke. The conversation with a specific person that revealed the gap.

**Step 2 — Write the Anecdote.** I write a first-person account of that moment in plain language. Not polished prose — the goal is accuracy, not elegance. What was happening? What did I observe? What did I feel? What did I try that didn't work?

**Step 3 — Locate or Take the Photograph.** I find the photograph I took at the time, or — where no photograph exists — I document the absence and explain why. In some cases, I return to the context and take a photograph now, with a note that it is a retrospective image. Transparency about the photograph's relationship to the origin moment is part of the requirement.

**Step 4 — Map to the Innovation.** I connect the anecdote and photograph explicitly to the innovation it inspired, documenting the path from observation to concept. This is often not linear — the connection may have taken months to become clear — and that non-linearity is worth documenting too.

**Step 5 — Publish.** The anecdote, photograph, and mapping are published in the platform's documentation system, linked to the relevant pudding.

This process is time-intensive. For 2,233+ innovations, it represents an enormous commitment of founder time. I am making that commitment because I believe it is the right foundation for everything else we are building.

## IX. Why This Is Worth the Time

I want to address the obvious objection directly: isn't this a distraction from building the platform?

No. This *is* building the platform.

A platform is not its features. A platform is the trust and shared understanding that makes features worth using. Trust is built through demonstrated integrity. Shared understanding is built through transparent documentation of why things exist and where they came from.

Every hour I spend on Founder-First Anecdote Mapping is an hour spent building the epistemological foundation of this platform — the layer beneath the features that determines whether those features will hold their meaning over time or drift into museum pieces.

The alternative is faster in the short term and more expensive in the long term. Build the features, skip the documentation, trust that the culture will preserve the intent. Every platform that has tried this has eventually paid the cost — in drift, in internal conflict, in the gradual erosion of the original vision into something nobody quite recognizes anymore.

I am choosing the slower, more expensive, more honest path now, because I believe it is the only path that leads where I want to go.

## X. An Invitation

This essay is not just an explanation of a system I am building. It is an invitation.

When you contribute to this platform — when you bring an innovation, a process, a solution to a real problem you have observed — I am going to ask you to do what I am doing now. I am going to ask you to document the real moment. To find or take the photograph. To resist the temptation to clean up the story before you tell it.

I am asking this because I believe it is the most valuable thing you can contribute. Not the finished solution — the real origin. The messy, specific, timestamped truth of what you noticed and when and where.

That is the material this platform is built from. Not strategy, not theory, not best practice — the unretconned record of what real people actually observed in real situations and were moved to address.

I am going first. I will be done with all 2,233+ before I ask you for your first one.

Watch me.

— Liana Banyan, Founder

## SECTION A: Oral History Methodology

### A1. Studs Terkel — *Working* (1974)

**Core Methodology:**
Terkel's method was radical in its simplicity. He conducted open-ended recorded interviews with workers across the full spectrum of American labor, prioritizing depth over breadth in individual conversations and making no attempt to impose a theoretical framework on what he heard before hearing it. He was explicit that his role was to listen rather than to interpret, and that the value of the project lay in the primary material rather than in any analysis he might offer.

**Relevance to Anecdote Mapping:**
Terkel's methodology establishes that first-person accounts of lived experience are primary data, not anecdotal supplementation of "real" research. His insistence on the tape recorder — on verbatim capture rather than paraphrased summary — is an early analogue to our photograph requirement. Both are commitment devices against the drift toward tidiness.
