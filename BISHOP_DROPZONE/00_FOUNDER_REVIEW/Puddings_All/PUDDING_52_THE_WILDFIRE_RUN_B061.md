# Pudding #52: The Wildfire Run

*What if growing a cooperative felt like a race you could actually win?*

---

## At a Glance

You have five days. Your mission: sign up 50 new members in your zip code. Every signup earns you Beacon Points. The leaderboard updates in real time. The top three runners get their names on the community's permanent honor roll.

This is a Wildfire Run — and it is how a cooperative grows without spending a dollar on advertising.

---

## More Info

### The Advertising Problem

Traditional platforms spend billions on advertising. Facebook spends more on ads to acquire users than most countries spend on education. The result: the cost of acquiring a customer gets baked into the platform's economics, which gets passed to the creators, which reduces the 83.3%.

A cooperative cannot do that. It has no advertising budget because it has no investors. And Cost+20% means it cannot raise margins to fund one. So how does a cooperative grow?

The answer: make growth a community activity, not a marketing expense.

### How Wildfire Runs Work

A Captain — a verified member with leadership standing — creates a Wildfire Run. The Run has:

- **A geographic target** — a zip code, a neighborhood, a city
- **A time window** — typically 3-7 days
- **A member goal** — how many new signups the run aims to achieve
- **A leaderboard** — tracking which runners (members participating in the run) are bringing in the most signups

When the Run goes live, any member can join as a runner. Runners share their personal referral link. Every new member who signs up through that link earns the runner Beacon Points. The leaderboard updates in real time.

There is no financial reward for winning. Beacon Points are not currency. They are recognition — a permanent record of who showed up and did the work when the cooperative needed to grow.

### Why It Works

Three reasons:

**1. People want to help.** Members who believe in the cooperative want to see it grow. They do not need to be paid. They need to be *organized*. A Wildfire Run gives them a specific goal, a specific timeframe, and a specific way to track progress.

**2. Competition motivates.** The leaderboard is not about prizes. It is about proving you can do it. Runners who have never recruited a single member discover they can bring in ten in a week — because the Run gave them a structure, a deadline, and a community doing the same thing.

**3. Geographic targeting works.** National advertising is expensive and unfocused. A Wildfire Run in zip code 78207 reaches exactly the people who will benefit from a cooperative food network in zip code 78207. The runner lives there. They know the neighborhood. They know who needs this.

## Full Detail

### The Beacon System

Wildfire Runs are part of a larger system called Beacons. A Beacon is a geographic marker — a point on the map that says "the cooperative is active here." When enough members cluster in a geography, the Beacon lights up.

Beacons serve two purposes:

**For members**: "You are not alone. There are 47 other cooperative members within 5 miles of you." This matters because a cooperative food network only works if there are enough members nearby to sustain demand for local restaurants. A Beacon tells you whether your area has reached critical mass.

**For Captains**: "This area needs more members." If a Beacon is dim — below the critical mass threshold — a Captain can launch a Wildfire Run to light it up. The Run becomes a targeted, time-limited growth sprint focused on the exact geography that needs it.

### The Economics of Free Growth

A Wildfire Run costs the cooperative nothing. The runners are members, not employees. The referral links are generated by the platform. The signups cost $5/year each — membership revenue that immediately exceeds the server cost of the new account.

Compare this to Facebook's customer acquisition cost of $15-25 per user. Or Uber's cost of $200+ per new driver. The cooperative's acquisition cost is *negative* — each new member generates more revenue than they cost, from day one.

This is only possible because:
1. The product is the cooperative itself (no advertising needed to explain what a $5 membership buys)
2. The runners are intrinsically motivated (they want the cooperative to succeed in their area)
3. The geographic targeting eliminates waste (you are not advertising to people 3,000 miles away)

### The Honor Roll

At the end of a Wildfire Run, the top runners' names go on a permanent honor roll. This is not a trophy. It is a historical record — "When the cooperative came to [neighborhood], these were the people who made it happen."

In ten years, when the cooperative has thousands of members in that zip code, the honor roll will still show who lit the first Beacon. That kind of recognition has no dollar value. It has something better: permanence.

### Wildfire Runs and Cold Start

The Wildfire Run system connects directly to the cooperative's Cold Start problem. A new geographic area has zero members. Zero members means zero transactions. Zero transactions means no food network, no cooperative purchasing, no community.

A Wildfire Run is the match that starts the fire. Fifty members in a zip code is enough to sustain a small food network. One hundred members is enough for cooperative purchasing. Five hundred is enough for a full-service cooperative economy.

Every Wildfire Run is a Cold Start solution for a specific place. And every lit Beacon is proof that the cooperative works — not in theory, not in a pitch deck, but on your street.
