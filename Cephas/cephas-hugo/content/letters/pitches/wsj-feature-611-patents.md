## WHY THIS IS A WSJ STORY

### 1. The Contrarian Bet

While Silicon Valley chases AI and crypto, Jones bet everything on... manufacturing? Cooperatives? A fixed-margin model that caps his own profit?

The conventional wisdom says you can't compete without extraction. Jones's math says extraction is what makes you *un*competitive.

### 2. The Numbers Don't Lie

His academic paper (peer-review ready, attached) proves that structural constraints reduce ROI variance from plus/minus 50-200% to plus/minus 5%. Not theory—empirical data from 170+ prototypes.

The formula is elegant:
- Fix the margin (Cost + 20%, locked in operating agreement)
- Fix the demand (pre-orders before production)
- Fix the cost (prototype-validated, not estimated)

Result: Profit variance collapses to manufacturing tolerance. That's hedge-fund-level predictability without hedge-fund fees.

### 3. The Iconoclast Factor

Jones isn't a disruptor in the hoodie-wearing, "move fast and break things" sense. He's a helicopter pilot who spent decades in IT, raised eight kids, and kept engineering notebooks like a 19th-century inventor.

### 4. The Giving Pledge Problem

He's written to Warren Buffett and MacKenzie Scott—not asking for charity, but pointing out that philanthropy doesn't solve concentration. Money flows out and concentrates again.

His pitch: "I'm not asking you to be Washington. I'm asking you to be the French fleet."
