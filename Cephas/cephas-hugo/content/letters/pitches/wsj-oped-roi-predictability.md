## PROPOSED OP-ED (850 words)

### The Risk Premium Is a Tax on Inefficiency

When a startup charges $15 for a product that costs $3 to make, we call that "healthy margins." When a platform takes 30% of every transaction, we call that "market rate." When a hedge fund demands 2-and-20, we call that "aligned incentives."

We're wrong about all of it.

These premiums aren't compensation for value created. They're compensation for uncertainty absorbed. The markup isn't for the product—it's for the risk that demand might evaporate, costs might spike, or competition might undercut. Strip away the uncertainty, and the premium becomes indefensible.

I've spent 47 years in engineering—aerospace, IT, manufacturing—and the math is clear: most business uncertainty isn't exogenous (an "act of God"). It's endogenous (a "design flaw"). We accept demand forecasting error, pricing volatility, and supply chain variance as inevitable. They're not. They're choices embedded in business architecture.

**The proof is structural.**

Consider three constraints:

1. **Fixed margin**: Price locked at cost plus 20%, written into the corporate operating agreement. Not a policy—a legal structure surviving change of control.

2. **Pre-order validation**: Production begins only after binding commitments. Volume is known, not estimated.

3. **Distributed manufacturing with empirical costs**: Unit costs measured through prototype development, not projected from supplier quotes.

Under these constraints, profit variance drops to plus/minus 5%—bounded only by irreducible manufacturing tolerances.

**The implications invert conventional wisdom.**

First, cooperative structures don't require accepting higher risk or lower returns. The assumption that "doing good" means "sacrificing profit" is empirically false when structure eliminates uncertainty.

Second, platform extraction becomes economically unnecessary. Uber, DoorDash, and Spotify charge high fees not because delivery or streaming costs that much, but because their demand forecasting and pricing models embed massive variance. Fix the structure, and the margin requirement drops.

Third, impact investing doesn't need concessionary returns. The tradeoff isn't impact versus profit—it's structural constraint versus optionality. Mission-driven capital can achieve market-rate returns with below-market variance while maintaining alignment.

**The most counterintuitive finding:** Under cost-plus-20%, there's no incentive to cut costs by degrading quality. Since margin is proportional to cost, cheaper inputs mean lower absolute profit per unit. Quality improvements that increase cost are *aligned* with profit growth.

This inverts the extractive logic of modern capitalism, where shareholders reward cost-cutting even when it destroys value. In the constrained model, the platform succeeds when creators produce higher-quality goods—not when they race to the bottom.

I've filed 611 patent claims on innovations built using this framework. 170+ products with empirical cost data. 1,200+ CAD diagrams. The theory isn't theoretical anymore.

Philanthropy is a patch. Structure is infrastructure.

The risk premium we pay on every transaction—the hidden tax on inefficiency—can be refunded. Not through charity. Through design.
