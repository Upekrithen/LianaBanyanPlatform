# LETTER: BRIAN MERCHANT
## Author, Blood in the Machine
## Labor & Technology Historian

**Date:** January 23, 2026

---

Dear Brian,

You wrote the definitive history of workers fighting against machines that threatened to replace them.

I've built a machine designed to work for them instead.

*Blood in the Machine* reframed the Luddites — not as anti-technology, but as anti-exploitation. They weren't smashing machines because they feared progress. They were smashing machines because the machines were being used to destroy their livelihoods while enriching factory owners.

I read your book and recognized the pattern you documented: technology that could liberate workers, deployed instead to extract from them.

I built something different.

---

## WHO I AM

I'm a 53-year-old ARNG veteran — Infantry and Aviation, helicopter pilot with FAA Commercial Rotary Wing IFR rating. Father of eight. 21 years in IT development. Chess player (2118 rating, top 0.4% globally).

I've been thinking about cooperative economics for four decades. Building this specific platform for nine. I have 18 handwritten journals documenting every iteration, 1,200+ technical diagrams, and 150+ innovations with 928 patent claims filed.

I'm not a historian. But I've read enough history to know that technology itself is neutral — it's the ownership and control that determines whether it liberates or extracts.

---

## WHAT I'VE BUILT

Liana Banyan is a cooperative platform built on one principle: **Cost + 20%**.

Creators keep 83.3% of every transaction. The platform margin is fixed in the operating agreement — not by choice, but by legal structure. We cannot raise it without violating our own charter.

Three commercial divisions generate revenue that permanently funds fourteen charitable initiatives: food security, healthcare access, education, financial inclusion, crisis response, and more.

The workers govern the platform. Each initiative has a council. Workers elect their own representative to the corporate Board. The founder has a seat, not a throne.

---

## THE PARALLEL YOU'LL RECOGNIZE

The frame loom operators in 1811 faced a specific problem: technology that could have made their work more productive was instead being used to eliminate their jobs and suppress their wages.

Platform workers in 2026 face the same problem: technology that could connect them to opportunities is instead being used to surveil them, rate them with opaque algorithms, and extract 30%+ of their earnings.

The Luddites' answer was destruction — the only power available to them.

I built an alternative: a platform where the workers own the technology.

---

## HOW THIS CONNECTS TO YOUR WORK

**On ownership:**
You documented how the factory system separated workers from ownership of the means of production. Liana Banyan reverses this — participation creates ownership stakes (Platform Joules). The more you work, the more you own.

**On the AI question:**
Your recent work on AI and labor focuses on how AI threatens to do to knowledge work what power looms did to textile work. Our AI system (Star Chamber) is designed differently — it verifies quality and prevents hallucination, but it doesn't replace workers. It helps humans do better work.

**On the gig economy:**
You've written about how gig platforms recreated factory discipline through algorithms. Our governance is the opposite — workers elect their own representatives, choose their compensation model, and can't be fired by algorithm.

**On collective action:**
The Luddites failed partly because they couldn't build alternative institutions. They could destroy machines, but they couldn't build factories that treated workers fairly. Liana Banyan is an attempt to build that alternative institution before the extraction becomes total.

---

## THE MANUFACTURING ANGLE

We're building distributed manufacturing nodes — local production facilities where operators can make products at cost + 20%. The 3/5 + 2/5 split: 60% of time for platform orders, 40% for their own projects.

This is the opposite of the factory system you documented. Workers control their time. They use the equipment for their own projects. They keep the value they create.

The technology doesn't belong to distant owners extracting rent. It belongs to the workers using it.

---

## THE HISTORICAL QUESTION

You've documented how workers have always fought against technology deployed against their interests. Sometimes they won temporary victories. Usually they lost.

But sometimes — rarely — the technology was structured to serve workers. Credit unions. Rural electrification cooperatives. Open source software.

I'm trying to add cooperative commerce to that list.

Is it possible? Can a platform genuinely serve workers while competing with platforms that extract from them? Or will the economics inevitably push toward extraction?

You've studied this question historically. I've tried to answer it architecturally. I'd like to know if my answer holds up.
