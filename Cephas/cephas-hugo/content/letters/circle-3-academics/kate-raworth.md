Dear Dr. Raworth,

You drew a doughnut and changed how a generation thinks about economics.

The simple idea — that we must meet human needs without overshooting planetary boundaries — has influenced policy from Amsterdam to Costa Rica. But implementing doughnut economics at the organizational level remains harder than drawing the concept.

I've spent fifteen years building a commercial platform designed to operate inside the doughnut.

My name is Jonathan Jones. I'm 52, an ARNG veteran, helicopter pilot, father of eight. I've invested $5,000 annually from a $30,000 income into this work — 47 years thinking about cooperative economics, 15 years actively building.

**The Doughnut Challenge**

Your framework identifies the sweet spot between the social foundation (below which people fall into deprivation) and the ecological ceiling (above which we overshoot planetary boundaries). Most businesses optimize for growth without acknowledging either boundary.

Liana Banyan is structured to stay in the safe space:

**Social Foundation:**
- Workers keep 83.3% of every transaction (addressing poverty)
- Microfinance through VSL initiative (addressing financial inclusion)
- LifeLine Medications for affordable prescriptions (addressing health)
- Let's Make Dinner for food security (addressing hunger)
- Didasko for education access (addressing knowledge)

**Ecological Ceiling:**
- Local manufacturing through Let's Make Bread (reducing shipping)
- Cost + 20% model discourages overproduction (reducing waste)
- No growth-at-all-costs pressure (ownership isn't equity-based)
- Cooperative structure resists extraction (sustainable margins)

**The Architecture**

The platform margin is locked at **Cost + 20%** in the operating agreement. Creators keep **83.3%** of every transaction. This cannot change — not through acquisition, not through leadership change, not through going public.

We've encoded degrowth-compatible economics into legal structure.

Three commercial divisions fund fourteen charitable initiatives:

**The Questions**

You've thought more deeply than almost anyone about what regenerative economics looks like in practice. I need your perspective:

1. Does the Cost + 20% model genuinely address the social foundation, or is it just less extractive extraction?
2. Can a platform this size stay inside the ecological ceiling as it scales?
3. What's missing from the doughnut lens that I haven't considered?

**Why I'm Writing**

I'm not asking for endorsement. I'm asking for analysis.

The documentation is at **Cephas.LianaBanyan.org**. The economics are published. I've tried to implement your framework at the organizational level — I need to know if I've succeeded or if I've just built another growth machine with a nicer logo.

Would you be willing to examine it? Or point me to researchers at Oxford who work on organizational implementations of doughnut economics?
