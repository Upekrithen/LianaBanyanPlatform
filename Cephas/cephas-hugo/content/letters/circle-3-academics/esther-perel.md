## WHAT I'VE BUILT

Liana Banyan is a cooperative platform built on one principle: **Cost + 20%**.

Creators keep 83.3% of every transaction. The platform margin is fixed — we cannot enshittify because we're legally prohibited from doing so.

Three commercial divisions fund fourteen charitable initiatives: food security, healthcare access, education, financial inclusion, crisis response.

But here's the thing: none of that works if people don't trust it.

---

## THE TRUST PROBLEM

Traditional platforms solve trust through surveillance. Uber rates drivers and passengers. Airbnb photographs every room. DoorDash tracks delivery workers by GPS.

This creates compliance, not trust. Workers behave because they're watched, not because they're invested.

We're trying something different: trust through ownership.

Every participant in Liana Banyan earns ownership stakes. Not as a gift from a generous founder — as a structural feature of every transaction. When you cook a meal for a neighbor, you're not just earning money. You're building equity in the system.

Does ownership create trust? I think so. But I'm an engineer. You're the expert on human relationships.

---

## WHERE YOUR WORK APPLIES

**On vulnerability:**
You've written that trust requires vulnerability — the willingness to be seen and the risk of being hurt. Cooperative economics requires the same thing. The cook trusts the neighbor to pay fairly. The neighbor trusts the cook to prepare food safely. The platform trusts both to resolve conflicts without constant intervention.

**On power dynamics:**
Your work on erotic relationships often focuses on power — who has it, how it's exchanged, when imbalance creates distance. Platform economics has the same dynamics. Traditional platforms hold all the power — they set rates, determine visibility, can deactivate accounts arbitrarily. We've tried to distribute power through council governance, worker representation on the Board, and member ownership.

**On modern isolation:**
You've spoken about how modern life creates connection hunger — we're more networked than ever but often less connected. Let's Make Dinner, one of our initiatives, is neighbors cooking for neighbors. It's not just food delivery. It's structured social connection. Does it work? Can you engineer serendipitous community?

---

## THE HARPER GUILD

We've created something called the Harper Guild — care coordinators who are embedded in every initiative, paid by the platform (not by the business they're assigned to), trained to watch for worker wellbeing.

They're relationship monitors, essentially. Not surveillance. Support.

The name comes from medieval harpers who traveled between courts, bound to no single lord, trusted precisely because they served everyone. Our Harpers serve the same function — independent observers who can identify when relationships are breaking down, when power is being abused, when someone needs help.

Is this the right structure? I built it because I couldn't think of another way to maintain relational health at scale. But you've spent decades thinking about these questions professionally.
