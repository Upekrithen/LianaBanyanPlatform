## Professor, Harvard Law School

**Date:** January 23, 2026

---

Dear Professor Benkler,

Twenty years ago, you described a new mode of economic production — decentralized, collaborative, enabled by networks. You called it commons-based peer production.

I've spent nine years building the commercial infrastructure to make it sustainable.

*The Wealth of Networks* wasn't just a description of what was happening. It was a blueprint for what could happen if we built the right systems. Wikipedia proved the model for knowledge. Linux proved it for software. But commerce remained stuck in the old paradigm.

Until now.

---

## WHO I AM

I'm a 53-year-old ARNG veteran — Infantry and Aviation, helicopter pilot with FAA Commercial Rotary Wing IFR rating. Father of eight. 21 years in IT development. Chess player (2118 rating, top 0.4% globally). Probably not who you expected to operationalize your theory.

I've been thinking about cooperative economics for 47 years. Building this for nine. I have 18 handwritten journals, 1,200+ technical diagrams, and 150+ innovations with 470+ patent claims filed.

I first read your work in 2008. I understood immediately that the same dynamics enabling Wikipedia could enable economic activity — if someone built the infrastructure to handle money, trust, and accountability at scale.

That's what I've built.

---

## WHAT I'VE BUILT

Liana Banyan is a cooperative platform with one fixed economic principle: **Cost + 20%**.

Creators keep 83.3% of every transaction. The platform margin is locked in the operating agreement — not aspirational, structural. We cannot enshittify because we're legally prohibited from doing so.

Three commercial divisions (.com, .biz, .net) generate revenue that permanently funds fourteen charitable initiatives: food security, healthcare access, education, financial inclusion, crisis response, and more.

Your framework identified three key conditions for commons-based peer production:
1. Modularity — tasks that can be broken into independent pieces
2. Granularity — pieces small enough for individual contribution
3. Low-cost integration — mechanisms to combine contributions effectively

Liana Banyan is built on all three:
- **Modularity:** Fourteen initiatives that operate independently but interlock
- **Granularity:** Membership starts at $5; work can be as small as one delivery, one meal, one song
- **Integration:** The Tab System tracks contributions across all initiatives, converting work into ownership stakes

---

## WHERE YOUR THEORY APPLIES

**On motivation:** You wrote that commons-based peer production thrives when intrinsic motivation aligns with effective collaboration. Our Position Funding system offers workers three compensation models — they choose based on their actual circumstances, not what maximizes platform extraction.

**On modularity:** Each initiative operates as a council with its own governance. The Crown holder leads, but workers and members elect their own representative to the corporate Board. Decentralized authority with aligned incentives.

**On social production:** Your insight that social production can rival market production applies directly to our meal delivery (Let's Make Dinner), where neighbors cook for neighbors. The social relationship is primary; the transaction is just accounting.

**On the institutional ecology:** You argued that commons-based production needs supporting legal and technical infrastructure. We've built both — operating agreements that lock in Cost + 20%, AI verification systems that ensure quality without surveillance, and manufacturing nodes that decentralize production.

---

## WHAT I'M ASKING

I want you to examine whether we've correctly operationalized your theory.

Not endorsement — examination. Does the structure we've built actually enable commons-based peer production at commercial scale? Where are the gaps between the theory and our implementation?

If the examination is favorable, I'd be honored if you'd consider:
- Writing about Liana Banyan as a case study in applied commons theory
- Advising on governance structure and institutional design
- Introducing us to scholars and practitioners working on similar problems

If the examination reveals structural problems, I'd be equally grateful. Better to know now.

## THE LEGAL ANGLE

You've written extensively about how law shapes the information economy. Liana Banyan's legal structure is unusual:
- Wyoming C-Corp (Liana Banyan Corporation)
- Operating agreement with fixed margin (Cost + 20%)
- Patent portfolio protecting the economic model (150+ innovations)
- Council governance with worker representation on the Board

The patents aren't weapons — they're shields. We've published everything in exhaustive detail. The patents protect the economic model from being captured by conventional platforms.

Is this the right legal architecture for commons-based commerce? I genuinely don't know. Your perspective would be invaluable.

## THE CONNECTION

You predicted that networked digital environments would enable new forms of economic production — peer production, sharing, collaboration. You were right about the potential.

What you couldn't predict was how thoroughly venture capital would capture that potential. The sharing economy became the extraction economy. Uber isn't commons-based peer production; it's algorithmic management with a friendly interface.

I built the thing you predicted could exist — before it got captured. Cost + 20%, locked in legal structure, owned by members, governed by workers.

I want to know if it actually works.
