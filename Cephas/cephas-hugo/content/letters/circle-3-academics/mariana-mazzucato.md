Dear Professor Mazzucato,

You've spent your career arguing that the state creates value — that public investment enables private innovation, and that the rewards should reflect that contribution.

I've spent fifteen years building a platform that captures value differently. Not for the state, but for the workers who create it.

My name is Jonathan Jones. I'm 52, an ARNG veteran, helicopter pilot, father of eight. I've invested $5,000 annually from a $30,000 income into this work — 47 years thinking about cooperative economics, 15 years actively building.

**The Value Problem**

In *The Value of Everything*, you documented how our economy has become expert at extracting value while calling it creation. Platforms that "connect" people extract 30%+ while producing nothing. Finance that "allocates capital" extracts fees while moving risk onto others.

Liana Banyan attempts the opposite: a platform that produces real connections while extracting minimal margin.

**The Architecture**

The platform operates on **Cost + 20%**.

Creators keep **83.3%** of every transaction. The margin is locked in the operating agreement — not by policy choice, but by legal structure. Acquisition cannot change it. Leadership change cannot change it. IPO cannot change it (and we won't IPO).

This isn't a promise. It's architecture.

**The 14 Initiatives**

Three commercial divisions fund fourteen charitable initiatives:

| Category | Initiatives |
|----------|-------------|
| Food & Home | Let's Make Dinner, Let's Get Groceries, Let's Go Shopping, Home Logistics |
| Health & Safety | LifeLine Medications, MSA, Defense Klaus, Rally Group |
| Finance & Work | VSL (microfinance), Let's Make Bread (manufacturing), Harper Guild |
| Creative & Learning | JukeBox (music), Didasko (education), International |

Each initiative creates tangible value — meals prepared, medications delivered, products manufactured, music licensed. The platform takes a margin for coordination. The rest stays with creators.

**The Mission Economy Connection**

Your recent work on mission-oriented innovation argues that society needs to organize around solving problems, not just maximizing returns.

Liana Banyan is mission-oriented by structure:
- **Mission:** Enable communities to provide for themselves
- **Measure:** Value retained by creators (83.3%)
- **Mechanism:** Locked margin prevents mission drift

The platform can't pivot to extraction because the legal structure forbids it. The mission is encoded, not just stated.

This isn't a startup searching for product-market fit. It's fifteen years of documented economic theory, refined through implementation attempts.

**The Questions**

You've thought more deeply than almost anyone about how value is created, captured, and distributed. I need your perspective:

1. Does the Cost + 20% model genuinely preserve creator value, or is there hidden extraction I'm not seeing?
2. Can mission-oriented structure survive at scale, or will market pressures find ways around the architecture?
3. What's the relationship between public value and cooperative value? Can private platforms serve public purposes?

**Why I'm Writing**

I'm not asking for endorsement. I'm asking for analysis.

The documentation is at **Cephas.LianaBanyan.org**. The economics are published. I've tried to build a platform that creates value without extracting it — I need to know if that's actually possible.

Would you be willing to examine it? Or point me to researchers at UCL who work on platform economics and value creation?
