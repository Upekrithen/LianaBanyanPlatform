**The Institutional Problem**

Your research shows that extractive institutions — those that concentrate power and extract resources from the many for the benefit of the few — persist because they're stable. Those who benefit from extraction use their power to maintain the extraction.

Digital platforms have become extractive institutions. They start inclusive (connecting people, enabling exchange) and become extractive (maximizing take rate, capturing data, locking in users). The pattern is predictable because the incentives are structural.

**An Institutional Experiment**

Liana Banyan attempts to build an inclusive institution that can't become extractive.

The mechanism: **Cost + 20%** locked in the operating agreement.

Creators keep **83.3%** of every transaction. This cannot change through:
- Acquisition (operating agreement survives)
- Leadership change (structure persists)
- Going public (we won't, but if we did, structure persists)
- Competitive pressure (margin floor, not ceiling)

The question: Is it possible to encode inclusive institutions at the organizational level? Or will extractive pressures find ways around any structure?

**The Power and Progress Connection**

Your recent work with Simon Johnson on technology and power distribution is particularly relevant. You argue that whether technology improves lives depends on who controls it and how the benefits are shared.

Liana Banyan is a technology platform designed with that insight built in:
- Control is distributed (council governance, elected representation)
- Benefits are shared (83.3% to creators, locked margin)
- Power is bounded (no one can change the margin)

But I'm not certain the design survives contact with reality. That's why I'm writing.

**The Questions**

You've studied institutional evolution more rigorously than almost anyone. I need your perspective:

1. Can inclusive institutions be designed at the organizational level, or do they require state-level implementation?
2. What mechanisms do extractive actors use to subvert inclusive structures? Where should I look for vulnerabilities?
3. Is the "locked margin" approach robust, or will competitive pressure or regulatory capture undermine it?
