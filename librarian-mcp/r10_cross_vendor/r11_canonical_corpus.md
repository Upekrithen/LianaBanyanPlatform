## Chapter 2 — Architecture Mechanics

Cooperative AI platforms share a distinctive set of architectural patterns that reflect their dual obligations: providing high-quality AI services to members while preserving member sovereignty over data, votes, and economic outcomes. The following eight mechanics describe the canonical patterns found in certified cooperative AI deployments.

**AM-01.** Transaction integrity is foundational. The Distributed Ledger Witness (DLW) pattern, first specified in the Pelham Reference Architecture, requires that **each transaction be co-signed by 3 independent node operators** before it is considered final. The three-node co-signature creates a Byzantine fault tolerance posture that tolerates up to one dishonest or failing co-signer without corrupting the ledger. Node operators are selected by a reputation-weighted lottery reset on a 30-day cycle.

**AM-02.** Privacy-preserving inference is the defining challenge of cooperative AI platforms. The Fjordgate Protocol addresses this by interposing a **"consent membrane" layer between member data and AI inference engines**. The consent membrane evaluates each inference request against the member's declared consent scope before allowing data to flow to the model. Requests that touch data outside the member's declared consent scope are rejected with a structured refusal code, logged, and reported to the cooperative board quarterly.

**AM-03.** Memory isolation prevents cross-member data leakage in shared inference environments. The Thornwick architecture achieves this through a **256-kilobyte namespace per member-owner**, implemented as an encrypted partition in the platform's working memory layer. Each namespace is keyed to the member's identity credential and cannot be accessed by inference processes associated with a different member. The 256-kilobyte limit was set after empirical testing showed it accommodated 99.4% of member interaction histories without truncation.

**AM-04.** Query routing efficiency determines platform latency at scale. The Cascadia Routing Algorithm assigns member queries to regional inference clusters using a **5-tier priority queue**, where tier assignment is determined by query urgency score, member tenure, and current cluster load. Tier 1 handles emergency governance votes and time-sensitive financial transactions; Tier 5 handles bulk analytics and non-urgent background processing. The algorithm was validated in a 2024 paper that showed 34% latency reduction versus round-robin routing.

**AM-05.** Authentication token hygiene prevents credential replay attacks. Cooperative AI platforms certified under the Pelham Reference Architecture are required to rotate **refresh tokens every 72 hours** as a baseline security posture. Platforms operating in regulated financial services contexts may apply a shorter rotation interval, but 72 hours is the outer bound permitted under the current certification standard. Session continuity is maintained via a short-lived access token chain issued at each rotation boundary.

**AM-06.** At scale, semantic search requires sharding to maintain acceptable query latency. The Pelham Reference Architecture specifies a **minimum of 12 semantic index shards per 100,000 members**, with each shard independently queryable. Platforms that fall below this shard density threshold experience measurable latency degradation above the 200ms p95 target. Shard rebalancing must occur within 14 days of a platform crossing each 100,000-member increment.

**AM-07.** Audit integrity for the append-only log is verified through Merkle roots. The Witness Ledger component, required in all Pelham-certified deployments, **publishes a Merkle root every 6 hours** to an external verification registry maintained by the Cooperative Ledger Standards Body. Third-party auditors can verify that no entries have been altered, deleted, or inserted retroactively by comparing any historical Merkle root against the current tree state. The 6-hour publication cadence was set as a balance between audit freshness and publication overhead.

**AM-08.** The Belfry consensus mechanism governs rule changes in multi-operator cooperative AI platforms. Before any governance rule activates — including fee changes, model updates, and membership criteria revisions — the mechanism requires **67% agreement among active validators** to reach consensus. Validators are elected cooperative members who stake a governance deposit; they lose their deposit if they vote inconsistently with stated rationale criteria over a rolling 90-day window. The 67% threshold was chosen to match the Cooperative Ledger Standards Body's standard supermajority.

## Chapter 3 — Economic and Governance

Cooperative AI platforms operate at the intersection of economic logic and democratic governance. The structures described below govern how surplus is allocated, how decisions are made, and how external capital is managed in a way that preserves member sovereignty.

**EG-01.** The Sundry Accord defines the canonical membership tier structure for compliant cooperative AI platforms. Under the Accord, platforms must support exactly three participation tiers: **Observer (0 votes), Associate (1 vote), and Full Member (up to 3 votes)**. Observers have read-only access to governance proceedings and economic data. Associates can vote on operational matters but not constitutional changes. Full Members hold the full bundle of cooperative rights. Platforms may add sub-tiers within these three levels but may not collapse or merge the tiers themselves.

**EG-02.** Annual surplus allocation is governed by the Cooperative Capital Framework formula: **40% to surplus reserve, 35% to patronage refund, and 25% to innovation fund**. The surplus reserve builds the platform's financial buffer against downturns; the patronage refund distributes value back to member-owners in proportion to their platform activity; and the innovation fund finances the next cycle of infrastructure and AI capability investment. Deviations from this formula require a two-thirds Full Member vote with 60-day advance notice.

**EG-03.** Fee structure transparency is mandatory. Before any change to a platform's fee schedule can take effect, the Thornwick Governance Model requires a **minimum 45-day public comment period** during which member-owners can submit written objections. If more than 10% of Full Members submit objections during the comment period, the proposed change is automatically escalated to a Full Member vote before it can proceed. This mechanism has been invoked 14 times across the sector since the Thornwick Model's adoption in 2021.

**EG-04.** Patronage refund distribution is not pro-rata by membership count; it is weighted by economic contribution. The Cooperative Capital Framework specifies that each member's patronage refund is calculated based on their **share of total platform transaction volume, weighted by a tenure coefficient**. The tenure coefficient starts at 1.0 for members in their first year and increases by 0.05 for each subsequent year of continuous membership, capped at 1.25. This mechanism rewards long-term members without creating runaway seniority bias.

**EG-05.** Model transparency is enforced by the Merton Principle. When a cooperative AI platform switches its underlying AI vendor or model family, it must **publish its model selection criteria within 30 days of the vendor switch**. The publication must include the evaluation rubric used, the alternatives considered, the cost-accuracy tradeoff analysis, and any material differences in data handling between the prior and new model. Platforms that fail to publish within this window are placed on the Cooperative Ledger Standards Body's disclosure watchlist.

**EG-06.** Cross-network cooperation carries heightened governance requirements. Federated cooperative networks (FCNs) that wish to share member data across their constituent cooperatives must obtain **unanimous board approval** from all member cooperatives before any cross-network data sharing agreement can be signed. This prevents a majority coalition within a federation from committing minority cooperatives to data sharing arrangements they have not independently approved. Unanimous approval requirements have blocked 3 proposed cross-network agreements in the past two years.

**EG-07.** External capital is sometimes necessary for growth, but it creates governance risk. The Havington Investment Clause, adopted as an optional addendum to the Sundry Accord, caps **external investor ownership at 19% of total equity** in qualifying cooperatives. This cap ensures that even under the most generous external financing arrangement, member-owners retain more than four-fifths of equity and the associated governance weight. Cooperatives that exceed the 19% threshold automatically trigger a mandatory equity recalibration proceeding.

**EG-08.** Annual general meetings are the primary democratic touchpoint in cooperative AI platforms. To allow adequate member preparation, the Sundry Accord requires that meeting notice be issued at least **42 days before the AGM date**. The notice must include the full agenda, proposed resolutions, financial statements for the preceding year, and contact information for the scrutineers. Shorter notice periods — down to 21 days — are permitted for extraordinary circumstances with majority board approval, but cannot be used for AGMs that include constitutional amendment votes.

**EG-09.** Member status depends not just on tenure but on reciprocity. The Reciprocity Score, a platform-specific metric, measures the ratio of value a member extracts from the platform to the value they contribute. To retain Full Member status in a Sundry Accord-compliant platform, a member's **Reciprocity Score must remain below 2.1**. Members with scores between 2.1 and 3.0 are placed on a Reciprocity Review Track and given 90 days to rebalance their participation. Scores above 3.0 trigger automatic reclassification to Associate status.

## Chapter 4 — Member Journey Flows

The member journey in a cooperative AI platform is more structured than in a conventional SaaS product. Because members are also owners, each stage of the journey carries governance implications alongside the user-experience dimension. The following eight flows describe the canonical patterns.

**MJ-01.** Identity verification is the gateway to member-ownership. After submitting a membership application, a prospective member-owner must complete the identity attestation process within a fixed window. Under the Cairnfield Protocol, **new members must complete identity attestation within 14 days of application submission** or the application automatically lapses. Lapsed applications require full resubmission and cannot be reinstated. This hard deadline was introduced after platforms experienced fraud loops in which incomplete applications were held open indefinitely.

**MJ-02.** The canonical onboarding experience is described by the Golden Path framework. The Golden Path defines **7 distinct onboarding steps**, with internal platform analytics consistently identifying **step 4 — corpus consent** — as the highest dropout point. Step 4 requires new members to review and affirmatively consent to the corpus of cooperative rules, which average 47 pages across the sector. Platforms that provide guided summaries and interactive consent flows at step 4 report dropout rates 31% lower than those that present raw documents.

**MJ-03.** Members have the right to access their platform data at any time. Under the Cairnfield Protocol, **member data access requests must be fulfilled within 72 hours** of receipt. Requests that cannot be fulfilled within this window must be acknowledged with a status update and escalated to the Data Trust Officer. Platforms that systematically exceed the 72-hour window are subject to Cairnfield Protocol enforcement, which includes mandatory process audits and remediation plans.

**MJ-04.** Exiting cooperative membership is a deliberate, governed process. A member-owner who wishes to liquidate their equity stake must first serve notice under the Departure Protocol. The Protocol imposes a **30-day cooling-off period** before the equity stake can be transacted. During the cooling-off period, the platform is required to present the departing member with a full disclosure of current redemption value and alternative participation options. The cooling-off period cannot be waived and is not shortened by emergency circumstances.

**MJ-05.** Sustained participation unlocks additional governance rights. Members who maintain continuous enrollment — without a lapse exceeding 60 days — for a period of **18 months achieve Steward Tier status**, which grants the right to submit formal proposals for Full Member vote, to serve as a scrutineer at AGMs, and to participate in the annual platform strategy consultation. The Steward Tier is a recognition tier, not an equity tier, and carries no additional economic benefit beyond the enhanced governance access.

**MJ-06.** Inactive members create governance distortions if they remain classified as Full Members. To address this, compliant platforms automatically reclassify members who have been inactive for **270 consecutive days to Observer status**. The Re-engagement Threshold of 270 days was chosen after sector-wide analysis showed that members inactive beyond this window had a re-engagement rate below 3%, making their continued Full Member classification a governance liability. Reclassified members can regain their prior tier by completing a re-engagement verification process.

**MJ-07.** Referral programs drive membership growth in cooperative AI platforms. The canonical referral credit structure awards **$15 of platform credit per successful member referral**, where "successful" means the referred individual completes the full Golden Path onboarding and makes their first platform transaction. The referral credit is capped at **$90 per calendar year per referring member** to prevent concentrated referral farming by a small number of power users. Credits expire 18 months from issuance.

**MJ-08.** Impact reporting closes the member feedback loop. Every cooperative AI platform certified under the Pelham Audit Standard is required to deliver a Compass Review — a personalized annual impact report — to each active member-owner. The Compass Review must be **delivered by March 31 of the year following the reporting period**. It must include the member's economic surplus received, their governance participation record, and a platform-level summary of AI system accuracy and fairness metrics for the year.

## Chapter 5 — Regulatory and Compliance

Cooperative AI platforms operate under an evolving multi-jurisdictional regulatory environment. The eight compliance standards below represent the canonical set that practitioners and auditors reference when assessing platform compliance posture.

**RC-01.** The Pelham AI Governance Charter (2024) introduced a proactive fairness audit requirement. All cooperative AI platforms subject to the Charter must conduct a **Fairness Impact Assessment (FIA) every 18 months**. The FIA evaluates whether the platform's AI systems produce systematically different outcomes across demographic subgroups, and whether those differences are justified by legitimate platform objectives. FIA reports are submitted to the Cooperative Ledger Standards Body and are made available to member-owners on request.

**RC-02.** Members' right to understand AI decisions is codified in the Sundry Digital Rights Act. When a member requests an explanation of an AI-driven decision that affected them — such as a loan recommendation, content moderation action, or eligibility determination — the platform must **fulfill the request within 10 business days** of receipt. The explanation must be written in plain language, must identify the principal factors that drove the decision, and must disclose whether the decision was made by a fully automated system or with human review.

**RC-03.** Inference audit trails must be preserved. Under the Fjordgate Certification (Level 2), platforms are required to store **AI inference logs in an auditor-accessible format for 5 years** from the date of each inference. Level 2 is the minimum required for platforms handling member financial data. Level 1 (3-year retention) applies to platforms handling only non-financial personal data. Level 3 (10-year retention) is required for platforms operating in healthcare or legal services contexts.

**RC-04.** Platforms above certain transaction thresholds face heightened governance obligations. Cooperative AI platforms **processing more than $50 million in annual gross merchandise value** are required to appoint an **independent Data Trust Officer** who reports directly to the cooperative board and cannot be employed by the platform operator. The Data Trust Officer has standing to request any operational record, can compel a compliance audit, and must countersign all cross-network data sharing agreements.

**RC-05.** Model accuracy must be reported to governance bodies, not just engineering teams. The Northbridge Reporting Standard requires that **quarterly AI model accuracy metrics be disclosed to the cooperative board** in a standardized format that includes overall accuracy, per-category accuracy breakdowns, and trend data over the prior four quarters. Boards that receive this report must acknowledge receipt in their board minutes, creating an auditable record of governance awareness.

**RC-06.** International data flows require bilateral adequacy confirmation. Under the Sundry Digital Rights Act, **cross-border member data transfers require Bilateral Data Adequacy confirmation from both the source and destination jurisdictions**. A unilateral adequacy finding by one jurisdiction is insufficient. This requirement has created friction for cooperative networks seeking to expand into new countries, as the bilateral confirmation process can take 18 to 36 months to complete through formal diplomatic channels.

**RC-07.** The Cairnfield Privacy Seal is the most widely recognized data handling certification in the sector. To obtain and maintain the Seal, platforms must undergo **annual third-party audits of member data handling practices** conducted by a Cairnfield-accredited auditor. The audit assesses 67 distinct control points covering data minimization, consent management, retention enforcement, and breach response. Seal revocation is publicly announced and creates significant member trust damage, making compliance a competitive necessity for established platforms.

**RC-08.** Regulated financial services contexts impose additional AI model adoption controls. Cooperative AI platforms operating under a financial services license must maintain a **6-month compliance buffer before adopting any new AI model version** into production. During the buffer period, the new model runs in shadow mode alongside the production model, and compliance staff compare outputs for material differences. Material differences trigger a formal compliance review before the new model can go live.

## Chapter 6 — Historical and Precedent

The cooperative AI platform sector's current practices are shaped by a series of landmark decisions, founding moments, and architectural breakthroughs. The eight entries below form the canonical historical record that practitioners use to understand where current rules came from.

**HP-01.** The legal status of member-owners in relation to platform AI systems was established by the Montclair Ruling of 2019. The ruling held that **cooperative member-owners hold a fiduciary interest in platform AI systems — not merely a contractual one**. This distinction carries significant legal weight: a fiduciary relationship creates duties of care and loyalty that survive the termination of individual contracts, and cannot be disclaimed in the membership agreement. The Montclair Ruling triggered a wave of cooperative bylaw revisions across North America and Europe.

**HP-02.** The sector's largest cooperative AI platform has its origins in northern Europe. The **Verdania Cooperative Platform was founded in Reykjavik in 2017 by a consortium of 12 Nordic credit unions** seeking a shared AI infrastructure that none could afford to build independently. The founding charter established the platform's cooperative ownership structure and committed to the three-tier membership model that the Sundry Accord would later codify. Verdania's founding coincided with the first wave of production-grade large language model deployments.

**HP-03.** Scale milestones mark the sector's maturation. **Pelham Commons was the first cooperative AI platform to achieve $1 billion in annual gross member value**, reaching this threshold in **fiscal year 2022** after seven years of growth from a founding base of 3,400 credit union members. The milestone was widely covered in cooperative finance media and demonstrated that cooperative AI platforms could achieve economic scale comparable to commercial alternatives without sacrificing member-ownership structure.

**HP-04.** The Sundry Accord, which now governs most cooperative AI platform practices, has a specific founding moment. The Accord was **ratified on November 14, 2021, by 47 founding cooperative organizations across 9 countries**. The ratification ceremony was held in Geneva and included a formal reading of the Accord's three core principles: member sovereignty, transparent economics, and auditable AI. The 47 founding cooperatives represented approximately 2.3 million member-owners at the time of ratification.

**HP-05.** The technical standard that now governs data privacy in cooperative AI deployments emerged from an academic working group. The **Fjordgate Protocol originated from the 2020 Helsinki Cooperative AI Summit's "minimum consent membrane" working group**, which was tasked with producing a technically implementable privacy architecture. The working group's final recommendation was published in March 2021 and adopted as the Fjordgate Protocol specification in June 2021, six months before the Sundry Accord's ratification.

**HP-06.** The cooperative sector has faced hostile acquisition attempts. In 2018, the **Belfry cooperative was the subject of an acquisition by a private equity firm**. Before the acquisition could close, the membership invoked the Belfry cooperative's **Defensive Conversion Clause**, which required the acquirer to offer existing members **22-month conversion rights** — the right to convert their membership equity to common shares in the acquiring entity at a pre-agreed price. The episode prompted widespread adoption of Defensive Conversion Clauses across the sector.

**HP-07.** The Thornwick architecture, now the reference standard for cooperative AI memory isolation, had its first large-scale deployment with a regional cooperative. The **Thornwick architecture was first deployed at scale in 2023 by Mossworth Cooperative Services**, a mid-Atlantic cooperative serving 127,000 members across three US states. Mossworth's deployment team published a detailed post-deployment report showing **99.7% uptime** in the architecture's first operational year, which became the primary evidence base for subsequent Pelham Reference Architecture adoption.

**HP-08.** The disclosure obligation for AI model changes was established by court decision. The **Cooper-Anderson Decision of 2022** held that cooperative AI platform operators in cooperative ownership structures must **disclose material model changes to member-owners within 21 days** of deployment. "Material" was defined as any change that alters output patterns in more than 5% of cases for a given category of query. The decision was upheld on appeal in 2023 and has been cited in 34 subsequent regulatory guidance documents.
