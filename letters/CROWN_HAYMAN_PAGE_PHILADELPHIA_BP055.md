# Crown Invitation — Let's Make Dinner / Philadelphia Local Civic

Dear Mr. Hayman,

You closed your post about the Marion Barry Avenue Market with a question:

*"What we discuss far less is food as economic development. Food as healthcare. Food as a vehicle for neighborhood ownership and wealth creation."*

And then: *"If you are working at the intersection of food systems, community development, or anchor institution strategy, I would love to connect."*

We are working at that intersection. And your question is, structurally, what we built the cooperative platform to answer.

**We are reaching out to invite a conversation about Liana Banyan Corporation's cooperative food-economy platform — and specifically about its first-locality launch opportunity in Philadelphia.** This is not a sponsorship ask and not a charity model. It is a direct response to the structural argument you made publicly about food as infrastructure.

## What Liana Banyan is, structurally

Liana Banyan is a member-sovereign cooperative substrate. The terms are canonical:

- **Membership is $5/year.**
- **Creators and workers may earn 83.3%** of what they generate on the platform.
- **Margin on goods and services routed through the substrate is held to Cost+20%** — a hard ceiling.
- **No advertising layer. No data resale. No engagement-extraction machinery.**

The Corporation (Wyoming, 2025-11-21) holds 19 USPTO provisional patent applications protecting the substrate primitives. 16 Initiatives coordinate cooperative responses to the categories of need markets have failed — food, housing, healthcare, family, education, manufacturing, music, civic life. Each Initiative is led by a Crown whose body of work already proves the cooperative thesis in practice.

## The Philadelphia opportunity

The cooperative substrate is built to launch city by city. Philadelphia is on the first-locality shortlist — and the argument you made publicly is the argument the Local Civic Eblet Project makes from the platform side: every city has its own food-systems story, and the platform provides the infrastructure to tell it cooperatively rather than through a charity lens.

What Philadelphia needs, you named precisely: food as economic development. Food as healthcare. Food as a vehicle for neighborhood ownership and wealth creation. The Marion Barry Market showed how that works at Ward 8 scale. The question for Philadelphia is which neighborhood becomes the first cooperative food hub that doesn't require an anchor institution to absorb the startup cost — because member-cooperative infrastructure absorbs it instead.

## The Let's Make Dinner Initiative

Initiative #1 anchors the cooperative food economy. Three primitives:

- **Freezer Hosts** — member-cooks who batch-prepare home meals for neighbors, and may earn 83.3% through cooperative infrastructure.
- **The Larder** — shared-pantry credit at Cost+20%, dollar staying inside the local member network.
- **Direct-employment lane** for food workers currently inside structures that capture most of their surplus.

Your PAGE Program lens — hospitality sector, food policy, creative economy — is the cross-sectoral view the Initiative needs in Philadelphia to make the cooperative food hub replicable at city scale.

## The ask

If the cooperative thesis composes with the work you are carrying at PAGE and in the Philadelphia food-economy space, we would welcome a conversation. We can send a briefing and arrange a call at your convenience.

The architecture is at **cephas.lianabanyan.com**.
