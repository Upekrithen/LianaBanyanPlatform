# Crown Invitation — Global Health Infrastructure / MSA + Health Accords

Dear Mr. Gates,

**This is a long-shot letter. I know that. I'm sending it anyway, because the structural argument is real and I believe you respect that more than a comfortable pitch.**

The Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation has spent twenty-five years proving a thesis about global health that the cooperative platform has spent nine years proving at the community level: that the primary driver of health outcomes is not the quality of individual treatment but the quality of the infrastructure that determines who gets access to treatment at all. Vaccines are not effective because they are medically sophisticated. They are effective because the infrastructure was built to reach the people who needed them, at the cost they could bear, in the communities where they live.

That is the cooperative-health argument. And it is structurally the same argument our two Health Initiatives (#6 Tatiana Schlossburg Health Accords and #7 MSA Medical Savings Accounts) are built around — not at global scale, but at the cooperative-community scale where markets have failed to provide the infrastructure access.

We are not building a global vaccine delivery network. We are building the cooperative infrastructure that determines whether a member of the platform has reliable healthcare access — whether they can save for a medical event, route their savings to participating healthcare providers at cooperative-negotiated rates, and access the health-outcomes accountability that tells them and the cooperative whether the structure is actually working.

The difference in scale is real. The structural argument is the same.

## What Liana Banyan is, structurally

Liana Banyan is a member-sovereign cooperative substrate. The terms are canonical:

- **Membership is $5/year.** That is the entire price of admission.
- **Creators and workers may earn 83.3%** of what they generate on the platform.
- **Margin on goods and services routed through the substrate is held to Cost+20%** — a hard ceiling, not a target.
- **No advertising layer. No data resale. No engagement-extraction machinery.** Members own their relationships and their work.

The Corporation (Wyoming, filed 2025-11-21) holds 19 USPTO provisional patent applications protecting the substrate primitives. The substrate is built to serve 16 Initiatives — coordinated cooperative responses to the categories of need that markets have systematically failed. Each Initiative is led by a Crown whose body of work has already proved the cooperative thesis in practice.

## The two Health Initiatives

**Initiative #6 — Tatiana Schlossburg Health Accords** (Crown: Tatiana Schlossburg): The cooperative's healthcare-policy and healthcare-access architecture. The Health Accords negotiates cooperative-class healthcare access on behalf of the member base, coordinates the platform's health-access primitives across all 16 Initiatives, and holds the platform to health-outcomes accountability — tracking whether the cooperative structure actually improves community health conditions and surfacing that data to the cooperative in the platform's transparent-accounting discipline.

**Initiative #7 — MSA (Medical Savings Accounts)** (Crown: Cathie Mahon · President & CEO, Inclusiv): The cooperative's healthcare-savings infrastructure. Members save in cooperative-currency instruments; savings route through Substitution at participating healthcare merchants at cooperative-negotiated rates; never fiat conversion at any boundary (preserving the cooperative's regulatory posture). The credit-union model — which Mahon has stewarded for decades at Inclusiv, representing 500+ community development credit unions serving 20 million members — applied to healthcare-savings instruments at member-cooperative scale.

Together, these two Initiatives describe the full cooperative healthcare-access architecture: the policy and negotiation layer (Health Accords) and the savings and payment layer (MSA).

## Why this letter

The Gates Foundation's cooperative-health investment thesis — whether it is the vaccine delivery infrastructure for underserved communities, the community-health worker programs, or the data-driven health-outcomes accountability work — aligns structurally with what the cooperative platform is building at the community level.

**I am not asking for a grant.** I am asking for something the Gates Foundation is better positioned than almost any institution on earth to provide: an introduction to one or two people in the cooperative-health infrastructure space who could help us avoid the mistakes that cooperative health organizations make when they scale — and who could help us think through how the cooperative-platform model composes with the global health infrastructure that the Foundation has spent twenty-five years building.

If the structural argument holds up under their scrutiny, I am prepared to offer the Crown Global Health Infrastructure Steward seat to whoever that conversation surfaces — or to you, if you choose to engage directly. One Crown, One Offer.
