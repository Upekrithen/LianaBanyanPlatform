# Institutional Outreach — DMPED · DC Cooperative Food Economy

To the Economic Development team at DMPED,

**We are reaching out because DMPED co-funded one of the most structurally significant cooperative-food-economy projects we have studied — the Marion Barry Avenue Market in Ward 8 — and we believe there is a direct institutional composition between that investment and the Liana Banyan cooperative platform.**

The Marion Barry Avenue Market is not just a market. It is a seven-layer cooperative food hub — grocery store, cafe, food hub sourcing from Black and women-owned farms, workforce development engine, demo kitchen, community gathering space, and local distribution system — all operating simultaneously in a neighborhood where fewer than three full-service grocery stores serve more than 75,000 residents.

DMPED's investment in that model, alongside Chef José Andrés' Longer Tables Fund, demonstrated a principle the cooperative platform is built around: municipal economic-development infrastructure works best when it enables self-sustaining community ownership, not dependency.

## What Liana Banyan is, structurally

Liana Banyan Corporation is a Wyoming cooperative-class corporation operating a member-sovereign platform. The canonical terms:

- **Membership is $5/year** — accessible to any DC resident.
- **Creators and workers may earn 83.3%** of what they generate on the platform — the cooperative share, not the extraction share.
- **Margin on goods and services is held to Cost+20%** — a constitutional floor that prevents the platform from becoming an extraction layer over the community it serves.
- **No advertising. No data resale. No engagement extraction.** Members own their relationships and their economic activity.

19 USPTO provisional patent applications protect the substrate primitives. 16 Initiatives coordinate cooperative responses to the categories of need that markets and charity have both failed to solve at scale — food, housing, healthcare, family, education, manufacturing, music, civic life.

## The DC composition opportunity

What you demonstrated at the Marion Barry Market — food as infrastructure, food as economic development, food as a vehicle for neighborhood ownership and community wealth — is what the Let's Make Dinner Initiative (#1) is designed to scale through cooperative-platform infrastructure.

Specifically, DMPED's institutional partner role in the cooperative platform could include:

- **Anchor-circle participation** in the Stand-in-the-Gap cooperative graduation pathway — the platform's workforce development layer, modeled on the same DOL-adjacent logic that backs community-employment programs.
- **First-locality partnership** in the Local Civic Eblet Project DC chapter — per-locality cooperative civic infrastructure that puts Ward 8, Anacostia, and other DC neighborhoods on the cooperative-platform map before national scale.
- **Data-sharing and impact-measurement** collaboration for the cooperative food-economy cluster (Let's Make Dinner + Let's Get Groceries) in DC neighborhoods with identified food-access gaps.
- **Co-investment modeling** — the Anchor-sponsor-circle structure enables municipal investment to be structured as cooperative-class participation rather than charity, with compounding community-wealth returns.

## The ask

We would welcome a conversation with DMPED's economic development team about how the cooperative platform's food and workforce-development cluster composes with the agency's existing Ward 8 and food-access portfolio. We can provide a full briefing on the cooperative structure, the patent-protected substrate, and the local-launch model.
