# Initiative Projects: Organization-Wide Programs

## Overview
Initiative Projects are special projects that affect LianaBanyan organization-wide, typically operating as charitable services under the .org non-profit division. Unlike standard projects, these serve the entire member community and external beneficiaries.

## Key Characteristics

### Differences from Standard Projects:
- **Scope**: Organization-wide impact (not single product/team)
- **Location**: Primarily under .org portal
- **Funding**: Mix of member contributions, donations, and operational surplus
- **Purpose**: Social benefit + member support services
- **Governance**: Banyan Council oversight + specialized committees

### Common Features:
- Member participation is optional
- Built-in charitable components
- Sustainable operational models
- Integration with core membership benefits

## Current Initiative Projects

### 1. Let's Make Dinner (LMD)
**Mission**: Connect home cooks with people who want homemade meals

**How It Works**:

#### For Meal Providers (Members Only):
1. Plan upcoming meals in advance
2. Add extra portions to their cooking
3. Post meal details:
   - Photo of dish
   - Complete ingredient list
   - Recipe (optional but encouraged)
   - Number of portions available
   - Pickup time/location
4. Earn credits: $5 per meal (5 credits)
5. Build reputation as reliable cook

#### For Meal Recipients:
**Preorder Option** (Recommended):
- Browse upcoming meals by date
- 24-hour minimum lead time
- Cost: $5/meal (5 credits)
- Choose specific meals
- Guaranteed portion

**Walk-Up Option**:
- Same day, no preorder
- Cost: $10/meal (10 credits)
- Pay first, then select from available
- Subject to availability

**Charitable Loan Account**:
- For those who can't pay
- Unlimited credit available
- Tracked separately as charitable loan
- Don't get to choose meal (first-come-first-serve)
- Allergy/dietary restrictions honored
- No collection enforcement

#### Delivery System:
- **Dinner Wagon**: LB-hired drivers (Members as Agents)
- Service areas: Nodes + surrounding regions
- Pickup from providers, delivery to recipients
- Paid from LMD fund
- Tips go to drivers

#### Financial Model:
```
Revenue Sources:
- Preorders: $5/meal → LMD fund
- Walk-ups: $10/meal → LMD fund
- Charitable donations → Charitable loan pool

Expenses:
- Providers: $5/meal delivered
- Drivers: $X per delivery (from fund)
- Operations: % cushion maintained

Fund Split:
- 60% to providers
- 30% to delivery
- 10% operations/cushion
```

#### UI Vision:
**Main Interface**: "The Dinner Table"
- Long scrollable table (left/right)
- Each place setting = one meal offering
- Plate shows food photo
- Settings labeled with:
  - Meal name
  - Provider name/reputation
  - Portions available
  - Pickup time
  - Price (always $5 preorder)
- Date selector at top
- Filter by dietary needs

## Reciprocal Role Fluidity Model

### Core Concept: "Circular Participation"
Members can transition fluidly between provider and consumer roles across all initiatives. This creates sustainable, self-reinforcing service ecosystems where:

- **Today's meal provider** is tomorrow's meal recipient
- **This week's shopper** becomes next week's bulk order organizer  
- **Current grocery orderer** may deliver for others later
- **MSA contributor** can access fund when needed
- **Defense Claws subscriber** might volunteer as advocate

### Economic Sustainability Principle
Each initiative generates revenue to fund itself and expand, creating opportunities especially for members within Node service range.

**No Minimum Earnings Requirement**: Instead of income thresholds, we provide:
- **Subsistence Guarantee**: All members have access to all services
- **Need-Based Access**: Charitable loan accounts for those unable to pay
- **Earning Opportunities**: Provider roles available to all members
- **Self-Funding Growth**: Revenue enables service expansion

### Service Range & Demand Scaling

**Node Service Radius**: Geographic reach determined by:
1. **Consistent Demand Levels**: Higher regular usage = wider service area
2. **Provider Density**: More providers = better coverage
3. **Delivery Economics**: Cost efficiency determines sustainable range
4. **Infrastructure Investment**: Node capacity scales with proven demand

**Examples by Initiative**:

#### Let's Make Dinner
- **High Demand Area**: 5-mile radius, daily delivery, 50+ providers
- **Medium Demand**: 2-mile radius, 3x/week delivery, 20+ providers  
- **Emerging Area**: Pickup-only at Node until critical mass

#### Let's Go Shopping / Let's Get Groceries
- **Range Factors**: Bulk delivery routes, refrigeration needs, order frequency
- **Scaling Trigger**: When weekly orders exceed delivery cost threshold
- **Expansion**: Add new delivery days/routes as demand proves sustainable

#### LifeLine Medications
- **Critical Service**: Widest possible range regardless of density
- **Subsidized Delivery**: Node operators prioritize medication runs
- **Partner Integration**: Pharmacy partnerships extend effective range

#### Defense Claws
- **Digital-First**: Legal services not range-limited
- **Physical Presence**: Court representation at county/state level
- **Scaling**: Attorney network grows with subscription base

#### MSA (Medical Savings Account)
- **No Geographic Limit**: Digital account accessible anywhere
- **Integration**: Works with LifeLine for holistic health support
- **Usage Pattern**: Contributions from surplus, withdrawals as needed

### Reciprocity Examples

**Let's Make Dinner**:
- Marie cooks extra lasagna Monday (earns 5 credits)
- Uses those credits for Ahmed's curry Thursday (spends 5 credits)
- Both benefit: supplemental income + convenient meals
- **Circular Value**: Credits recirculate within meal ecosystem

**Let's Go Shopping**:
- Chen organizes Halloween costume order (earns coordination fee)
- Joins Priya's Christmas gift bulk order (saves with volume discount)
- Both leverage collective buying power
- **Network Effect**: More participants = better pricing for all

**Let's Get Groceries**:
- Jordan places weekly produce order (pays delivery fee)
- Occasionally drives delivery route for extra income
- System self-balances supply/demand
- **Flexible Roles**: Members choose when to provide vs. consume

**MSA + LifeLine**:
- Taylor contributes to MSA during high-earning months
- Accesses LifeLine assistance during medication cost spike
- MSA covers copays, LifeLine negotiates pricing
- **Integrated Support**: Financial planning + charitable safety net

**Defense Claws**:
- Alex subscribes for coverage (pays monthly)
- Volunteers as intake coordinator (earns credits)
- Community-funded legal protection for all
- **Mutual Aid**: Pooled resources protect entire community

### Research Questions for Business Model

1. **Optimal Provider-Consumer Ratio**: What balance sustains each initiative?
2. **Credit Velocity**: How fast do credits need to circulate for liquidity?
3. **Subsidy Requirements**: What % of members need charitable support long-term?
4. **Range Optimization**: Algorithm for expanding service radius based on demand density?
5. **Cross-Initiative Effects**: Does participation in one service increase likelihood of joining others?
6. **Retention Impact**: How does reciprocal participation affect member lifetime value?

### Success Metrics

**Circular Participation Health**:
- % of members active in both provider and consumer roles
- Average role transitions per member per year
- Credit recirculation rate (velocity)
- Service expansion rate relative to member growth

**Economic Sustainability**:
- Revenue per initiative exceeding operating costs
- Charitable subsidy % remaining stable or decreasing
- Node service range expansion over time
- Member earning opportunities created per initiative

**Social Impact**:
- Members utilizing subsistence guarantee services
- Community connections formed through initiatives
- Economic activity generated within Node radius
- Quality of life improvements (meals, health, protection)
